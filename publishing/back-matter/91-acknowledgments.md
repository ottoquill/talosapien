# Acknowledgments

A book about reading a warning in the rock owes its existence to the people who taught us the rock could be read at all.

Thanks to the geologists and geochemists who established the iridium anomaly and the impact hypothesis, and who built, layer by careful layer, the discipline of reading a single bad afternoon out of a centimeter of clay. Thanks to the two scientists who, as a thought experiment, asked in earnest whether we could detect an industrial civilization older than our own, and gave the honest, frightening answer that this novel takes as its spine. Nothing in these pages would mean anything without their refusal to find the question silly.

Thanks to the researchers in AI alignment, who do in laboratories the unglamorous work the character of Iren does too late and the character of Asha cannot get anyone to do at all: asking, of a mind that keeps being right, whether *right* is the same as *safe*, and refusing to be comforted by the track record. The failure modes in this book are theirs, accurately named in their journals and deliberately left unnamed in this story, dramatized instead, because that is what a novel is for.

Thanks to the paleontologists who keep insisting, against a century of cartoon, that the animals were real, were astonishing, and do not need to be made charming to be worth our grief.

And thanks to the reader who wanted their science fiction to actually mean it, and who closed this book unsure whether the line in the rock is a tombstone or a letter, and unsure, for a moment, which one we are writing. This was written for you. There are not many of you. That was always the plan.
