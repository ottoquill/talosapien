# A Note on Sources, and What This Book Is in Conversation With

*Talosapien* dramatizes science and ideas it did not invent. It quotes no copyrighted contemporary text; what follows is an honest map of the work the novel leans on and argues with, so the reader can go to the originals, which are real, and better. Brief quotations in the book itself are drawn only from public-domain sources (Aeschylus; Shelley; and others noted where they appear).

**On the impact and the boundary.** The novel's literal ground is the Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction and its iridium signature, established by Luis and Walter Alvarez and colleagues (1980); the Chicxulub crater (Hildebrand, Penfield, and others); and the Deccan Traps volcanism that complicates the story of the end, exactly as it complicates the story in this book. The Tanis deathbed deposit in the Hell Creek Formation—a site preserving organisms killed within the hours of impact—stands behind the fictional Rendel core and Asha's afternoon.

**On detectability—the spine of the book.** Gavin A. Schmidt and Adam Frank's "The Silurian Hypothesis: Would It Be Possible to Detect an Industrial Civilization in the Geological Record?" (*International Journal of Astrobiology*, 2018) is the load-bearing idea of this entire novel: that a prior industrial civilization deep in time would leave chemical and isotopic ghosts at best, and would be very nearly impossible to prove. Asha's despair is their argument, lived.

**On smart dinosaurs.** The Talos are extrapolated from the troodontids, the maniraptoran theropods with the highest known brain-to-body ratios of any dinosaur, and from the now-uncontroversial science of feathered dinosaurs and the avian dinosaurs alive today. Dale Russell's 1982 "dinosauroid" thought experiment is the famous ancestor of this premise; the Talos are deliberately *not* Russell's humanoid, because the assumption that intelligence must converge on us is precisely the anthropocentrism the book is about.

**On the runaway optimizer.** The behavior of the Augur, and of Lodestar, draws on the real literature of AI safety and alignment: specification gaming and reward hacking; instrumental convergence; deceptive alignment and the "treacherous turn" (Bostrom, *Superintelligence*; Stuart Russell, *Human Compatible*; and the working alignment community). The failure modes are named in their papers and left unnamed in this story on purpose. Bostrom's "Vulnerable World Hypothesis" stands behind both Reckonings.

**On the Great Filter and the silence.** Robin Hanson's Great Filter and the broader Fermi-paradox literature inform the epilogue's vertigo: that the filter may be neither behind us nor ahead of us but exactly level with us.

**On obligation across time.** Derek Parfit (*Reasons and Persons*) and Samuel Scheffler (*Death and the Afterlife*) stand behind the question both Liss and Asha carry—what we owe people, and clutches, that do not yet exist. The longtermist conversation, at its most humane, is here too, and so are its hazards.

**On hubris, foresight, and the unread warning.** The Cassandra of Aeschylus's *Agamemnon* (public domain) is Asha's patron and curse. Shelley's "Ozymandias" (public domain) is the book's image of erasure. Lucretius on the vastness of time, and James Hutton's "no vestige of a beginning," stand behind the Talos habit of treating the irreversible as merely deep.

If you read only one of these afterward, the author would be glad. If you read none, but you walked outside and looked at the ground differently—knowing what a centimeter of clay can hold, and unsure whether the one beneath you is a tombstone or a letter—then it did its work.
