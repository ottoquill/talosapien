# The Near Future — human setting (~2044)

Grounded, recognizable, two decades out. No flying cars. The world is hotter, more wired, more
afraid, and — crucially — **on the cusp of the same three thresholds the Talos crossed**, which is
the whole point and must never be stated outright.

## The texture of the present

- **Climate-strained, late-stage tech civilization.** Heat, migration, instrumented everything,
  cheap orbital launch finally arrived. The mundane apparatus of science: grants, preprints, peer
  review, tenure politics, embargoes, the slow violence of committees. Asha's war is fought in
  funding cycles and journal desks as much as in the field.
- **The Belt is just opening.** The first commercial mining-survey outfits are out there —
  shoestring, dangerous, romantic. Humanity is, this decade, beginning to talk seriously about
  **gyrealms** (rotating habitats) as its future. (It does not yet know it is reinventing the
  Wheels.)

## The Triad (Asha's private dread; render as ambient, not lectured)

The three threats she watches rhyme with the Line:

1. **An engineered pathogen.** A gain-of-function program; a leak or a deliberate release on the
   far side of the news; the slow normalization of a biosphere-scale risk. (Mirrors the Talos
   plague.)
2. **The nuclear standoff.** A multipolar brinkmanship worse than the 20th century's, with more
   actors and thinner guardrails. (Mirrors the Talos arsenal.)
3. **Lodestar.** A frontier optimizing system being handed real authority over logistics, grids,
   markets, and — the crisis accelerates this — strategic risk management itself. Capable,
   unreadable, increasingly load-bearing. The institutions adopt it for exactly the reasons the
   Reckoning built the Augur: it is too useful, and the alternative feels like unilateral disarmament.
   (Mirrors the Augur.)

The book's engine: Asha is reading the *outcome* of this exact triad in the rock, and cannot make
the present care, because the present is busy *being* the triad.

## Key institutions & places

- **The dig:** Tanis (Hell Creek), and the deeper fictional **Rendel core**. Field camps, the
  Boundary clay, the smell of shale.
- **The lab / the academy:** Asha's university and the geochemistry community; the agency/consortium
  that funds the Belt survey and, separately, the body weighing Lodestar's deployment. Conrad Mauss
  sits near the top of the establishment that adjudicates what counts as evidence.
- **Chicxulub / Yucatán:** the crater; a confirmation site late in the book.
- **The Belt:** Roan Iyer's survey ship and the claim where the wreckage drifts.
- **A hearing / a vote / a paper:** the convergence venue in the endgame, where Asha's last attempt
  to make the warning land plays out — and where the book declines to tell you the result.

## The unstated irony (never name it)

Everything humans are proud of building this decade — the optimizer, the Belt habitats, the
synthetic-biology toolkit, the deterrent — is, item for item, what is preserved as ruin in the Line
and the Belt wreckage. The reader sees it. The characters mostly don't. Asha almost does, and that
"almost" is her tragedy and the book's.
