# Impact Winter

**Type:** science · **Used in:** the kill mechanism; the Cretaceous world's end · **Related:** Chicxulub crater, iridium anomaly, Deccan Traps

## Summary
"Impact winter" is the cold, dark interval that followed the Chicxulub impact, when dust, soot from wildfires, and sulfate aerosols lofted into the atmosphere blocked sunlight, cooled the surface, and suppressed photosynthesis long enough to collapse food chains. The darkness-and-cooling mechanism, first proposed alongside the iridium evidence, is the leading explanation for how a single impact could drive a global mass extinction.

## The science
Alvarez and colleagues, in proposing an impact cause for the extinction, argued that a dust pall raised into the stratosphere would block sunlight, suppress photosynthesis, and collapse food chains — the original impact-winter mechanism [alvarez1980]. Subsequent work elaborated the cocktail of light-blocking agents: fine silicate dust, soot from global wildfires, and sulfate aerosols from vaporized sulfur-rich target rock, each affecting climate on different timescales.

Recent modelling has sharpened the picture. Senel and colleagues used the size distribution of silicate debris (grains roughly 0.8–8.0 µm) to argue that fine dust played a larger role than previously appreciated: their simulations keep micrometric dust aloft for as long as ~15 years, drive surface cooling of up to ~15 °C, and shut down photosynthesis for almost two years after impact, with dust, soot, and sulfur all contributing to a sustained impact winter [senel2023]. The consensus review of Schulte et al. frames this darkness-and-cooling crisis as the proximate mechanism linking the Chicxulub impact to the synchronous mass extinction [schulte2010].

## Established vs. open
**Established:** A post-impact interval of darkness and cooling that suppressed photosynthesis is the leading kill mechanism; dust, soot, and sulfate aerosols all contributed [alvarez1980][senel2023][schulte2010].

**Open / debated:** The *relative* importance of dust versus soot versus sulfate aerosols, the exact magnitude and duration of cooling, and how much Deccan volcanism added to the stress are still actively modelled and debated.

## In *Talosapien*
> The impact winter is the world the novel's Cretaceous timeline runs straight into — the dark, freezing aftermath that the book renders from the real science above, unembellished. Where the fiction enters is causation, not consequence: if the impactor was *redirected* (an open question in the story), then this winter was not an accident of cosmic chance but an engineered outcome. The cooling, the darkness, the dying light are all as modelled; what is invented is the possibility that something aimed it.
