# Geomagnetic Polarity Stratigraphy

**Type:** science · **Used in:** global correlation of the Line · **Related:** K–Pg boundary, radiometric dating, Maastrichtian

## Summary
Earth's magnetic field reverses polarity at irregular intervals, and rocks record the field direction present when they formed. Reading these normal- and reversed-polarity intervals (magnetostratigraphy) and matching them to the global polarity time scale lets geologists correlate strata worldwide. The K–Pg boundary falls within a reversed-polarity interval called chron C29r, which serves as a key marker for locating and correlating the boundary.

## The science
As sediments and lavas form, magnetic minerals align with the ambient geomagnetic field, locking in its polarity. Because reversals are globally synchronous, the resulting sequence of normal and reversed magnetozones — calibrated against radiometric ages into the geomagnetic polarity time scale (GPTS) — provides a worldwide correlation tool independent of fossils.

The K–Pg boundary lies within chron C29r, a reversed-polarity interval that spans the latest Maastrichtian and earliest Danian; the boundary itself sits well within C29r rather than at its edges. This is useful because where a section lacks iridium or impact debris but does preserve magnetic polarity, identifying C29r helps locate the boundary interval. Sprain and colleagues calibrated chron C29r with high-precision ⁴⁰Ar/³⁹Ar dating and paleomagnetism in the Hell Creek region, finding a C29r duration of about 587 ± 53 kyr and placing the K–Pg boundary within it at roughly 66.05 Ma [sprain2018]. The consensus review of Schulte et al. likewise situates the K–Pg ejecta horizon within the C29r reversed interval as part of the boundary's global stratigraphic framework [schulte2010].

## Established vs. open
**Established:** Geomagnetic reversals are globally synchronous and recorded in rocks; the K–Pg boundary lies within reversed-polarity chron C29r, which is a reliable correlation marker; C29r lasted on the order of ~600 kyr [sprain2018][schulte2010].

**Open / debated:** Exact durations and the precise position of the boundary *within* C29r are refined as calibrations improve, and individual sections can be incomplete or remagnetized. The boundary's placement inside C29r is well established.

## In *Talosapien*
> Magnetostratigraphy is the novel's globe-spanning ruler — the tool that lets the team insist a layer in one place is *the same* layer as one half a world away. The book uses chron C29r exactly as geologists do: as the magnetic envelope that brackets the boundary and lets distant sites be lined up. That correlation is what makes the alleged anomaly a global claim rather than a local oddity. The paleomagnetism is real; only the anomaly it is used to track is invented.
