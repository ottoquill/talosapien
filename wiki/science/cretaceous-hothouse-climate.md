# Cretaceous Hothouse Climate

**Type:** science · **Used in:** the climate of the world before the boundary; baseline against which Asha's anomalies are read · **Related:** late Cretaceous paleogeography; Maastrichtian; δ¹³C/δ¹⁵N excursions

## Summary
The Cretaceous was a greenhouse ("hothouse") world: atmospheric CO₂ was elevated relative to the present, high latitudes were warm, and there was little or no permanent polar ice. That warmth was not steady — it peaked in the mid-Cretaceous "Hot Greenhouse" and then cooled through the Late Cretaceous toward the Maastrichtian, the final stage before the K–Pg boundary. This warm, ice-poor baseline climate is the natural backdrop against which the novel's claimed boundary-clay anomalies must be distinguished from ordinary Cretaceous conditions.

## The science
Across the Phanerozoic, atmospheric CO₂ has declined over the long term, and Cretaceous CO₂ stood above pre-industrial values within that broad downward trend. Foster, Royer and Lunt compiled over 1,200 proxy estimates of past atmospheric CO₂ spanning roughly 420 million years, documenting a long-term decline of order a few ppm per million years and placing the Mesozoic — including the Cretaceous — at CO₂ levels elevated relative to the late Cenozoic and pre-industrial baseline; they note that values closer to pre-industrial are reached again only by the late Cretaceous (~80 Ma) as that decline proceeds [foster2017]. Higher CO₂ (together with paleogeography and ocean circulation) is the standard explanation for the period's warmth.

The Cretaceous greenhouse was warm enough to keep the high latitudes mild and the poles largely free of permanent ice. Huber, MacLeod, Watkins and Coffin synthesized the temperature history and described a predominantly warm greenhouse world in which warm oceans surrounding and penetrating Antarctica prevented growth of Antarctic ice sheets at all but the highest elevations from the late Aptian through the late Campanian [huber2018]. Warmth was not constant: extreme warmth — the Cretaceous Thermal Maximum — occurred in the early-to-mid Turonian through late Santonian, after which long-term cooling began in the Campanian and culminated in Cretaceous temperature minima during the Maastrichtian [huber2018]. In other words, the world at the very end of the Cretaceous was still a warm, largely ice-free greenhouse, but cooler than the mid-Cretaceous peak.

## Established vs. open
**Established:** Cretaceous atmospheric CO₂ was elevated relative to the present as part of the long-term Phanerozoic CO₂ decline [foster2017]; the Cretaceous was a warm greenhouse with warm high latitudes and little or no permanent polar ice, peaking in the mid-Cretaceous and cooling into the Maastrichtian [huber2018].

**Open / debated:** Absolute Cretaceous CO₂ concentrations carry substantial proxy uncertainty, and reconstructions disagree on exact values [foster2017]; the magnitude of polar warmth, the climate sensitivity implied, and the degree to which any transient or high-elevation ice existed remain debated [huber2018].

## In *Talosapien*
> The hothouse climate is real and the novel does not tamper with it: the Maastrichtian world Asha studies is a warm, ice-poor greenhouse exactly as the literature describes, cooling from the mid-Cretaceous peak. This accurate climate baseline matters to the plot precisely because it sets the natural expectation — the ordinary δ¹³C, the ordinary carbon and nitrogen cycling of a warm Cretaceous ocean — against which the [δ¹³C/δ¹⁵N excursions](d13c-d15n-excursions.md) Asha flags stand out as anomalous. The fiction is never in the climate; it is in the signals she claims sit *inside* the boundary clay, the [transuranic and fission isotopes](transuranic-fission-isotopes.md) and industrial isotopic excursions that a natural greenhouse world cannot produce — the case she builds along [the Line](../lore/glossary/the-line.md) in pursuit of the [Silurian hypothesis](../ideas/silurian-hypothesis.md).
