# The Cretaceous–Paleogene Boundary

**Type:** science · **Used in:** the Line; Asha's case; the setting's deep-time floor · **Related:** iridium anomaly, Maastrichtian, Chicxulub crater

## Summary
The Cretaceous–Paleogene (K–Pg) boundary is the geologic horizon, dated to roughly 66.0 million years ago, that marks the end of the Cretaceous Period and one of the five largest mass extinctions in Earth history — the event that ended the non-avian dinosaurs. In marine and many continental sections it is expressed as a thin clay layer carrying an iridium spike and impact debris, and its global reference point (GSSP) is fixed at El Kef, Tunisia.

## The science
The K–Pg boundary separates the last stage of the Cretaceous (the Maastrichtian) from the first stage of the Paleogene (the Danian). Its formal definition — the Global Boundary Stratotype Section and Point — is placed at the base of the boundary clay in the El Kef section of northwestern Tunisia, where there is continuous sedimentation across the transition, a clear lithologic change from grey marl to dark boundary clay, an iridium anomaly, and an abrupt turnover in planktic foraminifera; the GSSP was ratified by the IUGS in 1991 and revised in 2006 [molina2006]. Practically, the boundary is read as marking the moment of the Chicxulub impact and the associated extinction [molina2006].

High-precision ⁴⁰Ar/³⁹Ar geochronology places the boundary at approximately 66.0 Ma and, critically, shows that the Chicxulub impact and the mass extinction were synchronous to within about 32,000 years — strong support for a causal link rather than mere coincidence [renne2013]. A later high-resolution calibration tied to the Hell Creek region returned a boundary age of about 66.05 Ma, consistent with the earlier figure [sprain2018]. The boundary clay itself is the carrier of the classic impact signals: anomalous iridium and other platinum-group elements, shocked quartz, and glassy impact spherules distributed worldwide, which together form the empirical core of the impact hypothesis as synthesized in the consensus review of Schulte et al. [schulte2010].

## Established vs. open
**Established:** The K–Pg boundary is a real, globally correlatable horizon at ~66.0 Ma; it coincides with a major mass extinction and with the Chicxulub impact to within tens of thousands of years; its reference section is El Kef [molina2006][renne2013][schulte2010].

**Open / debated:** The *relative* roles of the Chicxulub impact and Deccan Traps volcanism in driving the extinction remain debated, as do the fine-scale completeness and depositional dynamics of individual boundary sections. That a boundary exists and what defines it are not in dispute.

## In *Talosapien*
> The K–Pg boundary is the literal ground the novel stands on — the deep-time floor beneath every dig, every core, every argument. The book treats all of the above as real and unaltered: the age, the clay, the El Kef stratotype, the synchrony with Chicxulub. The fictional move is that, within this same boundary clay, Asha's team claims to find traces that do not belong to any natural impact — manufactured spherules, transuranic isotopes, an industrial nitrogen signature — the seam where a prior civilization's record is alleged to hide inside the planet's best-dated catastrophe. Whether the Chicxulub impactor was natural or a redirected Belt object is left unresolved in the story; the boundary itself is exactly as geology describes it.
