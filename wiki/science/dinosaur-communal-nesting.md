# Dinosaur Communal Nesting & Brooding

**Type:** science · **Used in:** the real basis for the Talos crèche / communal egg-laying · **Related:** [troodontids.md](troodontids.md), Talos sapiens

## Summary
Dinosaurs built nests, brooded eggs, and in some cases nested colonially with apparent parental care. The hadrosaur *Maiasaura* is known from a Montana site interpreted as a colonial nesting ground with associated juveniles; the troodontid "*Troodon*" is known from clutches and a brooding-adult association that document bird-like incubation. Together these establish nesting, clutch structure, brooding, and at least some parental care as real dinosaurian behaviours.

## The science
Horner & Makela described a Late Cretaceous (Two Medicine Formation, Montana) site containing nests, eggs, and juveniles of the hadrosaur they named *Maiasaura* ("good mother lizard"), arguing that the association of nestlings with the nest implied post-hatching care and family structure — and that the closely spaced nests indicated colonial (communal) nesting, with internest spacing comparable to the body length of an adult [horner1979]. This was among the first direct fossil evidence for parental behaviour and colonial nesting in dinosaurs.

Among maniraptorans close to bird ancestry, Varricchio et al. analyzed nests and egg clutches attributed to the troodontid *Troodon formosus* from Montana, documenting a clutch of paired eggs arranged in an open nest, partial burial, an absence of egg rotation, and association with a brooding adult [varricchio1997]. They read this as a mosaic of reproductive traits — some crocodilian-like (partial burial, no rotation), some bird-like (open nest with exposed eggs incubated by a brooding adult) — illuminating the assembly of avian reproductive biology within theropod dinosaurs [varricchio1997]. (Note the genus name *Troodon* is itself contested; see [troodontids.md](troodontids.md).) Similar brooding postures are independently documented in oviraptorosaurs, reinforcing that adult-on-nest brooding was real among maniraptoran theropods.

## Established vs. open
**Established:** Dinosaurs nested, laid structured egg clutches, and brooded; *Maiasaura* provides colonial-nesting and parental-care evidence [horner1979]; troodontid clutches plus a brooding-adult association document bird-like incubation in a near-bird theropod [varricchio1997].

**Open / debated:** The extent and kind of parental care varied across dinosaur groups and is partly inferential; specific genus assignments (e.g. *Troodon*) are unstable [varricchio1997]; whether a given nest site truly represents simultaneous colonial nesting vs. repeated reuse can be debated for individual localities.

## In *Talosapien*
> The Talos's **communal egg-laying in a crèche** is extrapolated from **real** dinosaur biology: colonial nesting [horner1979] and brooding clutches in a near-bird troodontid [varricchio1997] are documented, not invented. The novel's licensed addition is *sociality and culture* — a sapient species that turns nesting into an institution (a shared crèche, collective care, social meaning), which goes far beyond anything the fossils show. The clutch geometry and brooding posture are real; the crèche as a cultural and political structure is fiction.
