# Encephalization Quotient (EQ)

**Type:** science · **Used in:** why the Talos lineage is plausible as a "smart dinosaur" · **Related:** [troodontids.md](troodontids.md), Talos sapiens

## Summary
The encephalization quotient (EQ) is the ratio of an animal's actual brain size to the brain size predicted for its body size by an allometric (brain-vs-body) regression. Introduced by Harry Jerison, it is widely used as a rough comparative proxy for relative brain investment — and, with heavy caveats, for intelligence. Among non-avian dinosaurs, troodontids ("*Troodon*"-grade animals) show the highest relative brain size, approaching the lower end of the bird range.

## The science
Brain mass scales with body mass roughly as a power law: across many groups, brain mass increases with about the two-thirds power of body mass. Jerison formalized the deviation from that expectation as the encephalization quotient — EQ ≈ (actual brain mass) / (expected brain mass for that body mass), where the expected value comes from a fitted regression line; an EQ of 1 means "as big-brained as expected for the body," above 1 means more, below 1 means less. Jerison's 1973 book *Evolution of the Brain and Intelligence* is the standard origin point for the measure, and it is cited as such in the dinosaur-intelligence literature [reiner2023].

For dinosaurs, EQ has to be estimated indirectly: brain (endocast) volume is taken from the braincase, body mass is estimated from the skeleton, and — crucially — the regression baseline matters. Brain size is compared against a *reptile* baseline (a "reptile-EQ") rather than the mammal baseline Jerison originally fitted, because cold-blooded reptiles have systematically smaller brains for their bodies. Hopson's archosaur survey, using this reptile-grade frame, found that most dinosaurs sit within the living-reptile range but that the small coelurosaurs/troodontids stand well above it, overlapping the low end of birds [hopson1977]. A recent review concurs: many theropods exceed living reptiles, maniraptorans approach birds, and *Troodon* in particular sits at the lower margin of the overlapping avian/mammalian range — i.e. the highest relative brain size known for a non-avian dinosaur [reiner2023].

EQ is a blunt instrument. It compresses brain *organization* (neuron number, packing density, which regions are enlarged) into a single size ratio; body-mass estimates for fossils carry large error; and the choice of regression line strongly shifts the numbers. Reiner's review explicitly cautions that EQ and even neuron-count estimates are proxies, not direct readouts of cognition, and that body-size corrections must be applied carefully [reiner2023].

## Established vs. open
**Established:** EQ is a defined, widely used allometric ratio (Jerison) [reiner2023]; on a reptile baseline, troodontids have the highest relative brain size among non-avian dinosaurs, overlapping low-end birds [hopson1977][reiner2023].

**Open / debated:** How well *any* brain-size index tracks intelligence; the right regression baseline and body-mass estimates for extinct taxa; whether neuron counts (a newer proxy) overturn EQ-based rankings. Treat "*Troodon* was the smartest dinosaur" as a relative-brain-size statement, not a proven cognitive fact [reiner2023].

## In *Talosapien*
> EQ is the real scientific hook that makes the novel's premise *not* absurd: the lineage the Talos descend from genuinely had the highest relative brain size of any non-avian dinosaur. The book takes that real endpoint and runs the clock forward — a licensed speculation, clearly flagged as fiction. No real troodontid EQ implies language or tools; the novel's Talos are an invented continuation, not a paleontological claim. In-world, the characters are careful (as real workers are) to say "high relative brain size," not "as smart as us."
