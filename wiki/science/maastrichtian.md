# The Maastrichtian

**Type:** science · **Used in:** the Cretaceous world; deep-time setting · **Related:** K–Pg boundary, geomagnetic polarity stratigraphy, Deccan Traps

## Summary
The Maastrichtian is the final age (stage) of the Cretaceous Period, spanning roughly 72.1 to 66.0 million years ago and ending at the K–Pg boundary. It is the world of the last non-avian dinosaurs — *Tyrannosaurus*, *Triceratops*, mosasaurs, ammonites — and its base is formally defined by a reference point in southwestern France.

## The science
The Maastrichtian's lower boundary is fixed by a Global Boundary Stratotype Section and Point (GSSP) at Tercis les Bains, near Dax in the Landes region of southwestern France, where the base of the stage is placed at level 115.2 of the section, defined by the arithmetic mean of twelve biostratigraphic criteria of equal weight clustered closely around the first appearance of the ammonite *Pachydiscus neubergicus*; the average age of the Campanian–Maastrichtian boundary there is about 72.1 ± 0.1 Ma [odin2001]. The section is a thick sequence of open-marine limestones deposited at moderate depths.

The top of the Maastrichtian is the K–Pg boundary itself, formally defined at El Kef, Tunisia, at the base of the iridium-bearing boundary clay and dated to about 66.0 Ma [molina2006][renne2013]. The Maastrichtian therefore frames the latest Cretaceous biosphere right up to the impact: ammonites, marine reptiles, and the terminal dinosaur faunas, alongside the onset of major Deccan Traps volcanism late in the stage.

## Established vs. open
**Established:** The Maastrichtian is the last Cretaceous stage, ~72.1–66.0 Ma, with a ratified GSSP at Tercis les Bains keyed to *Pachydiscus neubergicus*, and an upper limit at the El Kef K–Pg boundary [odin2001][molina2006].

**Open / debated:** Precise correlation of the Tercis biomarkers to other continents, and exact numerical ages, are periodically refined as geochronology improves; the stage's definition and ordinal place are settled.

## In *Talosapien*
> The Maastrichtian is the novel's "before" — the living Cretaceous world in its final chapter, rendered from real stratigraphy and fauna without alteration. The book uses the stage as the stage: the last full ecosystem before the boundary clay, the world that the impact (natural or otherwise) ends. None of the Maastrichtian science is bent; it is the honest baseline that makes the boundary's anomalies legible.
