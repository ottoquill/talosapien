# δ¹³C and δ¹⁵N Excursions

**Type:** science · **Used in:** Asha's case; the boundary clay chemistry · **Related:** the Line; iridium anomaly; Haber–Bosch nitrogen fixation

## Summary
Stable-isotope ratios in fossils and sediments record how the carbon and nitrogen cycles behaved in deep time. Across the Cretaceous–Paleogene (K–Pg) boundary the marine carbon-isotope record shows an abrupt collapse of the normal surface-to-deep δ¹³C gradient — the "Strangelove ocean" — interpreted as a crash in ocean primary productivity. Nitrogen isotopes (δ¹⁵N) track the nitrogen cycle and productivity, and together δ¹³C and δ¹⁵N are the primary geochemical fingerprints a researcher reads when asking what the biosphere was doing at a boundary.

## The science
Stable-isotope ratios are reported in delta notation, in parts per thousand (per mil, ‰), as the deviation of a sample's heavy/light isotope ratio from a standard: δ = (R_sample/R_standard − 1) × 1000, where R is ¹³C/¹²C or ¹⁵N/¹⁴N. Carbon-isotope values are reported relative to the VPDB (Vienna Pee Dee Belemnite) carbonate standard; nitrogen-isotope values are reported relative to atmospheric N₂ (the "AIR" standard), which is assigned δ¹⁵N = 0 [sigman2019]. An "excursion" is a transient shift of these values away from their background, marking a perturbation of the cycle the isotope tracks.

In a normal, biologically productive ocean, photosynthetic plankton in the sunlit surface preferentially fix the light isotope ¹²C, leaving surface waters enriched in ¹³C; when that organic matter sinks and is respired at depth, it returns ¹²C to deep water. This sets up a vertical δ¹³C gradient — heavier (higher δ¹³C) at the surface, lighter at depth — recorded by the shells of surface-dwelling (planktic) versus bottom-dwelling (benthic) foraminifera. Hsü and McKenzie documented that across the K–Pg boundary the planktic skeletons record a decrease of up to ~3‰ in δ¹³C while benthic skeletons show little corresponding change, erasing the surface-to-deep gradient; they argued that the biological pump had effectively shut down because the plankton biomass collapsed, and named the resulting near-lifeless surface ocean the "Strangelove ocean" [hsu1985]. Zachos, Arthur and Dean confirmed a rapid and complete breakdown of the surface-to-deep ¹³C gradient at the boundary, with proxies for organic-carbon and carbonate flux (including barium) dropping sharply, and estimated that the implied suppression of oceanic primary productivity persisted on the order of ~0.5 million years before the gradient was re-established [zachos1989].

Nitrogen isotopes record the nitrogen cycle. Because biological and chemical transformations of nitrogen — nitrogen fixation, assimilation, denitrification — discriminate kinetically against the heavy isotope ¹⁵N (the light ¹⁴N is preferentially converted from substrate to product), the δ¹⁵N of sinking organic matter and of the residual nitrate pool carries information about which processes dominated, and hence about productivity and the nitrogen inventory [sigman2019]. Sedimentary δ¹⁵N is therefore used as a proxy for past states of the marine nitrogen cycle, with the important caveat that the signal can be altered after deposition (diagenesis) and that most interpretive frameworks rest on analogy with the modern ocean [sigman2019].

## Established vs. open
**Established:** Delta notation and the VPDB and AIR standards are settled conventions; the abrupt collapse of the marine surface-to-deep δ¹³C gradient at the K–Pg boundary is a real, repeatedly documented feature, read as a productivity crash [hsu1985][zachos1989]; nitrogen-isotope systematics are a recognized, if more interpretively cautious, proxy for the nitrogen cycle and productivity [sigman2019].

**Open / debated:** The *duration* and *mechanism* of the post-boundary productivity collapse are debated — whether the surface ocean was truly "Strangelove" (near-zero export) or merely reorganized ("living ocean" / heterogeneous models), and how much of the recorded δ¹³C signal reflects diagenesis versus primary ocean chemistry. δ¹⁵N records in particular are vulnerable to post-depositional alteration, so individual ancient δ¹⁵N values carry real uncertainty [sigman2019].

## In *Talosapien*
> The geochemistry above is the real stage, and the book changes none of it: the δ¹³C collapse and the Strangelove ocean are exactly as the literature describes. The fiction is what Asha claims to find *layered into* that signal. Beside the natural productivity-crash δ¹³C excursion she reads a second, sharper carbon excursion that no natural source — not the impact, not the Deccan, not the dying ocean — accounts for, and an anomalous δ¹⁵N excursion whose shape looks like the isotopic fingerprint of *industrial* nitrogen fixation rather than the natural nitrogen cycle (see [Haber–Bosch nitrogen fixation](haber-bosch-nitrogen-fixation.md) for the real process the novel borrows). In reality there is no such industrial δ¹⁵N or anomalous δ¹³C signal at the K–Pg; the natural proxies above are the whole story. In the novel each anomaly is individually deniable — diagenesis, contamination, a bad sample — but together with the [transuranic and fission isotopes](transuranic-fission-isotopes.md) they are the case Asha builds along [the Line](../lore/glossary/the-line.md), the speculative pursuit of the [Silurian hypothesis](../ideas/silurian-hypothesis.md).
