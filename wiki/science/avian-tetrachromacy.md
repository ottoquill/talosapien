# Avian Tetrachromacy

**Type:** science · **Used in:** why the Talos see (and signal in) colours humans cannot · **Related:** [feathered-dinosaurs.md](feathered-dinosaurs.md), [structural-color-signaling.md](structural-color-signaling.md), Talos sapiens

## Summary
Birds have tetrachromatic colour vision: four spectrally distinct single-cone types — typically including one sensitive to short wavelengths in the violet or ultraviolet (UV) range — versus the three cone types of normal human vision. Their cones also carry coloured oil droplets that sharpen colour discrimination. As living dinosaurs, birds make UV/violet-extended tetrachromacy a reasonable (though *inferred*, not directly observed) ancestral condition for non-avian dinosaurs.

## The science
Hart's review of avian photoreceptors lays out the standard picture: birds possess rods plus several cone classes, including four types of single cone whose visual pigments span the spectrum, with the shortest-wavelength class sensitive in the violet/UV region, giving genuine tetrachromacy [hart2001]. This exceeds human trichromacy both in the number of independent colour channels and in spectral reach into the UV. A second avian feature amplifies this: each cone contains a coloured oil droplet that acts as a long-pass filter in front of the photoreceptor, narrowing and separating the effective spectral sensitivities and thereby improving colour discrimination — a refinement the human eye lacks [hart2001]. Hart notes the multiplicity of pigment and photoreceptor types and that spectral tuning varies between bird species according to habitat and visual task [hart2001].

Because birds are nested within Dinosauria, the same opsin-based colour system is reasonably inferred for many non-avian dinosaurs, and an extra UV/violet channel is part of the broader reptilian/archosaur condition. This is an *inference from phylogeny*, not a measurement on extinct animals — fossils do not preserve retinas — and that distinction matters for honest science writing.

## Established vs. open
**Established:** Living birds are tetrachromatic, with four single-cone types (often UV/violet-sensitive) and coloured oil droplets, exceeding human trichromacy [hart2001].

**Open / debated:** The colour vision of *non-avian* dinosaurs is inferred from living relatives, not observed; the exact spectral tuning (true-UV vs. violet-shifted) varies among birds and is unknown for any extinct dinosaur. Treat "dinosaurs saw UV" as a phylogenetic inference, not a fact about a specific fossil taxon.

## In *Talosapien*
> The Talos seeing into the UV/violet and reading colour channels invisible to humans is grounded in **real** avian tetrachromacy and oil-droplet optics [hart2001]; extending it to a non-avian-derived lineage is a defensible phylogenetic inference. The novel's invention is what the Talos *do* with it: a signalling system (and a [crest-flush](../lore/glossary/crest-flush.md)) that operates partly in bands humans cannot perceive, so human observers literally cannot see the whole conversation. Their wide-band colour vision is also why the displays of [structural-color-signaling.md](structural-color-signaling.md) carry so much information for them. The biology is real; the sapient use is fiction.
