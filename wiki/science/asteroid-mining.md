# Asteroid Mining

**Type:** science · **Used in:** the Ark; the Wheels · **Related:** the asteroid belt, O'Neill cylinders, delta-v

## Summary
Asteroid mining is the prospective extraction of useful materials — water and other volatiles, and metals — from near-Earth and main-belt asteroids, for use *in space* rather than on Earth. Its appeal rests on in-situ resource utilization (ISRU): a habitat or spacecraft that can source water, oxygen, and structural metal from a nearby body avoids the enormous cost of lifting that mass out of Earth's gravity well. The industry remains aspirational, but the *characterization* of candidate asteroids is now real: sample-return missions to the carbonaceous asteroids Ryugu (Hayabusa2) and Bennu (OSIRIS-REx) have returned and analysed material confirming abundant water-bearing minerals and organics.

## The science
The economic logic is the same one that drove O'Neill's habitat designs: launching mass from Earth is expensive, so anything you can obtain from material already in space — especially water and rock — is hugely advantageous (see [O'Neill cylinders](oneill-cylinders.md) and [delta-v](delta-v-orbital-mechanics.md)). Two resource classes dominate proposals: **volatiles**, chiefly water (which can be split into hydrogen and oxygen for propellant and life support), most abundant in carbonaceous (C-type) asteroids; and **metals** — iron, nickel, and platinum-group metals — concentrated in metallic (M-type) bodies. These map directly onto the belt's composition classes (see [the asteroid belt](asteroid-belt.md)).

What has actually been done is *characterization*, not mining. Two sample-return missions are the ground truth:

- **Hayabusa2 / Ryugu (JAXA):** Samples returned from the C-type near-Earth asteroid (162173) Ryugu were found to be compositionally similar to **Ivuna-type (CI) carbonaceous chondrites** — the most chemically primitive meteorites known, whose composition closely matches the Sun's photosphere [yokoyama2023]. The Ryugu material is dominated by minerals that formed through reaction with **liquid water on the parent body**, direct evidence that such asteroids carry hydrated, water-bearing minerals [yokoyama2023].

- **OSIRIS-REx / Bennu (NASA):** The mission returned **121.6 g** of pristine regolith from the near-Earth asteroid (101955) Bennu — the largest asteroid sample ever collected in space, more than twice the 60 g the mission required [nasaosirismass]. Early laboratory analysis found Bennu to be a hydrated, carbon-rich carbonaceous body, dominated by water-bearing clay minerals (serpentine) along with carbon- and organic-rich material [lauretta2024].

Together these results confirm the central premise of volatile-rich asteroid mining: water-bearing, carbon-rich asteroids are real and reachable, and their composition can now be measured in the laboratory rather than inferred from telescopes [yokoyama2023][lauretta2024]. Earlier near-Earth rendezvous and the original Hayabusa mission to the S-type asteroid Itokawa established the engineering of approaching, touching, and sampling such bodies.

## Established vs. open
**Established:** That near-Earth and belt asteroids contain water-bearing minerals, metals, and organics, and that material can be reached, sampled, and returned — these are now demonstrated facts from flown missions [yokoyama2023][lauretta2024][nasaosirismass].

**Open / debated:** *Commercial* asteroid mining at any meaningful scale does not yet exist. Whether it is economically viable, what extraction and processing methods would work in microgravity, and the legal regime for off-world resources are all unresolved. The realistic near-term frontier is prospecting and small-scale ISRU demonstrations, not an asteroid-metals industry.

## In *Talosapien*
> In the novel the Talos had moved well past prospecting into full industrial ISRU: [the Wheels](../lore/glossary/the-wheels.md) and the wider [Ark](../lore/glossary/the-ark.md) were built and provisioned from Belt material — water and volatiles from C-type bodies, structural metal from M-types — exactly the resource map real planetary scientists are confirming today. See [the asteroid (tech)](../lore/tech/the-asteroid.md) and [the Wheels and Ark (tech)](../lore/tech/the-wheels-and-ark.md).
> The novel's darkest unresolved use of this infrastructure is the suggestion that the same Ark mining-and-towing capability that built the Wheels could have been turned to *moving* a Belt body onto an Earth-crossing path — the Chicxulub impactor as redirected asteroid. The book never resolves whether this was a weapon, a failed gambit, or an erasure; the orbital-mechanics plausibility is treated in [delta-v](delta-v-orbital-mechanics.md) and [asteroid deflection](asteroid-deflection.md).
> The mission results and resource science above are real; the ancient industrial-scale operation is the fiction.
