# Late Cretaceous Paleogeography

**Type:** science · **Used in:** the world the boundary clay was laid down in; Tanis and the Western Interior · **Related:** Maastrichtian; Tanis deathbed deposit; the Line

## Summary
In the latest Cretaceous, global sea level stood high and broad, shallow seas flooded the interiors of continents. In North America the Western Interior Seaway split the continent in two — the landmass Laramidia to the west and Appalachia to the east — connecting Arctic and Gulf waters across the present-day Great Plains. That seaway was already withdrawing during the Maastrichtian and was gone by the end of the period. This drowned-continent geography is the physical stage on which the K–Pg boundary sediments — and the novel's anomalies within them — were deposited.

## The science
The Cretaceous was a time of unusually high global (eustatic) sea level. Synthesizing global stratigraphic records, Haq reconstructed Cretaceous sea-level history and confirmed that long-term eustatic levels stood well above the present, peaking in the early Late Cretaceous (Turonian) and remaining high through much of the period, while distinguishing genuinely global eustatic change from local/regional shifts driven by inherited topography and tectonics (which he termed "eurybatic" shifts) [haq2014]. High eustasy, combined with the room created by tectonic subsidence, flooded low-lying continental interiors to produce extensive shallow epicontinental (epeiric) seas.

The most prominent of these in North America was the Western Interior Seaway. During the Late Cretaceous, North America was divided into two landmasses — Laramidia to the west and Appalachia to the east — by this epicontinental seaway [maidment2021]. The seaway extended across the continental interior, broadly connecting northern (Arctic/Boreal) waters with the Gulf of Mexico to the south. It was not permanent: it advanced and retreated with sea-level fluctuations, and during the Maastrichtian — the final stage of the Cretaceous — it underwent overall regression, withdrawing from the continental interior toward the close of the period [maidment2021]. The marine and marginal-marine sediments laid down along its shifting margins (including the Hell Creek–Fox Hills–Pierre Shale succession of the northern Great Plains) are the strata that host many of the best-studied K–Pg boundary sections in North America.

## Established vs. open
**Established:** Cretaceous global sea level stood high relative to the present and flooded continental interiors with epicontinental seas [haq2014]; the Western Interior Seaway split Late Cretaceous North America into Laramidia and Appalachia and regressed through the Maastrichtian to disappear near the K–Pg boundary [maidment2021].

**Open / debated:** The precise *amplitudes* of Cretaceous eustatic highstands, and how to separate true global eustasy from regional tectonic and isostatic effects, remain actively debated [haq2014]; the detailed timing, drivers, and step-by-step geometry of the Western Interior Seaway's final regression are likewise still refined by ongoing work.

## In *Talosapien*
> This paleogeography is real and the novel keeps it as the literal setting: a Maastrichtian world of high seas and a draining Western Interior Seaway, with the [Tanis deathbed deposit](tanis-deathbed-deposit.md) sitting in exactly the kind of marginal-marine, river-fed setting the real stratigraphy provides. The fiction lies not in the geography but in what the boundary clay deposited on this stage is claimed to contain — the [transuranic and fission isotopes](transuranic-fission-isotopes.md) and the industrial [δ¹³C/δ¹⁵N excursions](d13c-d15n-excursions.md) that Asha reads along [the Line](../lore/glossary/the-line.md). The drowned-continent world is the believable, accurate frame that makes the buried anomaly feel like it belongs to a real place.
