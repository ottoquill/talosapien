# O'Neill Cylinders

**Type:** science · **Used in:** the Wheels; the Ark · **Related:** artificial gravity by rotation, asteroid mining, the Belt

## Summary
In the mid-1970s the Princeton physicist Gerard K. O'Neill proposed that large, self-sufficient human settlements could be built free-floating in space rather than on planetary surfaces, using material mined from the Moon and asteroids and assembled at gravitationally stable points. His flagship design — later called the "O'Neill cylinder" — is a pair of enormous counter-rotating cylinders that spin to produce Earth-normal pseudogravity on their inner surface. The concept was developed in O'Neill's 1974 *Physics Today* article and his 1976 book *The High Frontier*, and was elaborated by a 1975 NASA–Stanford summer study.

## The science
O'Neill argued, against the then-default assumption that colonization meant landing on Mars or the Moon, that the energetically and economically sensible target was open space itself: a habitat could be sited where sunlight is continuous, raw material is reachable, and there is no deep gravity well to climb back out of [oneill1974]. Pseudogravity would be supplied not by mass but by rotation — spinning the habitat so that its inhabitants are pressed against the inner wall by the centripetal requirement of circular motion (see [artificial gravity by rotation](artificial-gravity-rotation.md) for the mechanics) [oneill1974].

The most ambitious of O'Neill's designs, which he labelled "Model 3" (popularly the "Island Three" or O'Neill cylinder), is a pair of cylinders on the order of several kilometres in radius and tens of kilometres long, arranged so that the two members of the pair rotate in opposite directions [oneill1974][oneill1976]. The counter-rotation is deliberate: a single large spinning body would behave as a gyroscope and resist being re-aimed at the Sun over the orbital year, but a coupled counter-rotating pair has near-zero net angular momentum and can be turned to track the Sun without fighting gyroscopic torque [oneill1976]. Each cylinder is divided lengthwise into alternating "land" strips and transparent "window" strips, with external mirrors steering sunlight in to drive a day–night cycle and agriculture [oneill1974][oneill1976].

Crucially, O'Neill's case rested on *where the mass comes from*. Lifting habitat-scale tonnage up from Earth's surface is prohibitive; instead he proposed extracting structural metals, oxygen, and soil feedstock from lunar regolith (launched off the Moon by an electromagnetic "mass-driver") and, in later stages, from asteroids, with construction taking place at the Earth–Moon Lagrange points (notably L4/L5) where a structure can sit in a stable orbit relative to both bodies [oneill1974][oneill1976]. The 1975 NASA Ames / Stanford summer study, published as NASA SP-413 *Space Settlements: A Design Study*, took these ideas and worked a reference settlement (the toroidal "Stanford torus" variant) through engineering systems design — radiation shielding, agriculture, mass budgets, and the lunar-material supply chain — as a feasibility-level demonstration rather than a construction plan [nasasp413].

## Established vs. open
**Established:** The underlying physics is sound and uncontested — rotation produces a centripetal pseudogravity, counter-rotating pairs cancel net angular momentum, and the orbital mechanics of L4/L5 stability are textbook [oneill1974][nasasp413]. Nothing in the concept requires new physics.

**Open / debated:** Everything about *feasibility* remains speculative. No structure remotely approaching kilometre-radius cylinders has been built; the required materials science (multi-kilometre pressure vessels), the lunar/asteroid mining and mass-driver logistics, radiation protection over decades, closed-loop life support at city scale, and above all the cost and political will are all unproven. The designs are engineering thought-experiments grounded in real physics, not validated blueprints.

## In *Talosapien*
> *Talosapien* takes O'Neill's idea and asks: what if it had already been done — 66 million years ago, by someone else? The Talos built rotating habitats in the asteroid belt, called **the Wheels**, as an Ark and survival hedge; in the novel these are explicitly O'Neill cylinders in the engineering sense — counter-rotating, spun for pseudogravity, fed by [asteroid mining](asteroid-mining.md). See [the Wheels](../lore/glossary/the-wheels.md), [the Wheels and Ark (tech)](../lore/tech/the-wheels-and-ark.md), and [the Ark](../lore/glossary/the-ark.md).
> The human characters, in the near-future timeline, are only beginning to *reinvent* the same concept — their nascent rotating habitats are nicknamed **gyrealms** — which sharpens the novel's irony: humanity is reaching the High Frontier just as it uncovers proof that another lineage got there first and left wreckage still riding Belt orbits.
> All of this is fiction. The real O'Neill physics in the body above is unchanged; the novel only relocates the *achievement* of it into deep time and onto a non-human builder.
