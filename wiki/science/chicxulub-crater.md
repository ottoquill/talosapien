# The Chicxulub Crater

**Type:** science · **Used in:** the Line; Asha's case; the asteroid question · **Related:** K–Pg boundary, iridium anomaly, shocked quartz

## Summary
Chicxulub is a buried impact crater about 180 km in diameter centred near the town of Chicxulub on Mexico's Yucatán Peninsula. Identified from geophysical anomalies and old oil-exploration wells, it is dated to the K–Pg boundary and is regarded as the structure left by the asteroid impact implicated in the end-Cretaceous mass extinction.

## The science
The crater was proposed by Hildebrand and colleagues, who described a buried ~180-km-diameter circular structure on the Yucatán Peninsula revealed by magnetic and gravity-field anomalies and by oil wells drilled inside and near the structure; the well stratigraphy includes shock-metamorphosed breccias and impact glass whose chemistry resembles K–Pg ejecta, and a thick boundary breccia occurs outside the crater rim [hildebrand1991]. (The Yucatán anomalies had been noted earlier by petroleum geophysicists, including Penfield, a co-author on the discovery paper.) The consensus review of Schulte et al. ties the crater to the global K–Pg ejecta layer — iridium, shocked quartz, and spherules — and to the synchronous extinction [schulte2010].

In 2016 the joint IODP–ICDP Expedition 364 drilled the crater's *peak ring*, recovering cores of uplifted, fractured, shocked felsic basement rock. These cores support the "dynamic collapse" model, in which an over-heightened central uplift collapses outward to form the ring; Chicxulub is the only confirmed terrestrial crater with an intact peak ring, making it a natural laboratory for the mechanics of large impacts [morgan2016]. Independent high-precision dating ties the Chicxulub impact to the K–Pg boundary to within about 32,000 years, reinforcing the causal link to the extinction [renne2013].

## Established vs. open
**Established:** Chicxulub is a real, buried ~180-km impact structure of K–Pg age on the Yucatán; it has a genuine peak ring of shocked basement rock and is the source of the global K–Pg ejecta layer [hildebrand1991][morgan2016][schulte2010].

**Open / debated:** The precise impactor size, angle, and composition, and the relative contribution of the impact versus Deccan volcanism to the extinction, remain actively researched. The existence and age of the crater are not in dispute.

## In *Talosapien*
> Chicxulub is the stage the novel never rebuilds — the crater, its peak ring, its age, and its link to the boundary clay are all taken straight from the science above. What the book adds is a question, never an answer: was the impactor a natural Belt asteroid, or an object that had been *redirected*? The story leaves this unresolved on purpose. The geophysics, the drill cores, and the ejecta are real; the only fiction is the unproven possibility that something chose Chicxulub's trajectory.
