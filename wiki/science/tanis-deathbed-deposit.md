# The Tanis Deathbed Deposit

**Type:** science · **Used in:** the hours-after scene; Asha's case · **Related:** Chicxulub crater, microtektites, K–Pg boundary

## Summary
Tanis is a fossil site in the Hell Creek Formation of southwestern North Dakota that is interpreted to record organisms killed within minutes to hours of the Chicxulub impact. A rapidly emplaced sediment package — capped by the K–Pg boundary clay and laced with impact ejecta spherules, including spherules lodged in fish gills — has been read as a near-instantaneous snapshot of the impact's first day. It is a real and scientifically debated locality.

## The science
DePalma and colleagues describe Tanis as a turbulently deposited, rapidly emplaced sediment body lying directly beneath the K–Pg boundary tonstein in the continental Hell Creek Formation [depalma2019]. The deposit contains ejecta spherules whose calculated arrival times match seismic-wave travel times from Chicxulub: spherules would have begun reaching the site about 15 minutes after impact, with most deposited within roughly one to two hours [depalma2019]. The authors report ejecta spherules in the gills of more than half of the sampled sturgeon and paddlefish, interpreted as aspirated by the fish during the event [depalma2019].

Crucially, the trigger is interpreted not as a transoceanic tsunami (too slow to arrive in time) but as a *seismically induced seiche* — standing waves set off in a local water body by seismic energy from the impact arriving minutes beforehand [depalma2019]. Tanis is significant precisely because it claims to capture a span of time directly following the impact — tens of minutes to hours — a resolution essentially undocumented elsewhere in continental K–Pg sections.

## Established vs. open
**Established:** Tanis is a real K–Pg-aged Hell Creek locality with a rapidly emplaced deposit, impact spherules, and a boundary-clay cap; the published interpretation links its emplacement to Chicxulub seismic energy via a seiche [depalma2019].

**Open / debated:** Some specific high-profile claims associated with the broader Tanis project (for example the precise season of the impact, and certain fossils announced primarily through media) have drawn scrutiny and calls for fuller peer-reviewed documentation. The peer-reviewed seiche-deposit framework above is the citable core; surrounding extraordinary claims should be treated cautiously.

## In *Talosapien*
> Tanis is the book's model for what a "deathbed deposit" can preserve — a layer that records not an epoch but an afternoon. The novel keeps the real science intact: spherules in gills, a seismic seiche, the first hours after impact. Its fictional extension is to imagine a *second* deathbed layer where the spherules are subtly wrong — too uniform, the wrong glass chemistry — manufactured rather than melted. The real Tanis proves such fine resolution is geologically possible; what the team claims to find in it is invented.
