# The Block Universe

**Type:** idea · **Used in:** the fossil record is already written; the Talos thread can't end any other way · **Related:** deep time, future-generations ethics, non-identity problem

## Summary
The "block universe" is a philosophical view of time — more formally, *eternalism* — on which the past, present, and future are all equally real. On this picture there is no objective, moving "now"; the whole of spacetime simply *is*, like a four-dimensional block, and the sense that time "flows" is a feature of how conscious beings are embedded in it rather than a fundamental fact about the world. It is a *position* in the philosophy of time, debated, not a settled result of physics.

## The idea
Eternalism contrasts with "presentism" (only the present moment is real) and the "growing block" view (the past and present are real, the future not yet). On the eternalist/block view, as the Stanford Encyclopedia puts it, "future and past events … are no more or less real than distant events at a time" [sep-bebecome]. The most influential argument for taking the block picture seriously comes from special relativity. Hilary Putnam, in his 1967 paper "Time and Physical Geometry," argued that because relativity makes simultaneity relative to an observer's state of motion, there is no observer-independent fact about which distant events are happening "now"; if reality is not relative to observers, then events we would call future for one observer must be just as real as present ones — collapsing the privileged present [putnam1967]. This line of reasoning, developed independently by C. W. Rietdijk and later associated with Roger Penrose, is known as the Rietdijk–Putnam argument [sep-bebecome].

The conclusion many draw is that the apparent *flow* or *passage* of time is not part of the fundamental furniture of the world — physics describes the ordering and geometry of events but, as Savitt's SEP survey notes, "seem[s] to find no place" for passage itself [sep-bebecome]. The block universe is the metaphysical picture that takes this seriously: every event, including every future one, is "already there" in the four-dimensional whole.

## Established vs. open
**Established:** That relativity makes simultaneity frame-relative is settled physics. That this *has been used* to argue for eternalism, via the Rietdijk–Putnam argument, is a real and well-documented strand of the philosophy of time [putnam1967][sep-bebecome].

**Open / debated:** Whether the argument actually succeeds, and whether the block universe is *true*, are very much open philosophical questions, not settled facts. The SEP survey stresses that reactions to relativity here are nuanced and "it seems unlikely that a simple argument will decide between these two metaphysical pictures" [sep-bebecome]. The block universe should be presented — and is presented in this novel — as a contested philosophical position, not as established physics.

## In *Talosapien*
> The block universe is the novel's quiet structural conceit, and it must be held *as a metaphor*, not a physics claim. The deep-past thread (the [Talos](../world/the-talos.md)) cannot end any other way than it does, because — from the near-future thread's vantage — it has *already* ended: the fossil record is written, the centimeter of clay is laid down, the outcome is fixed in the rock 66 million years before [Asha](../characters/asha-rendel.md) reads it. The reader experiences the Talos chapters with the eternalist's dread: these people are alive on the page and already, irrevocably, dead in the strata, both at once. The book uses the *feeling* of the block universe — the future as something that, seen from far enough away, is already there — to give its tragedy its particular airlessness. It does not assert eternalism is true. See [deep time](deep-time.md).
