# Future-Generations Ethics

**Type:** idea · **Used in:** Liss's clutch; Asha's warning to people not yet born · **Related:** non-identity problem, deep time, vulnerable world hypothesis

## Summary
Future-generations ethics asks what, if anything, we owe to people who do not yet exist — and whose very existence depends on what we do now. Two landmarks frame the field: Derek Parfit's *Reasons and Persons* (1984), which showed how strange and difficult our obligations to future people become once we notice that our choices determine *which* people will exist; and Samuel Scheffler's *Death and the Afterlife* (2013), which argues that the continued existence of humanity after our own deaths matters to us far more than we usually admit.

## The idea
Parfit devotes the final part of *Reasons and Persons* to future generations [parfit1984]. He shows that many ordinary moral intuitions break down when applied across time: policies that seem to "harm" future people often, on closer inspection, change *who* those future people are, so that the very individuals affected owe their existence to the choice in question. This sets up the [non-identity problem](non-identity-problem.md) and the broader difficulty of grounding obligations to a future whose population is not fixed but is itself an outcome of our acts [parfit1984]. Parfit's treatment made future-generations ethics a serious, technical field rather than a vague gesture toward "our grandchildren."

Scheffler approaches the question from a different angle. In *Death and the Afterlife* — drawn from his Berkeley Tanner Lectures and published with commentaries — he uses a thought experiment (imagine learning that, though you will live a normal lifespan, all human life will end shortly after your own death) to argue that the prospect would drain much present activity of its meaning [scheffler2013]. His conclusion is that the survival of humanity beyond our individual lives — what he calls the "afterlife," meaning the ongoing human future, not a personal one — matters to us to a degree we rarely acknowledge, and in some respects more than our own survival [scheffler2013]. The continuation of people we will never meet is, he argues, quietly load-bearing for the value we find in our own lives.

## Established vs. open
**Established:** These are the canonical philosophical texts of the field, and their core moves are not in dispute as *positions*: Parfit really did set the agenda for population ethics and identify the non-identity difficulty [parfit1984]; Scheffler really does argue that humanity's continuation matters profoundly to the living [scheffler2013]. Future-generations ethics is a recognized, active area of moral philosophy.

**Open / debated:** Almost every substantive answer — how to weigh future against present interests, how to handle the non-identity problem, whether there is a duty to *create* happy people, how much humanity's continuation grounds present meaning. These are arguments, not settled facts, and reasonable philosophers disagree.

## In *Talosapien*
> The novel turns these abstractions into bodies. [Liss's](../characters/liss.md) clutch — eggs that will hatch into a world she already knows is ending — is Scheffler's thought experiment made literal and unbearable: a parent for whom the human (Talos) future is not a philosophical premise but the thing she is physically carrying. And [Asha's](../characters/asha-rendel.md) entire project is a warning addressed to people not yet born — the deepest form of the future-generations problem, because the people her warning is *for* will be the ones our choices bring into existence (or don't). The book never cites Parfit or Scheffler; it stages the question across 66 million years, twice. See also [the near future](../world/near-future.md).
