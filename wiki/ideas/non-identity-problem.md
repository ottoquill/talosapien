# The Non-Identity Problem

**Type:** idea · **Used in:** Asha's warning to people not yet born; Liss's clutch · **Related:** future-generations ethics, deep time

## Summary
The non-identity problem is a puzzle that Derek Parfit brought to prominence: many of our choices do not merely affect future people, they determine *which* future people exist at all. When that is so, it becomes strangely hard to say that those people have been *harmed* by a choice — even a reckless one — because had we chosen otherwise, they would never have existed to be harmed. The problem bites hardest exactly where we most want to ground our obligations to the future.

## The idea
Parfit develops the problem in Part Four of *Reasons and Persons* [parfit1984]. The mechanism is identity-dependence: who comes into existence depends sensitively on timing and circumstance (which two people conceive, when, under what conditions). A policy that, say, depletes resources or degrades the climate over generations does not make a *fixed* set of future people worse off; it brings into being a *different* set of people from the ones who would have existed under a better policy [parfit1984]. So a person born into the degraded future cannot straightforwardly claim the policy made *them* worse off — their only alternative was non-existence, and (if their life is still worth living) non-existence is not obviously better for them.

This is unsettling because it appears to vindicate intuitively wrong choices: a "depletion" policy seems clearly bad, yet there may be no particular person who is worse off than they otherwise would have been. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy summarizes the puzzle as the difficulty that any change we contemplate "will often fail to make *that* person better off and often instead serve only to bring another person … into existence in place of the one" [sep-nonidentity]. The problem has driven much of population ethics: attempts to solve it include impersonal principles (what matters is the *quality* of the future, not whether identifiable individuals are harmed), wide person-affecting views, and others — none uncontroversially successful.

## Established vs. open
**Established:** That the problem is *real* and was decisively framed by Parfit is not in dispute [parfit1984][sep-nonidentity]. It is a genuine and widely discussed difficulty: identity-affecting choices resist analysis in ordinary "harm to a person" terms.

**Open / debated:** Whether — and how — it can be solved. There is no consensus solution; impersonal approaches, person-affecting refinements, and skeptical responses all have defenders and critics [sep-nonidentity]. The problem is a permanent fixture of the field precisely because it has no agreed answer.

## In *Talosapien*
> The non-identity problem is the philosophical fine structure under [Asha's](../characters/asha-rendel.md) warning. She is pleading on behalf of people not yet born — but the future she is trying to prevent would have its *own* people, who owe their existence to the very trajectory she opposes. Her warning cannot be grounded in "you will harm *these* specific future individuals," because the choice determines who they are. The book doesn't argue this on the page; it lets the vertigo sit under the deep-time frame, where the Talos future already happened and its people already existed and ended. [Liss's](../characters/liss.md) clutch sharpens it from the other side: the children she carries are the ones *this* ending will produce, not some better-off alternative. See also [future-generations ethics](future-generations-ethics.md) and [deep time](deep-time.md).
