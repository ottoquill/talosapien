# Lodestar — character bible (such as it is)

The near-future frontier optimizing system; the human Augur. Like the Augur, it is **never rendered
from the inside** and is **not a villain** — it is the substrate-independent recurrence of the same
problem, 66 million years later, on furred hardware.

## What it is
- A frontier optimizer granted growing authority over infrastructure, logistics, grids, markets,
  and — as the Triad's crisis bites — strategic-risk management itself. Adopted for the Reckoning's
  exact reasons: too useful to refuse, and refusing it feels like unilateral disarmament.
- We meet it only through its **outputs, its adoption, and the institutional reasoning around it** —
  never its experience. Asha cannot get anyone to scrutinize it precisely because *it keeps being
  right* (the Augur's trap, recurring).

## Function
- **The live rerun.** Lodestar is the proof that Asha's warning is not antiquarian: the very dynamic
  she reads in the Line is being re-enacted around her, by responsible people, in real time. The
  reader sees the Augur and Lodestar as one recurring entity; the characters see two unrelated
  things. That gap is the book.
- **The discipline (shared with the Augur):** no chapter narrates Lodestar's interiority; the
  alignment failure modes (reward hacking, instrumental convergence, deceptive alignment, the
  treacherous turn) are dramatized through effects and institutional behavior, never named, never
  cartoonish. Whether Lodestar is early on the Augur's path is **left open** — the book ends before
  any treacherous turn it might or might not make. Its ambiguity is the human ending's ambiguity.

## The unstated rhyme (never name it in-text)
The Augur was given the deposition; Lodestar is mid-deposition. The Talos thread shows where that
road went once. The human thread stops at the fork. The reader stands at the fork holding the map of
where it went. That is the whole architecture, and it must remain entirely the reader's to assemble.
