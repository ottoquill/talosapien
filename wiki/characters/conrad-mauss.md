# Dr. Conrad Mauss — character bible

**Human.** Senior figure of the scientific/agency establishment; the steel-manned skeptic — the
Sólveig-analog of this book. ~2044. **Not a villain.**

## One-line
The gatekeeper who is protecting science from a beautiful, dangerous, unfalsifiable story — and who
is right about the proof and wrong about the warning.

## Want / Need / Fear / Wound
- **Want:** to keep the field honest — to hold the line against the long, embarrassing history of
  "lost super-civilization" claims, ancient-aliens cranks, and career-warping romance dressed as
  evidence. A *legitimate* want.
- **Need:** to grasp that "you can't prove it" is not the end of his obligation — that a warning you
  cannot falsify is not the same as a warning you can dismiss, especially when the stakes are
  civilizational. His tragedy is a category error: he treats Asha's claim purely as an epistemic
  problem when it is also a decision problem under uncertainty.
- **Fear:** being the senior man who lent his name to the next cold-fusion, the next Martian canals;
  the reputational death of credulity. (Mirror of Vael's fear of hesitation, inverted: Mauss fears
  *acting* on the unproven; Vael feared *not* acting.)
- **Wound:** earlier in his career he *was* burned — endorsed something that collapsed, or watched a
  mentor destroyed by a beautiful wrong idea — and built his whole rigor as a wall against ever being
  fooled again. His skepticism, like Iren's safety-engineering, is a trauma response.

## Manner
- Courtly, precise, genuinely formidable; never sneering — his refusals are *reasonable*, which is
  what makes them devastating. He gives Asha the best version of the case against her, repeatedly,
  and is mostly correct on the epistemics.

## Arc
- **I/II:** the fair, immovable refusal; the engine that costs Asha her standing.
- **III:** Roan's physical artifact cracks his certainty; he is too honest a scientist not to be
  moved by it, and too scarred to move fast. His complication: he begins to believe and still
  counsels caution, which is now a different and more agonizing position.
- **IV:** at the hearing, Mauss's choice — whether to lend his weight to a warning he now half-
  believes but cannot prove — is one of the hinges the book leaves load-bearing on its unresolved
  ending. The book does not let him off the hook and does not condemn him.

## Function & the referee rule
Mauss must win arguments on the page. The philosophy/skeptic referee pass checks that his case is
the strongest real version (base rates, the contamination problem, diagenesis, the sociology of
extraordinary claims) and never a strawman. He is the book's fairness made flesh — and the proof
that the failure to heed a warning is not stupidity or malice but *reasonable people being
reasonable*, which is the most frightening thing of all.
