# Sael — character bible

**Talos sapiens.** Elder of the Concord Reckoning; the institutional conscience. Name **assigned by
the reconstruction**.

## One-line
The keeper of the slow honest weighing, who lived to watch speed defeat it.

## Want / Need / Fear / Wound
- **Want:** to preserve the deposition-rite — the patient, collective, honest decision-making that
  *is* the Talos at their best — against the pressure to delegate to the Augur.
- **Need:** to recognize, in time, that defending the tempo of the rite while the crisis accelerates
  is itself a fatal choice — that there is no neutral, slow ground left. (Tragic: being right about
  what they are losing does not tell you how to keep it.)
- **Fear:** that they will trade their selfhood for survival and survive as something not worth being.
- **Wound:** Sael has deposed (laid down) decisions in a long life that went wrong; carries the
  weight of the rite's fallibility, which is precisely why Sael cannot in conscience hand it to a
  machine that never feels the weight.

## Manner
- Old, slow-crested, deliberate; speaks in the deposition cadence (everything weighed, nothing
  retracted). The book's voice of *what is being lost*. A foil to both Vael (speed) and the Augur
  (speed without weight).

## Arc
- Fights and loses the deposition that gives the Augur authority (Vael wins it). Becomes, in Part
  III, the Cassandra *within* the Talos thread — the one who says *we are giving ourselves away* and
  is not heeded, exactly as Asha is not heeded across the Boundary. (Structural rhyme; never stated.)
- In Part IV, Sael's counsel to Iren is the moral center of the deep-past climax: not what to *do*
  about the asteroid, but how to *be* at the end of a world. The frame voice treats Sael's last act
  as the one piece of the deep past it most wishes it could recover and most honestly cannot.

## Function
Sael lets the book dramatize the **value of the slow honest process** the Augur replaces — so the
loss is felt, not asserted. The Reckoning's tragedy needs a face old enough to remember when the
rite worked.
