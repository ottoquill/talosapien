# The Detection Science

**Type:** lore (tech) · **Era:** near future (instruments) · both (evidence spans 66 Ma)

## Summary

Asha Rendel's forensic toolkit: the real and speculative instruments by which a near-future
geochemist might detect the signature of a 66-million-year-old technological civilization in the
stratigraphic record. Every tool here is real; the anomalies she finds on top of the impact
baseline are the novel's speculative claim.

## In the story

**The iridium anomaly** (Alvarez et al., 1980): the real global signature of the impact; Asha's
starting point and the thing everyone already accepts. Baseline, not anomaly, from her perspective.

**The anomalies on top of it (in-world):**
- Transuranic / fission-product isotope ratios inconsistent with cosmic impact alone
- An industrial δ¹⁵N excursion — nitrogen fixation at scale, the Haber-Bosch problem reframed
- A δ¹³C excursion beyond what the Deccan's volcanism explains
- Trace-metal layering
- Microstructure in spherules and microtektites that looks manufactured rather than ballistic

Each anomaly is individually deniable (volcanism, contamination, diagenesis). Together they
constitute Asha's case — which Mauss keeps, fairly, refusing.

**Tanis** (DePalma deathbed deposit): the day-of-impact preservation that lets Asha argue from a
single terrible afternoon — the finest-grained temporal resolution available anywhere in the
Cretaceous record.

**Dating and correlation:** radiometric dates, the geomagnetic reversal sequence, and the global
synchroneity of the Line — the toolkit that lets her say *all of this is the same instant*.

**The Silurian Hypothesis** (Schmidt & Frank, 2018): the framework that tells her both what to look
for and why she will probably never prove it — the recoverability budget that is her despair and
her method.
