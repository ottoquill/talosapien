# The Schmidt–Frank Problem

**Type:** lore (glossary) · **Era:** near future / frame

## Summary

In-world shorthand for the central epistemic despair: a prior industrial civilization would be nearly undetectable, so being right is almost unprovable. Also called the Silurian problem.

## Details

The Schmidt–Frank problem (also: the Silurian problem, in-world) is Asha's term for the epistemic trap at the center of her case: if a prior industrial civilization existed and was destroyed, the evidence for it would be so degraded after tens of millions of years that a scientist holding the correct interpretation could not prove it by the normal standards of extraordinary evidence for extraordinary claims. Being right and being able to demonstrate being right are decoupled.

Named for the in-world scientists (Schmidt and Frank) who formalized the argument in the novel's near-future. The real-world version is the Silurian Hypothesis (Gavin Schmidt and Adam Frank, 2018): the question of whether a prior industrial civilization on Earth would be detectable in the geological record, and the uncomfortable answer that it might not be.

The problem cuts both ways: it makes Asha's case very hard to prove, but it also means the absence of detectable evidence is not evidence of absence. Conrad Mauss's methodological objections are correct on the first half; Asha's response focuses on the second. Neither is wrong. That is the structure of her tragedy.
