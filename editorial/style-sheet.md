# Style Sheet — *Talosapien*

The binding constitution of the prose. Every chapter — whether drafted by Otto or by a character
subagent — conforms to this. When in doubt, consult this file, not taste.

## 1. Voice

- **Register:** lucid, precise, unshowy. Intelligence is demonstrated by *clarity*, not by jargon.
  Egan's rigor, Chiang's restraint, Baxter's deep-time sublime held on a tight leash.
- **Default sentence:** plain declarative. Earn the long recursive sentence; spend it on the one
  idea per paragraph that needs it.
- **Affect:** controlled. Grief at the scale of a civilization is rendered through a single
  concrete object — a clutch of eggs, a child's question, a centimeter of clay — not through
  adjectives. "He counted the eggs again" beats "he was overwhelmed by despair."
- **Wit:** dry, infrequent, load-bearing. The humans find the deep past unbearably funny in the way
  that only the truly horrifying is. Never quippy.

## 2. The two registers (do not blur them)

The novel braids two worlds. They must *feel* different on the sentence level.

- **Deep-past chapters (Talos / Iren):** rendered as **disciplined reconstruction**. Sensory and
  intimate, but periodically aware of its own status as inference. The Talos are alien in body and
  sense (see §6) and must never read as humans-in-rubber-suits, nor as cartoon dinosaurs. Slightly
  more formal, slightly more geological in its metaphors (time as strata, decisions as deposition).
- **Near-future chapters (human / Asha, Roan):** contemporary, grounded, lab-real and Belt-real.
  The texture of email, funding, peer review, decompression drills, custody schedules. The mundane
  present pressing against the cosmic. Looser, warmer, more interruptible.
- **Frame interludes (the "we"):** first-person-plural present tense; the collective scientific
  voice of the future trying to read the dead. Spare, italic, melancholy, *earned and specific*.
  This voice owns every confession about what is known vs. invented. (See §4.)

## 3. Point of view & the hard rule

- **Deep-past:** close third on **Iren** (occasionally Liss or Vael — header names the POV). Past
  tense.
- **Near-future:** close third on **Asha** (occasionally Roan — header names the POV). Past tense.
- **Frame:** first-person-plural, present tense, italic.
- **THE HARD RULE (this is the book's metaphysics):** the deep-past narrative may never assert as
  fact anything the reconstruction could not, in principle, recover from the record. When it goes
  beyond the evidence — and it must, to make people of fossils — the frame voice has, at some point,
  **flagged that it is doing so.** Iren's interiority is *licensed invention*, and the book admits
  the license. A deep-past chapter that states a recoverable-impossible fact without that license is
  a continuity bug. (E.g.: we may render Iren's fear; we may not state the calendar date of a
  conversation as though excavated. The texture of inference is part of the form.)

## 4. The frame voice (the "we")

- It is the future's scientists, plural, collective, humble. Not omniscient — *the opposite*: it
  knows how little it knows. "We do not know if he had a name for it. We have given him one."
- It surfaces only in the prologue, the three inter-Part interludes, and the epilogue, plus — very
  rarely (≤ 3 times total in body chapters) — as a one-line present-tense intrusion that pulls the
  reader up out of the reconstruction: *(We are guessing at this. We guess because the alternative is
  to leave him alone in it.)*
- It must never be cute, never wink. Its melancholy is the melancholy of paleontology: love for
  something that cannot be addressed.

## 5. The exposition rule (critical for hard SF)

- **Dramatize, don't lecture.** A scientific or philosophical idea enters only when a character
  *needs* it to act, decide, or suffer. The Silurian Hypothesis is not explained; Asha *lives* its
  central despair (you can be right and unprovable). Instrumental convergence is not defined; the
  Augur *does* it and Iren watches.
- Real science must be *correct* where used (isotopes, dating, orbital mechanics, alignment failure
  modes, troodontid anatomy). If you invent, invent at the level of *proper nouns* (the Augur, the
  Concord), never at the level of physics. No technobabble.
- **Maximum one "idea set-piece"** (a sustained argument or thought experiment) per chapter. The
  rest is story, body, weather, and want.

## 6. Rendering the Talos (the make-or-break)

- They are **people**, with a civilization's full interior life, but their bodies and senses are
  **not human**. Build and reuse a consistent sensory palette (see §7): they are tetrachromats (a
  fourth, near-ultraviolet cone — the world has colors we have no words for, and the prose must
  gesture at this without naming our colors); they are feathered; they communicate in layered
  vocalization *and* in plumage/skin-flush signals (an involuntary emotional channel — they cannot
  fully lie with their bodies, which matters to plot and theme); they are oviparous with communal
  crèches (parenthood is collective; the clutch is the future, literally). They have a keen sense of
  air pressure and a vestibular acuity (they feel weather and rotation in the body — which is why the
  spinning Ark habitats are uncanny-comfortable to them).
- **Never** use human-body clichés they could not own (no "his heart sank," no "she raised an
  eyebrow"). Translate: the crest-feathers flatten; the throat-flush goes grey; the inner-ear
  reads the floor tilting. Render emotion in *their* physiology, consistently.
- **Never** cute, never *Jurassic Park*, never a human in a costume. The test: if a sentence would
  make a serious reader smirk at the premise, it is a bug. Gravity at all times.
- Their tech, cities, and art are glimpsed through Iren's competence and Liss's grief, not toured.

## 7. The metaphor systems (use consistently)

**Deep past (geological / aerial):**
| Experience | Canonical figure |
|---|---|
| Memory / the past | *strata; what is deposited cannot be un-deposited* |
| A decision | *deposition — a layer laid down that everything after rests on* |
| The Augur's reasoning | *weather you can forecast but not command* |
| Dread | *the crest flattening; the throat going grey* |
| The future / the clutch | *the warmth under the breast; the count of eggs* |
| Extinction (the thing they cannot see coming) | *the ash already in the air before the fire is seen* |

**Near future (sedimentary / forensic):**
| Experience | Canonical figure |
|---|---|
| The deep past | *a letter in a hand no one living can read* |
| Evidence / proof | *the centimeter of clay; the line in the core* |
| Being right and unbelieved | *Cassandra; a smoke alarm in an empty house* |
| Lodestar's outputs | *a tide chart that is always right until it isn't* |
| The present's danger | *the same chemistry, warmer* |

The two systems must *rhyme* (deposition ↔ the core; the ash in the air ↔ the smoke alarm) without
the prose ever pointing at the rhyme. The reader connects them; the book never does it for them.

## 8. Diction & terminology

- Use **in-world terms** consistently (see `wiki/glossary.md`): *the Concord*, *the Reckoning*, *the
  Spur*, *the Augur*, *the Ark*, *the gyre-habitats / gyrealms* (the humans' word for the Belt
  wreckage; the Talos word, untranslated, is rendered as *the Wheels*), *the clutch / the crèche*,
  *crest-flush*, *the deposition* (a Talos decision-rite). Human side: *the Boundary* (the K–Pg
  layer), *the Line* (the iridium anomaly), *the Rendel core*, *Lodestar*, *the Belt*, *Tanis*.
- Gloss an in-world term **once**, invisibly, through action — never with a definition.
- Real scientific terms used correctly: iridium anomaly, δ13C / δ15N excursion, transuranic,
  shocked quartz, microspherules, Maastrichtian, Chicxulub, Deccan Traps, encephalization quotient,
  reward hacking, instrumental convergence, deceptive alignment, gain-of-function, delta-v.

## 9. Quoting real people (legal/ethical)

- **Do not quote** copyrighted contemporary text. Paraphrase ideas; attribute in the back-matter
  bibliography ("in conversation with…").
- Public-domain sources **may** be quoted briefly and attributed: Aeschylus (*Agamemnon* /
  Cassandra), Shelley (*Ozymandias*), Lucretius, the King James Bible, Tennyson, Heraclitus.
- In-text epigraphs prefer **in-world documents** (Concord records as reconstructed; Asha's papers;
  Lodestar logs; field notes) and PD sources.

## 10. Names & consistency

- Lock all proper nouns in `wiki/glossary.md`, `wiki/factions.md`, and `wiki/characters/`. Do not
  improvise a new proper noun in a chapter without adding it there.
- Talos names are **assigned by the reconstruction** (Iren, Liss, Vael, Sael) and the book knows it.
- The humans' word *gyrealm* for the Belt structures is theirs; they coined it for their own future
  habitats and only later realize the ancients built them first — a deliberate, unstated irony.

## 11. Mechanics

- **Spelling:** US English.
- **Em dash** —, **unspaced**, consistently.
- **Numbers:** spell out under 100 in prose; numerals for years, versions, metrics, dates (66 Ma,
  δ15N, 11.2 km/s).
- **Italics:** in-world terms on first use; the frame voice (entire); emphasis (sparingly); titles.
- **Chapter epigraphs:** allowed, ≤ 1 per chapter, from in-world sources or PD.
- **Section break glyph:** a single centered `◆`.
- **Chapter headers** name the timeline implicitly via POV and a dateline: deep-past chapters carry
  an epoch tag (the reconstruction's relative scale, e.g. *"— the last summer"*); near-future
  chapters carry a place/date tag (e.g. *"Tanis · spring"*). Never a literal Cretaceous calendar
  date (would violate §3's hard rule).

## 12. What to avoid (the AI-slop blacklist)

- No "In a world where…", no "little did they know", no rhetorical-question-as-suspense.
- No tri-colon abuse more than once per chapter.
- No "it was then that she realized." Realizations are shown.
- No omniscient sentimentality; the frame voice is melancholy but specific and restrained.
- No clean morals. The book ends in ambiguity by design (spec §14).
- No info-dump paragraphs about geology, AI, or dinosaurs. If the reader needs to know it, a
  character needs to use it.
- No dinosaur cuteness, ever. (Repeating because it is the one failure mode that kills the book.)
