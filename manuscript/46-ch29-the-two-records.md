# The Two Records

The keeping-book was not in the crèche, because she had stopped keeping it in the crèche when the count went to none for the criers, and she could not write a none in the same room where the four were loud. So it was on the seaward rail in its wrap of treated hide, where she had left it that last morning open to the page with the count of the day, and where the surge found it.

We are going to follow it down, because we can. This is the one thing in the whole account that we can follow further than we followed him, and we are going to do it, not because it is bearable but because the alternative is to look away from the only piece of the end whose physics we actually hold.

The wall of water took the rail. It took the rail and the warm stone and the terraces and the wrap of hide and the book inside the wrap, and it rolled all of it inland in the dark, turning, in a slurry of river-mud and sand and the falling sky already cooling out of the air, and the book turned in the slurry with everything else that had been a city an hour before, and the medium she had written in—we do not know what it was; some worked plant-fiber, some pressed and sized surface that took a mark and held it; we have never found one; we are inferring the book itself from the habit of a woman we invented—the medium she had written in came apart in the water the way everything she had ever loved came apart, slowly at first and then all at once, the marks lifting off the surface and dispersing into the load the surge carried, the count of the day and the morning and the air and the line she let whatever read it draw for itself, all of it going into solution, into the same suspended load as the bone and the down and the shell, so that when the surge slowed in the slack hours and let its burden fall, what settled over the drowned delta was a single graded bed of everything the city had been, mixed past separation, and her record was in it, and was indistinguishable in it from the mud, because it had become the mud, because that is what the mud is.

There is no page down there. We want to be exact, because she was exact. There is no recoverable mark, no fiber holding a sign, nothing that an instrument of ours will ever lift out of the bed and read as the thing she set down. The keeping-book is not lost the way a thing is lost that someone might find. It is lost the way a word is lost that was spoken into a wind sixty-six million years ago. We have the bed it dissolved into. We have, in that bed, the fixed nitrogen and the split atom's daughters and the glass that should be formless, the chemistry of the world she was keeping the record *of*—we have the death she documented, written in the only ink that lasts, which is the ink she did not choose and could not read. But the documenting, the keeping, the hand laid against the loss in long love and longer attention—that is gone, wholly, into the same centimeter that kept the poison. The marsh took her record back. She had always said the record was the marsh's. She was righter than she meant.

The logbook was in Asha's bag, the second one, the one with the margin name in it she had told herself to erase and would not now ever erase, and she carried it into the building where the hearing had ended and she did not yet know how, and she set it on the table in the small room they had given her to wait in, and she did not open it, because she had decided on the drive in that the next hand to open it would not be hers.

Téo was already there. He had not been allowed in the room itself—he was a graduate student with a fellowship application due in a month and a supervisor who had spent her credibility like water, and the kindest thing she had ever done for him she did now, which was to have kept his name off the worst of it, so that he could still, if he chose, walk out of this and be a person with a future and not a footnote to a thing the field laughed at. He stood by the window with the winter light behind him, and he did not ask how it had gone, because her face was doing the thing she never let it do and he had learned, over a hard year, when not to ask whether.

"I want you to have this," she said, and put her flat hand on the cover of the book, the way you put a hand on a thing you are keeping. "Not the data. The data's published, or rejected, or both; the data can take care of itself; the data was never the thing I was afraid of losing." She slid it an inch toward him across the table and stopped, because she found she could not yet take her hand off it. "This is the other record. The one I kept by hand because I didn't trust myself to a file that could be edited without leaving a mark. It's got the runs in it, the forty-one runs from the old core ten years ago, the ones I let a senior man talk me out of, and the eleven runs from this core that I didn't let anyone talk me out of, and what it cost me to tell the difference. And it's got the names in it." She heard her own voice do something and held it. "I named them, Téo. The dead. I gave a drowned maker a name out of the metal in the clay and I gave the four small ones beside him a number that I have decided is not a number, and I wrote, under it, *there were four, he was not alone when it came.* I know it isn't science. I've stopped being able to tell whether that's the worst thing in the book or the only true thing in it."

Téo looked at the book under her hand and did not reach for it yet, because he had been taught by her to assume that the thing he most wanted was the thing most likely to be lying, and he was applying it now to the wanting itself.

"Why me," he said. "Why not the archive. Why not—I don't know—a vault, a server, ten servers, why not put it somewhere it can't be lost."

"Because that's not what a record is for." She took her hand off the cover. It was harder than she had thought it would be. "I used to think it was. I thought you kept the record so that later someone could prove the thing. Version control on the truth. And then I spent a year learning that the thing can't be proven, that it was never going to be provable, that the whole shape of it is a real case that's permanently deniable, and I had to figure out what a record is *for* when it can't ever close the case it's a record of. And the answer turns out to be embarrassing and I'm going to say it to you anyway because you're the one I'm handing it to." She pushed it the last inch. "It's for the next hand. That's all. It's not for the archive, it's not for the proof, it's not for after. It's for the one person willing to open it and let it change what they do next. A record that no living hand opens is just a tombstone. The only thing that makes it a letter is you taking it."

He picked it up then. He held it the way she had hoped he would, not like a deliverable, like a thing that was warm, and she watched him do it and let her throat—she did not have a throat that could do anything, she had a face, and she let her face do, in front of him, where she never let it, the thing it had been holding through the whole of the room she had just lost in.

"You're not erasing the names," he said. It was not a question.

"No."

"Even though it isn't science."

"*Because* it isn't science," she said. "Science is the part that's published and can take care of itself. This is the other part. This is the part that's only true if somebody keeps it. I kept it. Now you keep it. That's the whole of it. That's the entire mechanism. There isn't a more durable version. There's just whether the next hand opens it in time."

◆

Here is the thing we have to say plainly, once, on this page, and then never again, because it is the load-bearing fact of the whole account and we have spent a book and sixty-six million years trying to earn the right to say it and we do not think we are going to do better than plainly.

The two records are the same record. They are not alike. They are not a rhyme, though we have built the whole telling so that they rhyme, and you have heard it, and we will not insult you by pointing at the place in the rock where the figure repeats. They are the same act performed twice by the same kind of creature on opposite sides of a centimeter of clay, the act of laying a hand against a loss you cannot stop and keeping faith with it in a medium, because the keeping is the fidelity and the fidelity does not require an after to be worth doing. Liss said it without the word. Iren reached the end of the sentence on the shoal and found the word and it was *fidelity*. Asha found the same word by a different road and called it the next hand. It is one act. It has been one act the whole time.

And one of the two records was kept, and one was lost, and we want you to sit with the fact that the difference between them—the entire difference, the whole of why we have Asha's logbook and not Liss's keeping-book—is not a difference in the records. It is not that Asha kept hers better, or loved harder, or wrote in a more durable hand. Liss wrote in the only medium her world gave her and it dissolved into mud, and Asha wrote in the only medium hers gave her and it sits in a bag on a table in a building in winter, and the difference between dissolution and survival is sixty-six million years and a flood-surge and the blind indifference of a planet that recycles its own crust and keeps no promises about which of its children's records it will hold. The lost record was not worse. It was earlier. That is all. It was earlier, and it had no one downstream, and the rule the universe seems willing to enforce is that the earlier you are the less of you is kept, and Liss was the earliest keeper there has ever been, the first of the first, and so almost nothing of her is kept, and what is kept of her we made up, with love, and confessed.

You hold both. That is the part we cannot get past. Liss kept her record and lost it and never knew it was lost, because there was no after in which she could know; Asha kept her record and handed it to Téo and does not know whether the handing will matter, because the after has not happened yet. Neither woman has ever held both records at once. Neither one ever can. Liss is in the bed. Asha is in the room. The only place where the keeping-book that dissolved and the logbook that survived sit open on the same table, where the count of the day in a hand that is gone and the *there were four* in a hand that is not gone yet can be read by one mind in one act of attention—the only place that has ever existed is the one you are standing in right now, which is yourself, which is the reading, which is the whole of what we have been trying to give you and the whole of what we are afraid we cannot make you keep.

◆

Liss did not write the crier down, the last of its stretch, fishing the empty shoal, because the crier was hers, the one true thing she kept that was only her own and not the marsh's. We have no record of the crier. There cannot be a record of the crier. It is the most certain loss in the whole account, more certain than the keeping-book, because she chose for it not to exist, chose it out of the same fidelity that made her keep everything else, the dignity of letting one loved thing go unkept. We know about it only because we invented her, and gave her the choice, and the choice is invention all the way down. We tell you about the unrecorded crier so that you understand the size of what is gone: not only the records that dissolved, but every record that was, in love, never made. The marsh is full of them. The bed is full of them. Most of what was ever kept by anyone was kept the way Liss kept the crier, in one mind, for the duration of one watching, and went out of the world when the watcher did, unwritten, on purpose, as a mercy or a privacy or a refusal to make a grief into a procedure. That is the ordinary fate of the kept. Survival is the freak.

Asha will not publish the names either. The names are the crier. They are the part of her record that she keeps the way Liss kept the last fisher of the empty shoal—the unscientific part, the part that is only hers and the dead's, the part she has now put into the one other living hand willing to hold it and otherwise will let go out of the world when she does. The logbook survived the surge of her own life, the divorce and the rejected paper and the room she lost in this afternoon. It will not survive everything. Nothing does. She knows this. It is why she handed it on. A record handed on is the only kind that has a chance, and it is not a good chance, and she took it anyway, because taking the unprovable chance is the thing she finally learned to do, the thing her whole discipline could never certify, the leap past the evidence that turns out, in the only court that never sits, to have been right.

◆

So we will leave the two of them at the rail and the table, which are the same place.

Liss is at the seaward rail in the last of the river-light with the keeping-book open to the count of the day, and she does not know the surge is coming, and she will never know it took the book, and the book is going to become the centimeter of clay that a woman not yet born by sixty-six million years will spend eleven years standing on without knowing whose home it was. Asha is at the table in the winter building with the logbook just gone out of her hand into Téo's, and she does not know how the room came out, and neither do we, and we have sworn not to tell you even the part we could guess, because the not-knowing is the whole of the warning and we will not steal it to make the evening easier.

Between them is a centimeter of clay. On one side of it a maker's bondmate keeps a record that the earth will dissolve into the proof of its own subject and never give back. On the other side of it a maker of a different kind keeps a record that one more living hand has just agreed to carry a little further into a future no one can see. The records are the same record. One is lost and one is kept and the difference is not in the records.

It is in the reader.

We have brought you as far as we can bring you. There is one more thing to say, and it is not ours to say; it belongs to the line in the rock, which has been waiting the whole time, patient in the way that only the truly dead and the not-yet-acted are patient, for someone to decide whether it is a tombstone or a letter.

You are the someone. You always were. There was never anyone else; that is what the two records are for; that is what they were always for.

Turn the page. It is the last of it. It is addressed to you.
