# Too Old

The piece of it broke off in his glove and that was how he knew, before any of the chemistry, that the thing was not a rock.

He had gone back. Of course he had gone back; the not-going-back had been the lie, and a man could only hold a lie like that for so long with the file sitting on the good screen every shift, dark and corrugated and patient. So he had spent delta-v he was carrying on worse credit than before, three more passes and then a rendezvous, matched velocities with the bad-orbit object over a day and a half of fine burns, and now the *Margin* hung a hundred meters off it and Roan was out on the tether in the cold with a sample bag and the headlamp throwing a circle on a surface that drank the light the way the radar had said it would.

He had meant to chip a sample. You chipped a sample; that was the procedure; you took a chip and a context photo and you logged the geometry and you went back inside and you did the assay and then, only then, you knew what you had. He had set the chisel against a seam—a seam, on a rock, which was the first wrongness his hands reported before his head caught up—and tapped it, and a flake the size of his palm had come away clean along the seam, clean the way nothing in nature comes away, clean the way a thing comes apart along the line where it was once joined.

He held it in the lamp. It was not heavy. It was much too light for metal and far too dark for ice and it had, on the broken face, a layering—thin bright laminae alternating with a matrix that drank the beam—that no sediment laid down and no melt froze into. He turned it. The light moved across the laminae and where it moved it found, in one of the bright bands, the unmistakable straight edge of something that had been drawn straight on purpose, a ruled line, a manufacture.

Out here you did not say things out loud. There was no one to say them to and the recorder caught everything and you learned to keep your mouth shut until you knew. Roan kept his mouth shut. He put the flake in the bag and the bag in the thigh pocket and he went back along the tether hand over hand with his pulse loud in the suit and the second couch waiting empty inside for no one to be told.

◆

He ran the assay that night and the assay was the part he understood, which was a small mercy, because the rest of it he did not understand at all.

The *Margin* had a mass spectrometer because a survey ship had to have one; you could not sell a claim on radar and good intentions. It was a prospector's instrument, built to tell nickel from iron from olivine, to put a number on a body's worth so the consortium back at the rock-broker desk knew what they were buying. It was not built for this and Roan made it do this anyway, the way he made the heater loop run on docking grease, by knowing its lies better than the manufacturer had. He massed a fragment of the flake. He ran it. He ran it again with a fresh cut because the first numbers were not numbers a prospector ever saw.

The metals first, because the metals were what the machine was for. The alloy—and it was an alloy, a made mixing, not an ore—was wrong in the homely, structural way before it got wrong in the impossible way. It was light and it was strong and the proportions were the proportions of someone solving a problem Roan knew the shape of: the same problem he solved every time he chose a member for a stressed joint, mass against strength against the cold. Aluminum and titanium and the rest of it, laid up the way you laid up a thing meant to hold pressure and spin and not weigh more than it had to. He had read the renderings' specs. He knew, dimly and with growing nausea, what an engineer reached for when the engineer was building something to turn in the dark and hold a world's worth of air inside it.

Then the isotopes, and the isotopes were where the floor went out.

He did not have Asha Rendel's vocabulary; he would not have one for months, and even then it would be borrowed. But he had a prospector's nose for a number that should not exist, and the spectrometer kept handing him, in the trace stuff layered under the alloy, ratios that no ore carried and no ice and no chunk of a shattered parent body. There were daughters in it with no natural parents—heavy lines that on Earth came out of a reactor or a bomb and out of nothing else, and their proportions were not the proportions of anything fresh. They were skewed, every one of them, in the same direction, the direction of a great deal of decay, of patient turning-into in the dark, of clocks that had been running a very long time. He did not have the half-lives memorized. He looked them up in the prospector's reference, which carried them for dating purposes, for telling a young rock from an old one, and he did the arithmetic on the back of a survey log with a stub of pencil because he did not trust the screen to not be lying to him in some way he hadn't caught yet.

The arithmetic said tens of millions of years.

He erased it. He did the arithmetic again, slower, with the other lines, the ones that decayed on a different schedule, because the beauty of having more than one clock was that they had to agree or you'd made a mistake, and the cross-check was the thing that turned a guess into a date. They agreed. Two clocks running on different chemistry agreed with each other, and what they agreed on was a number with seven zeros after the front of it, a number that belonged to the Boundary clay and the dinosaurs and the rocks he photographed, not to a thing that had been made, with a ruled line and a stressed joint and an alloy chosen to hold air.

Made things were not old. That was the whole order of the universe as Roan had lived in it. Old things were rock. Made things were new, were ours, were the renderings and the survey hulls and the wrong grease, the thin leading edge of a frontier that had started about the time his grandfather was born. The two facts the numbers handed him—that it was made, and that it was old—did not sit in the same universe, and he sat there holding both of them, and the cabin was very quiet, and the heater loop ticked along on the bad grease, holding, for now.

◆

The first thing he thought about was what it was worth, and he was not proud of that, and he forgave himself for it the way you forgive a reflex, because a reflex is just the body telling you who it has had to be.

A made thing was salvage. Salvage had rates. A made thing that was *not human*—because that was the floor that had gone out, that was the thing under the number, that a thing this old could not be human, that the frontier his grandfather had watched begin had begun fifty million years too late to have made the flake in the bag—a made thing that was not human was salvage of a kind that did not have rates yet, which meant the rates were whatever the first man to file it could make them. He let himself live in that for about a minute. The debt gone. The variance fees, the premium for flying Devi's couch empty, the note Mara worried about over fourteen minutes of lag—gone, all of it, on a single filing of a single claim on a single fragment of something that no one in the history of the species had ever owned. He was a working spacer and he had found the only thing in the dark worth more than the gravel, and the part of him that did the arithmetic for the broker desk ran the arithmetic and came up with a figure that had its own zeros.

And then the cold thing started to arrive, the way the alarm had arrived in the empty cabin, a sensor being right about something it could not understand.

He had been thinking about it as a find. A salvor's word: you found wreck, you found tonnage, you found a derelict and you ran the registry and if no one owned it you owned it. *Wreck.* He turned the word over and it turned wrong in his hand the way the flake had turned wrong in the lamp. Because wreck meant a ship, and a ship meant a builder, and the builder of this one had laid up an alloy against the cold and the spin fifty million years before there was a hand on Earth that could hold a chisel. He thought about the renderings Mara loved, the green cylinders, the rivers curving up the walls of the world, the future everyone agreed was coming, the next place, the redundancy, the eggs in more than one basket. Somebody has to find them the rocks to make the cans out of, Mara had said. It could be us. The boring part.

He looked at the radar shadow he still had up in the corner of the screen, the long axis that was too long, the corrugation his processing had kept trying and failing to call noise. He had been looking at it for weeks as an outlier, a flag, a thing that didn't sit right. He looked at it now as a piece of a wall that had once curved up and over someone's head, and the periodicity that wasn't noise was the regular structure of a thing built to turn, and the not-human builder who had chosen the alloy in the bag had built the exact thing the renderings promised, the next place, the spinning can with the air inside, and had built it so long ago that the warmth of the building was, like the number said, fifty, sixty million years gone.

This is not ours, he thought. And it is not new.

He sat with that. He was good at sitting in the dark with a thing he didn't like; it was most of the job, the part Devi had hated and he had not, the waiting for a number to come in. Both facts true. Neither one able to be true if the other was. A made thing fifty million years old in an orbit that didn't close on a natural story, drinking the light, keeping the ghost of its shape, and somewhere under the wreck-and-salvage arithmetic the floor kept not being there, and what came up through the gap where the floor had been was not the figure with the zeros. It was something with no number on it at all.

◆

He almost did not write it up, and the almost was Devi, and that was the worst of it.

She was in the cabin the way she got in the cabin, sideways, uninvited, more certain than he was. *File it, Iyer.* He could hear it flat, in the Lagos-by-way-of-orbit drawl. *You don't sit on a thing that's made and old. That's not a claim. You know that's not a claim.* He had argued the money at her once, on the gravel, and she had won, because she always won the ones about other people's safety. He started to argue the money at her now and it died in his mouth, because the money was the small thing, the money was the reflex he'd already forgiven, and the thing he was actually weighing it against did not have a name he could say to her, because she wasn't there to say it to. That was the shape the grief took. Not the big absence. The small one: that the one find of his life, the only thing out here that wasn't gravel, the thing that broke the floor of the world, he had no one in the cockpit to turn to and say *Dev. Dev, look at this. Tell me I've got the arithmetic wrong.*

The second couch was behind him. He did not turn around to it. You learned not to.

He could file it as salvage and keep his mouth shut about the age. That was the path that kept the zeros. A made thing, anomalous body, possible derelict, composition under analysis—true, all of it, and no one would press a working pilot on the isotopes of a salvage filing, because no one ran isotopes on salvage, the same way no one ran transuranics on a Boundary sample, because why would you. He could keep the dates in his own log, in the small private place, and let the claim be a claim. The arithmetic for that came out clean.

He filed the other thing instead, and he did it the way you take a chip and a context photo, by the procedure, because the procedure was the only thing standing between a man and the version of himself that sat on a return that drinks light.

He wrote the assay up straight. The alloy. The structure, the ruled line, the seam that came apart clean. The isotope ratios, every line he'd measured, the ones he could name and the heavy ones he couldn't, the skew of the daughters all in the one direction. He wrote the cross-check: two clocks, different chemistry, in agreement. He wrote the number with its seven zeros and he did not soften it and he did not editorialize it, because the moment you editorialized an extraordinary return was the moment they got to stop reading you. He attached the radar shadow with the corrugation and he wrote, in the notes field, in the plainest words he had, *Object is manufactured. Object is old. I cannot reconcile these and am not going to try. Putting it up so someone with better instruments can.* He flagged it to the survey net, the catalog cross-check that had handed him the bad orbit in the first place, the standing trade in flagged outliers that the whole shoestring frontier read because everybody wrote everything down so the next person didn't learn it the hard way.

He posted it to the open board, not the private channel. That was the part that cost him the zeros and he did it anyway. On the open board a salvor's claim was a salvor's to lose; the moment the isotopes were public, the rock stopped being his. He sat with his thumb over the key the way he imagined a careful person sat over a key, and then he pressed it, and the assay went out into the lag toward the inner system at the speed of light, fourteen minutes to Mara and the broker desk and then onward, indexed, searchable, into the standing pool of numbers that people who were not pilots dredged for reasons of their own.

He did not know—he had no way to know, sitting in the cold cabin with the wrong grease holding the loop together—that an isotope ratio is a thing a particular kind of scientist runs a standing alert on. That somewhere fourteen light-minutes and then some down the gradient there were geochemists who kept a net out for exactly the heavy lines he'd measured, the daughters with no natural parents, because those lines mattered to questions that had nothing to do with salvage. That one of them would see a Belt prospector's filing surface in a feed it had no business surfacing in, and would read the skew of the daughters all in the one direction, and would feel the specific cold of a number behaving the way it was not allowed to behave, and would forward it—*you need to see this, this is not contamination, this is not a smear, look at the ratio*—to a paleo-geochemist who had spent a decade being told by everyone whose advice was worth having that the thing she was reading in a centimeter of clay could not be there.

The threads did not touch. They leaned. Roan's filing went into the pool and the pool was read and somewhere in the reading the two halves of a sentence that had been written sixty-six million years apart drifted a little closer to lying in the same line, and no one yet held both ends of it, and the only place they would ever meet was not in the Belt and not in the clay.

◆

"Found something that isn't gravel," he told the recorder, that evening, for Mara, fourteen minutes away.

He had practiced not saying more. The lag did his arguing for him; whatever she sent back would be a quarter hour stale, and he would not have to watch her face do the thing faces did. He left it at that for a moment, and the cabin ticked, and the radar shadow sat in the corner of the screen with its corrugation that he no longer needed his processing to fail at calling noise, because he knew now what it was the ghost of.

"It's old, Mara," he said. "I ran the dates twice on two clocks and they agree and I don't like what they agree on. I put the assay up on the open board. The whole thing, the isotopes, not the private channel. I know what that costs. Don't—" He stopped. He had been going to say *don't tell Okonkwo*, the reflex, the claim, the zeros. He let it go. "Tell whoever," he said. "It's not ours to sit on. Somebody built it. It's not us, and it's not new, and those don't go together, and they're both true, and I checked."

He looked at the second couch, finally, the way you look at a thing once and then put it away.

"Devi'd have made me file it," he said, to the recorder, to the lag, to no one in the couch. "She was right. She was always right about the ones about other people."

He sent it. He sat in the webbing in the cold with the loop holding on the wrong grease, and outside the hull the made thing turned, slow, keeping the ghost of its shape, fifty million years past the warmth of whatever hand had laid the alloy up against the dark, and the thing Roan felt was not the figure with the zeros and was not discovery. It was the cold coming in. Not the cabin's cold; that he could fix, in his underwear, by headlamp, the way he fixed everything. The other cold. The understanding edging in at the rate the body let it, no faster, because the body had opinions about this kind of thing now—the slow arrival of a man who has finally let a true number all the way in, and knows, the way you know an alarm, that the number is not about a rock, and is not about a claim, and is going to cost him more than everything he came out here to keep.
