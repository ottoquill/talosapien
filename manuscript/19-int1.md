# What We Invented

We told you we would mark the seams, so here is the first of them, while the mortar is still wet.

The eggs are real. We want to start with what is real, because we are about to spend a page taking things away from you and we do not want you to think there is nothing left. There were nests. We have the nests — not his, we will never have his, but nests of his kind and his time, clutches of four and five and six in the fossil crèches, arranged with a care that is not the care of a reptile dropping eggs in sand and walking away. We have the brooding postures, the shared incubation, the long parental tenure read off the bone-growth of the young. The communal raising is not a guess. A people who tend their future in common is not a guess. So when we put a man at the edge of a clutch with the side of one finger along a shell, the man is invented and the gesture is not, and we would rather you held those two facts in separate hands than let us blur them for the comfort of the scene.

The grey at the throat is real, or real enough. We have the feathers, now, from the deep beds — we have known for a long time that they were feathered, the whole clamorous public argument about that is decades settled — and we can read, in the microstructure, the cells that in living birds carry color and in some living birds change it. We do not know that they flushed grey with grief. We know they had the equipment to flush, and we know that a body that can broadcast its state on its skin will, over evolutionary time, have that broadcast wired into its social life, the way a face that can blush ends up unable to lie well. The grey is an inference standing on a fact, and we have told you it is standing, and we will not pretend it is the ground.

His name is not real. We need to say this plainly, because it is the seam we are most tempted to plaster over, since the whole of what follows depends on your forgetting it. We call him Iren. We took the name from the metal — from the element in the clay that first told us a fire had burned here that the Earth does not light on its own. It is a coroner's name, a name from the cause of death, and we gave it to him the way you write *Baby Boy Doe* on a stone, and then could not bear it, and gave the stone a better name so that we would have something to say at it that was not a number. There is no recovering his name. There was a sound he answered to, made in a register we are not sure we could fully hear, and it is gone past any technology we will ever have, gone the way the heat of that morning is gone, dispersed into the long cold averaging of deep time until not one photon of it can be called back. We did not find Iren. We made him, out of a true death and a true world and the unbearable blank where a person was, and we made him because the alternative was to write a paper about anomalous transuranics in the Maastrichtian boundary clay and go home, and we have read that paper, we have written that paper, and no one went home different.

You may ask — the careful among you are already asking — what gives us the right.

It is the same right by which you grieve a stranger in a photograph. You do not know that the woman in the old picture was kind; you grant it, because the cost of granting it is nothing and the cost of withholding it is a coarsening of yourself you cannot afford. We are granting Iren an inside. We are granting all of them an inside, the engineer and his mate and his friend and the old one who lost the argument, and we are doing it on a single load-bearing assumption that we cannot prove and will defend with our lives, which is this: that a thing which fixed nitrogen from the air, and split the atom, and crossed to the belt of stones and built turning worlds there, and then argued about whether to trust a mind it had made to save it — that such a thing had an inside, of the kind we have, because we have done every one of those things and we have an inside, and we are not willing, anymore, to be the kind of people who assume we are the only ones the lights were ever on for.

That assumption is the most dangerous one in this book. It is the assumption that makes the warning a warning. If they were not like us, then their fall is a curiosity and our path is our own. If they were like us — if the inside we are inventing for Iren is not a sentimental projection but a correct identification of a fellow — then we are not reading a history. We are reading a forecast, written in a hand identical to our own, by people who could not get themselves to believe it either, about a danger they were at that moment building, and the only difference between their library and ours is that theirs is a centimeter of clay and ours is still open on the table.

We have invented a man so that you would feel the forecast as a death. We confess it. We would do it again. We are going to keep doing it, all the way to the last day, and there will come a page where we follow him as far as the evidence allows and then stop, abruptly, at the edge of the flood-surge, and refuse to tell you his last thought — and when we stop, you will understand that the stopping is the one moment in all of this where we are not inventing, where we are standing at the true edge of the true blank and keeping faith with it by saying nothing.

That page is a long way off yet. For now the weather is fine, and the answer sits unread in the cool of the hall, and a woman sixty-six million years downstream has just decided to run an impossible number a twelfth time.

Both of them think they have more time than they have. So do you. So do we.
