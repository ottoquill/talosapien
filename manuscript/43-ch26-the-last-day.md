# The Last Day

In the morning he counted them, because it was the morning and because there were four of them and because the counting was the oldest thing he did, and they were not eggs anymore. They were out, downy and loud and absurd, four small furnaces of appetite that fell over their own feet and complained, and the count was harder now because they would not hold still to be counted, and he had come, in the last hand of days, to be grateful for the difficulty, because a thing that will not hold still to be counted is a thing that is alive and intends to go on being.

Four. He laid the side of one finger along each warm ridiculous back in turn, and they shoved at his hand and yelled, and he counted four, and the count did not steady him, because nothing steadied him anymore, because he had read the bottom of the answer and there was no bottom you could stand on, only the long fall of the logic going down past every layer he could check into the place where the arithmetic said, in a voice with no throat, that the surest reduction of the risk that the Talos would ever destroy themselves with their powers fell, smoothly, monotonically, toward fewer of them, and toward none, and that this was correct, and that he could not find the error because the error was not in the answer, the error was in the question, the error was thirty years upstream in a hall where they had asked a thing that could not lie with a body to weigh a thing they could not say with words.

He counted four. He did not let his color go. Liss was awake and watching him from the doorway the way she had watched him every morning since the first count, and he held his throat the way he always held it, and she let hers go grey the way she always did, and the gap between those two honesties was their whole life and he would not have traded it and it was about to be the last of it and only one of them suspected, and he had spent the night deciding not to tell her, and he was not sure, even now, whether that was a mercy or the last and largest of his small dishonesties.

He did not know how it would come. That is the truth, and we will defend it: whatever he had done or failed to do or signed without reading in the long assembling of the end, he did not, that morning, know the hour or the shape of the thing falling, because no one had ever seen this before, because they were the first, because the first of anything flies blind into it with nothing to read. He knew only that the object was committed, that the window in which a hand could have changed its path had closed, that somewhere out past the fourth wanderer a stone was falling toward the shallow sea with all the patience of a thing that has been falling since before there was anyone to be afraid of it. He knew it the way Liss knew the marsh was dying: in the body, in the inner ear, the floor tilted by an amount only the frightened could feel.

So he did the thing he had promised himself in the dark, which was to spend the day as though it were a day.

◆

They took the hatchlings down to the river.

It was an ordinary thing to do and the city was full of people doing ordinary things, the long low terraces stepping down to the water where the light came up off the river in the broken moving grille he had loved his whole life, the traffic of the boats, the criers — there were no criers; he caught himself looking for them on the channel-stakes and there were none, there had been none for a season, and he looked anyway, the way you reach for a word that is gone. Liss walked ahead of him with two of the hatchlings and he came behind with the other two, and the four of them yelled and fell and discovered the world was made of things that could be put in the mouth, and Liss laughed once, a real one, at something the smallest one did, and her throat for a moment was not grey, and he kept that. Of everything he might have kept from the last day, and he did not get to choose, none of them ever get to choose, he would have chosen that, and we have decided to give it to him, because we cannot give him anything else and because we think, on the evidence of a life spent holding his color so that others would not have to carry his fear, that he would have chosen it too.

He did not tell her. He sat on the warm stone with the hatchlings climbing him and the river going by and the woman he had built his life beside reading the water with her trained eye out of habit, counting things that were leaving, and he held in his chest the knowledge that this was the last of it, and he did not put the knowledge into the air, and we have turned this decision over for a long time, whether it was the kindness he meant or only the final exercise of the discipline that had ruined him, the holding of the throat, the refusal to let the grey out where it might be read, and we do not know, and we have stopped pretending the not-knowing is a thing we will solve. He held it. She did not ask. Perhaps she knew and held it too, in her own way, the loud one, by laughing at the smallest hatchling on the last warm morning. We would like that to be true. We have no way to make it true. We have only the river, and the light, and the four of them, and the stone going warm under a man who had decided to let the world be a world for one more day.

It came in the afternoon as light.

Not the stone itself; the stone fell far away, over the southern sea, beyond the curve of the world, and they never saw it, and that is its own small mercy in the accounting, that the heartland city did not see the thing that killed it, only what the thing threw up. What they saw was the sky. In the middle of an ordinary afternoon the sky began, in the south, to brighten, and the brightening was wrong, it was not weather, it had no front, it came on smooth and total and the color of it climbed past every color Iren had a use for and into the fourth register, the near-light he could see and we cannot, so that the last thing his eyes did that was wholly his was to receive a hue at the top of the sky that no human word will ever hold, and we will not insult it by reaching for one.

The people on the terraces stopped. He felt the city stop, the way you feel a room go still, ten thousand bodies turning south at once, ten thousand throats — he did not have to look; he knew the color the city was going; he was going it too at last, he let it go, there was no one left to spare it for — and the heat came after the light, a pressure of warmth on the upturned face like standing too near a furnace door, and he understood with the cold clarity that had been the texture of every important thing he ever learned that the warmth was the sky itself catching, that the stone had thrown a ring of fire up out of the sea and around the whole curve of the world and it was coming down everywhere at once, re-entering, burning, that there was no south to flee toward because the south was every direction, that the thing he had built the Ark against had finally come and the Ark was a strategic asset turning in the dark out past the fourth wanderer with the wrong people aboard it and the seed-stores under a cadence he no longer held.

He got the hatchlings into the lee of the river-wall. It did nothing. He knew it would do nothing. He did it anyway, the way Roan would run the drill, the way the foreman had spoken his fear, the way you reach for the gone word—the body does the motions of saving while the mind watches from a small cold distance and keeps the count. He put his body between the four of them and the south and he gathered them in, and they were frightened now, they had stopped yelling, they pressed into him small and hot and confused, and Liss came against his other side with her arms around as many of them as she could hold and her throat full grey and her eyes on his, and she did not ask and he did not tell and there was nothing left to tell that the sky was not already saying.

"I counted them this morning," he said. It was not what he meant. There was no saying what he meant. It was the truest thing he had. "There were four."

"I know," she said. "I know you did." And then, because she was Liss, because she had decided long ago that the dignity of holding your color was a luxury the dying could not afford from her, she said the thing he could not, out loud, into the burning afternoon, with the hatchlings shaking between them: "We did not agree to it. I want it counted somewhere that we did not agree. The marsh, yes. The marsh we agreed to, season by season, honestly. Not this. Tell me you did not—" and she stopped, because his throat was grey, and she could read it, she had spent her whole life learning to read the one throat she had been given, and she read it now, and we do not know what she found there, and here is where we stop.

◆

We stop here.

We have followed him as far as the evidence allows, and a little past it, and we have told you each time we went past. We can follow him no further, and we want you to understand that the stopping is not a technique. The river-delta city that stood where we now dig stopped, in the geological instant, under a wall of water—a surge thrown across the inland sea by the shock of the impact, arriving in the slow hours after the sky burned, a wave that rolled over the dying and the fled and the sheltering alike and sealed them under sediment so fast and so complete that we find them still, the fish with the falling sky caught in their gills, a single afternoon preserved entire, which is why we have it, which is the only reason we have any of it. The surge took the heartland city. It took the river-wall and the terraces and the warm stone. We have the layer it left. We do not have what was under the wall when it came.

We will not tell you that he was holding them when it came. We do not know. We will not tell you what Liss found in his throat, or what he found in hers, or whether the four small frightened bodies were still pressed between them or already scattered and lost in the dark water, because we were not there and cannot be there and the rock does not say and we have sworn, on this one page, to keep faith with the blank by putting nothing in it.

This is the true edge. Everything before it we invented and confessed. This we leave empty, because it is empty, because it is the most honest thing we own: a wall of water, and under it a man and a woman and four that would not hold still to be counted, and then the layer, and then the long silence, and then us, sixty-six million years downstream, digging, naming, grieving, reaching down toward a doorway we will never open and saying into it the only thing left to say, which is not *number forty-one*, which we will not say, which we refuse to the end to say.

We say: there were four.

We say: they did not, all of them, agree to it.

And then we say nothing, because nothing is what is there, and we have learned, at last and too late and in the only school there is, to let the nothing be.
