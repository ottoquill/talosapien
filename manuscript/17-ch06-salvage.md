# Salvage

The smoke alarm went off in the empty cabin at oh-three-forty, which was how Roan learned the heater pump on the number-two radiator loop had finally died, because the alarm was the only thing on the *Margin* that still worked the way it was built to.

There was no smoke. There was no fire. There was a pump seizing in a way that warmed a sensor it shouldn't have, and the alarm did the one job it had, screaming into a cabin with one man in it and a co-pilot's couch that had been empty for two years, and Roan lay in the webbing for a full three seconds before he moved, listening to it the way you listen to a thing that is right about something it doesn't understand, and then he killed it and went to fix the pump.

He fixed the pump. That was the job. Not the survey, not the claim, not the long bet on the orbit he'd been working for six weeks—the job, the actual job, the one that paid in continuing to breathe, was that when something on a forty-year-old survey hull broke at oh-three-forty you got up and fixed it, in your underwear, in micro-g, by the light of a headlamp, with the cold coming through the deck plating into your bare feet, because the alternative was to be a frozen man in a drifting can that someone would eventually have to come out and salvage, and the salvage rates on a dead pilot were terrible.

The pump was a sealed unit. It was not supposed to be field-serviceable. Roan had serviced it in the field four times, because the *Margin* was held together by him doing things to it that the manufacturer would have voided the warranty over, if the manufacturer still existed, which it didn't. He pulled the dead unit, warmed the bearing with his hands and a heat strip until it turned, packed it with the wrong grease because it was the grease he had, and put it back. The whole loop came up to temperature over the next twenty minutes while he watched the gauge and did not go back to sleep.

You did not go back to sleep after the alarm. He'd learned that. The body didn't let you. The body had its own opinions about alarms now.

◆

Devi would have laughed at the grease.

That was the shape the grief took, two years on—not the big absences, the ones he'd braced for and gotten through, but the small ones that ambushed him sideways. He'd packed a sealed pump with bearing grease meant for a docking collar and somewhere in him a voice that was not quite a voice said *that's not going to hold, Iyer, and you know it's not going to hold, and you're going to do it anyway, and write it up so it sounds deliberate,* in Devi's flat Lagos-by-way-of-orbit drawl, and the missing of her was so specific and so physical that he had to stop with one hand on the cold curve of the bulkhead and breathe through it.

They'd flown six years together. She had been better than him at everything except the part where you sat still in the dark and waited for a number to come in, which was most of the job, and which she'd hated, and which was why she'd gone outside on the day the seal on the forward lock failed—not on his watch, not on anyone's watch, on no one's watch, a fatigue crack in a component rated for ten times the cycles it had seen, a thing that simply was not supposed to happen and had—and the cabin pressure had gone in the time it took the alarm to finish its first cry, and Devi had been on the wrong side of it.

Decompression is fast. That was the one mercy and Roan held onto it the way you hold a coin in a pocket, worrying it smooth. It is faster than understanding. The drills they made you run, the muscle-memory cascade—mask, hatch, valve, brace—were all built around the lie that you would have time, and the truth the drills were designed to hide was that if it was the kind of failure that killed you, it killed you before the first step, and if it wasn't, the drill was just choreography for your hands while the automatics did the actual saving. He'd run the drill. He ran it now, alone, twice a month, mask hatch valve brace, talking himself through it in the empty cabin, and he could not have said whether he ran it because it might save him or because Devi had run it beside him a thousand times and the motions were a place she still was.

He flew solo now. Outfits like his were supposed to crew two—it was in the safety filings, it was the whole point of the second couch—and Roan had filed the variance and paid the higher premium and flown alone for two years, because the math of hiring a stranger to sit in Devi's couch had never once come out in favor of doing it. The debt service alone. A second mouth, a second air ration, a second cut of a claim that wasn't a claim yet. And under the math, which was real, the other thing, which he did not look at directly: that he would have to watch a stranger not be her, every shift, in the place where she had been.

So: solo. The *Margin*, and the dark, and the count.

◆

The dark out here was not empty, which was the thing the planetside people never got.

He talked to one of them most evenings—Mara, dispatch-and-everything-else for the little three-ship outfit he flew under, fourteen light-minutes away on a good geometry, which meant their conversations were not conversations so much as two people leaving long careful messages in each other's silence. You learned to talk in complete paragraphs. You learned to not ask questions, because a question and its answer were half an hour apart and by the time you got the answer you'd forgotten the question's weather. So you stated things. You sent her the day in declaratives and she sent hers back, and somewhere in the lag the two of you built a kind of intimacy out of patience that Roan had come to prize more than he'd expected to.

"Pump's back up," he told her, that morning, into the recorder, with the gauge holding steady behind him. "Number-two loop. Don't tell the insurer how. Survey's on schedule, more or less. I'll have the sweep of the new sector done inside the week and then we can talk about whether any of these rocks are worth a filing or whether I've spent six weeks photographing gravel." He thought about it. "It's gravel, Mara. I'm going to save us both the lag and tell you now. It's very high-resolution gravel."

Her reply came in over breakfast, which was a pouch of something the label optimistically called eggs.

"Gravel's fine," Mara said. "Gravel pays the note if there's enough of it and it's the right gravel. Listen—" and here her voice did the thing it did when she was about to be hopeful at him, which he'd learned to brace for, "—Okonkwo was at the consortium thing, the orbital-development panel, and she says the gyrealm money is finally real. Not next-decade real. This-decade real. They're chartering survey for habitat feedstock now, not just metals. Which means claims like ours, the boring nickel-iron stuff, the volatiles—that's the stuff you build a wheel out of. We could be sitting on the start of something. Everybody's going to want to live up here in twenty years, Roan, in the big spinning cans, and somebody has to find them the rocks to make the cans out of." A pause, on her end, fourteen minutes stale by the time it reached him. "It could be us. The boring part. The part nobody writes the articles about."

Roan ate his eggs and thought about the gyrealms.

He'd seen the renderings, same as everyone—the great spun cylinders, green inside, rivers that curved up the walls of the world and over your head, whole landscapes wrapped around the inside of a tube turning in the dark to make a gravity out of nothing but patience and engineering. The future everyone agreed was coming. The next place. You couldn't keep a species on one rock, the argument went; one rock was one bad day from over, one impact, one plague, one war, and the answer was to spread out, to build the redundancy, to put some of the eggs somewhere else.

He believed it, more or less. He was, after all, out here, in the thin leading edge of exactly that. The unromantic edge. The renderings never showed the part where a man fixed a sealed pump with the wrong grease at oh-three-forty so that the boring nickel-iron could eventually become someone's curving green river. They never showed the debt. But that was every frontier, he figured. Somebody always did the gravel.

"Maybe it's us," he told the recorder. "Tell Okonkwo I'm thrilled. Tell her I'm photographing her feedstock as we speak." He looked at the eggs. "Tell her to send better eggs."

◆

The orbit he'd been working for six weeks did not behave, and that was the only reason he'd come to this sector at all.

It had come up as an outlier in the consortium's catalog cross-check, the kind of thing the automated survey flagged and nobody chased, because chasing it cost delta-v and delta-v was money and the flag was almost certainly a bad solution from too few observations. A small body, or a piece of one, on an orbit that didn't sit right—too eccentric for its neighborhood, the inclination wrong, the kind of path a thing got onto by being hit, or by being where a family of fragments had once been a single parent body, except the dynamics didn't quite close on either story. Roan had run the numbers three ways. The honest answer was *insufficient data*, which was also the answer that justified flying out to get more, which was also the answer a man told himself when he wanted a reason to chase a thing.

He'd told Mara it was a feedstock survey. It was, mostly. But he'd built the sweep around the bad orbit, and on the fourth day he'd gotten close enough for the long-baseline radar to give him a return, and the return was when the survey stopped being boring.

It was too dark.

Everything out here was dark—that was the point, that was why you used radar and not your eyes—but albedo was a fingerprint, and the families of rocks each had their own. Carbonaceous stuff drank the light. Metals threw it back. This thing drank more than carbon should, across a band of the spectrum where Roan had never seen a natural body drink, and when he'd cleaned the return up over three days of passes the shape of it sat wrong in his gut the way the heater alarm had sat wrong—a sensor being right about something it didn't understand.

It was not round. Fragments weren't round; that was fine. But it was not *broken-round* either, not the cracked-potato shape of a collision shard. It had a long axis that was too long, and a regularity to the radar shadow along that axis, a periodicity, faint, that his processing kept trying to call noise and kept failing to make go away. Like a corrugation. Like something that had once had a structure to it and had kept the ghost of the structure after the structure was gone.

And it was cold. Colder than it should have been for its orbit and its presumed composition, which meant either he had the composition wrong or it had a thermal history he couldn't account for, and he had run that three ways too.

He'd taken a spectrum on the closest pass. He'd meant to look at it properly. He'd looked at it for about ninety seconds, long enough to see lines in it he didn't recognize—not the clean signatures of the ices and silicates and metals he knew, but something layered under those, trace stuff, faint, in proportions he had no slot in his head for—and then the closest-approach window had closed and the survey clock had pulled him off to the next assigned target, and he had told himself he'd come back to it.

That had been eleven days ago.

◆

He kept meaning to write it up.

That was the thing he turned over, that morning, after the eggs and the gyrealm talk, with the bad orbit's object sitting in a file he hadn't opened in eleven days. The protocol was clear and Roan was, despite the grease and the variances, a man who respected protocol where protocol mattered. An anomalous body got a flag, a flag got an observation log, an observation log went up the chain, and someone with more instruments and less debt eventually looked at it. That was how it worked. That was how a frontier didn't kill more people than it had to—everybody wrote everything down, so the next person didn't learn it the hard way.

He hadn't written it up.

He told himself it was the survey schedule, which was real. He told himself it was that a single ship's data on a faint return was worthless until he had more passes, which was true. He told himself that the spectrum was probably a calibration ghost, his sensors were old, the *Margin* was held together with the wrong grease, and a man who filed an extraordinary return off a flaky instrument was a man the consortium learned to stop reading. All of that was true and none of it was why.

The why was smaller and he didn't like it.

The why was that the thing was wrong in a way that was going to cost him. If he filed it—if he filed it *honestly*, with the dark band and the periodicity and the lines he couldn't name—one of two things happened. Either they laughed at it, in which case he'd burned credibility he couldn't spare, the credibility being the only collateral he had left after the debt had eaten the rest. Or they didn't laugh. They didn't laugh, and they sent someone with real instruments, and the claim—if there was a claim, if the bad orbit had brought him to something that was *worth* anything—stopped being his. You didn't file an anomaly and keep the rock. He'd spent six weeks and a lot of delta-v he was carrying on credit to get out here, and the one find that wasn't gravel was a find he couldn't afford to give away and couldn't afford to understand.

So it sat in a file. And he ran the survey, the honest gravel survey, and at the end of each shift he opened the file and looked at the radar shadow with its faint impossible corrugation and the spectrum with its lines he didn't have words for, and he did not write it up, and he told himself tomorrow.

He thought about Devi, who would not have let him do this. Devi had been the conscience of the *Margin* the way the smoke alarm was the conscience of the *Margin*—she'd have screamed at him about a thing she didn't fully understand, *file it, Iyer, you don't sit on a return that drinks light, that's not a claim, that's somebody's safety, what if there's more of them, what if one of them's on a path that matters,* and he'd have argued the money at her and she'd have won, because she always won the ones about other people's safety, that was who she was, and then the lock had failed and taken her on no one's watch and the lesson Roan had taken from it, the lesson he was not proud of, was that the universe killed you on a fatigue crack you couldn't see no matter how carefully you wrote things down, so you might as well take care of your own air first.

It was a bad lesson. He knew it was a bad lesson. He went to bed with it anyway.

◆

On the twelfth day he did the thing he'd been not doing.

Not the filing. He wasn't ready for the filing, he told himself, and the telling had the worn quality of a coin handled too long. But he opened the file, and he pulled the spectrum up properly, on the good screen, with the survey clock paused, in the middle of his shift where he had no excuse, and he looked at the lines.

He didn't have the library to identify them. The *Margin*'s reference set was for prospecting—it told you nickel from iron from olivine from water ice, it told you what a rock was worth, and it was useless for what he was looking at, which was not a rock's worth. He sat with the unlabeled lines, the layered trace signatures, the faint heavy stuff sitting under the ordinary stuff, and he had the strong and unwelcome sense of a man looking at handwriting in an alphabet he didn't read—the certainty that it *was* handwriting, that the marks were not weather and not noise and not the wrong grease, that some hand had made them, against the total absence of any idea what they said.

He thought: nothing natural drinks light like that.

He thought: nothing broken keeps a corrugation.

He thought, and this was the one he put away fastest, the one he covered the way you cover an alarm at oh-three-forty: it's cold because it's old. It's not cold for its orbit. It's cold for its *age*. It's been out here so long the warmth of whatever made it is sixty million years gone.

He did not know where the number came from. He had no business with a number like that; he was a pilot, he photographed gravel, he fixed pumps. The number had simply arrived, the way Devi's voice arrived, sideways, uninvited, more certain than he was, and he sat in the empty cabin with the second couch behind him and the bad orbit's object turning slow and dark and far too old in the file on the good screen, and the thing he felt was not discovery. It was the specific cold of a competent man who has noticed something he cannot afford to understand, and who knows, the way the body knows an alarm, that he is going to have to understand it anyway, and that it is going to cost him everything he came out here to keep.

He closed the file.

"Survey's on schedule," he told the recorder, that evening, for Mara, fourteen minutes away. "Gravel's looking like gravel." He looked at the dark band in the corner of the screen where he'd left the radar shadow up, the corrugation his processing kept trying and failing to call noise. "I've got an outlier I want to run a few more passes on before I bother anybody with it. Probably nothing. Bad instrument, bad geometry. You know how it is." A pause, his own, that the lag would carry to her clean. "I'll write it up when it's worth writing up."

He sent it. He sat in the webbing in the cold cabin with the heater loop ticking along on the wrong grease, holding, for now, and he did not run the drill, and he did not open the file again, and outside the hull the thing the dead had built large enough and far enough away that even sixty-six million years had not been able to grind it down turned once more, slow, in the dark, keeping the ghost of its shape, waiting, with the patience of something that no longer had anywhere to be, for the one man within fourteen light-minutes of it to stop being able to afford to look away.
