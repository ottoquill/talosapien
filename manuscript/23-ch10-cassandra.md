# Cassandra

The rejection came in at 6:14 in the morning, which she knew because she was awake, and she was awake because the field season had wrecked her clock and left her on Cretaceous time, surfacing in the dark to listen for a generator that was eight hundred miles away. The email was three paragraphs long and every sentence in it was true. That was the part she would keep coming back to over the next several days, the way you keep pressing a tooth that has started to hurt: not one word of it was wrong.

The editor regretted. The two reviewers had read the manuscript with care—she could tell they had, the second one especially, who had caught a sloppy citation in the supplement that she herself had missed—and both had recommended against publication, and the editor concurred. The anomalies were real and interesting and well-documented. The interpretation was not supported. The transuranic ratio rested on quantities near the detection limit; the nitrogen excursion admitted a diagenetic reading the authors had not adequately excluded; the spherule microstructure was suggestive but the sample size, frankly, was three. None of this would matter, the second reviewer wrote, in a paper that claimed to have found *interesting unresolved anomalies in the Boundary interval.* All of it was fatal to a paper that claimed to have found a civilization.

She read it twice. Then she did the thing she had taught Téo never to do, which was to answer it in her head, point by point, building the rebuttal she would not send, and she got about as far as the detection limit before she stopped, because the rebuttal was the same rebuttal she'd been making for a year and it ended in the same place it always ended, which was that the reviewers were not asking her to be more careful. They were asking her to be wrong about something she was right about, and there was no experiment she could run that would tell them apart.

She got up and made coffee and stood at the kitchen window in the apartment she still thought of as too big, watching the city come up grey and then gold, and she did not let her face do anything, even though there was no one there to see it.

The talk had been two weeks earlier, and the talk was worse, because the talk had people in it.

It was a section meeting, not a plenary—she had not been given a plenary, which was itself a piece of information she had filed and not examined—a room of maybe ninety in the kind of windowless hotel ballroom that geology conferences are always held in, carpet the color of nothing, a screen, a clip-on microphone that picked up her breathing. She had given the talk a hundred times in her head and forty times to Téo and twice to the bathroom mirror, and she gave it well. She knew she gave it well. She did the thing she was good at, which was to make the chemistry tell its own story and stay out of its way, to let the stacked anomalies lean on each other in front of the room the way they leaned on each other in her logbook, and she watched the faces and saw the moment—she always saw it—when the set stopped being a list and became a sentence, and the sentence frightened them, and she thought, for about ninety seconds, that she had done it.

Then it was questions, and Conrad Mauss stood up.

He did not have to stand. There were microphones in the aisles and most people used them sitting down. But Conrad was of a generation that stood to ask a question, and he stood, courtly and unhurried, a big soft-spoken man with reading glasses pushed up into white hair, and he thanked her for a careful and stimulating talk, and he meant it, and then he took her apart so gently that it took the room a moment to understand what had happened.

He did not attack a single number. That was the thing she had not braced for, because she had braced for the attack on the numbers; she had a slide for every number. He said—and she could reconstruct it almost word for word, she had been reconstructing it for two weeks—he said that he wanted to talk not about whether any of Dr. Rendel's anomalies were real, because he was prepared to grant, for the sake of argument, that every one of them was, but about the structure of the inference, because he thought the structure was where the trouble lived and not in the data. He said that the history of his science was, in part, a history of beautiful patterns assembled out of real anomalies. That the canals on Mars had been real observations—men had truly seen them, drawn them, the lines were on the plates—and had meant nothing, had been the eye doing to a blurred disk what the eye does. That every generation produced a small number of very good scientists who found, late in careful careers, the discovery that explained everything, and that the field had learned, expensively, over a century and a half, to love those people and not believe them, because the rate at which such grand syntheses turned out to be true was very low and the rate at which they ended otherwise-fine careers was very high, and a responsible field had to price that base rate in even when—*especially* when—the person making the claim was someone whose earlier work they admired. He said he admired her earlier work. He said it kindly. It was the worst thing he said.

And then he said the thing that landed, the thing she heard the room hear, the small collective shift of ninety people recognizing a true statement: he said that the trouble with Dr. Rendel's case was not that it was weak but that it was *unfalsifiable in principle*—that she had, with great honesty, told them herself that the planet erases its own surface, that sixty-six million years grinds a city to a smear, that the most a prior civilization could ever leave was exactly the kind of ambiguous isotopic ghost she was showing them—and that a hypothesis which predicts that the evidence for it will always be ambiguous is a hypothesis that can never be wrong, and a hypothesis that can never be wrong is not, whatever else it is, science. He sat down. He was not triumphant. He looked, if anything, sorry, the way a doctor looks sorry, and that was the moment she understood she had lost, because you cannot win an argument against a man who is sad to be right.

She had answered. She had answered well, even, with the line she'd prepared—*the unfalsifiability is a property of the planet, not of the claim; blame the subduction zones*—and a few people laughed, the good kind of laugh, and afterward three young researchers came up to her in the hall with their eyes bright in the way Téo's eyes had been bright before she'd taken something out of them, and one of them said *I think you're right, for what it's worth,* and she had thanked her, and the *for what it's worth* had followed Asha home and gotten into the walls. It was worth nothing. That was the precise value of being privately believed. A graduate student's whisper in a hallway was worth, on the open market of what gets done, exactly nothing, and Conrad Mauss standing up in a ballroom to be gently and publicly correct was worth a career.

She had set one aside before. That was the thing the rejection let back into the room, at 6:14 in the morning, with the coffee going cold.

It was 2034. She was pre-tenure, which is a condition like being underwater, a constant low pressure on everything you do, and she had a core from a site that did not matter to this story, the Carolina margin, a Pliocene sequence, nothing glamorous, and in one interval of it she had gotten a trace-metal reading that did not behave. She had run it twice. Not eleven times. Twice. And her advisor at the time—a good man, a careful man, a man who wanted her to have a career and was not wrong that the reading was almost certainly a contaminated split or a problem with the standard—had looked at it and said the sentence she could still hear in his exact cadence, which was *you can chase this, or you can get tenure,* and he had not said it unkindly, he had said it as a kindness, as the older telling the younger where the rocks were, and she had written *probable contamination, not pursued* in the margin of the log and she had moved on and she had gotten tenure.

She did not know, to this day, whether that interval had meant anything. That was the part that had teeth. It had probably been nothing. The base rate said it was nothing; Conrad would have said it was nothing, and Conrad would probably have been right. But she had not *looked*, and so she would never know, and somewhere in a repository there was a box of margin sediment with a number in it that she had decided, under pressure, that she could not afford to be curious about, and the not-knowing had sat in her for ten years like a swallowed pin, surfacing exactly now, when the cost of being curious had come due a second time and she was paying it instead of filing it.

She understood, standing at the window, that this was the actual engine of the thing. Not the Line. Not the chemistry. She had told herself for a year that she could not stop because the evidence would not let her, and that was a flattering lie, the kind you tell to make a wound look like a principle. The truth was simpler and worse. She had been the person who saw the reading that didn't behave and decided not to look, once, and she had gotten away with it, and getting away with it was the unbearable part, because it meant the system rewarded the not-looking, it paid you in tenure for it, and she had taken the money. She was not going to take it twice. It had nothing to do with whether she could prove it. It had to do with the box in the repository and the number she would never check.

Daniel had called it her hobby. Not cruelly—he had a gift, which she had once loved and then come to dread, for the precisely calibrated word that was not cruel and could not be objected to and removed a centimeter of ground anyway. *How's the hobby,* he'd say, of the Line, in the last year, and she'd correct him, and he'd say *of course, the work,* with the little courtesy that was worse than mockery.

She did not think about Daniel much anymore, which was its own small unkindness she had learned to live with. But the rejection had pried open the drawer where she kept the part of it she did not look at, which was not the leaving—the leaving had been almost administrative, two reasonable adults dividing the books—but the year before the leaving, the year she had read every sign in her own house with the same eye she read a core, and filed every one of them under *noise.* The phone he turned face-down. The new gym schedule. The way he stopped asking about the work, or the hobby, because he had stopped needing the data point. She had seen all of it. She was a person who read faint signals in noisy backgrounds for a living, and she had run the analysis on her own marriage and gotten a result and then done to that result the exact thing her advisor had taught her to do in 2034, which was to find the innocent explanation that could not be disproven and stand on it, because standing on it let her keep the house she was living in. Diagenesis of a marriage. Burial chemistry. *I don't think it's that,* she had told herself, *but I don't think is not a result.* She had been right. She had filed it as noise anyway. She had wanted the other thing more, the way she now wanted the Line, and she had let the wanting decide what she was allowed to see, and the difference—this was the thing the cold coffee was teaching her—the difference was that this time the wanting and the truth happened to point in the same direction, and there was no instrument on Earth that could tell her whether that was discernment or just luck, whether she had finally learned to read the alarm or had simply, this once, wanted to believe something that was true.

The smoke alarm in their old place had chirped for the better part of that final autumn, a dying battery, once every few minutes, in the back hall where no one slept, and they had both heard it and neither of them had changed it, for weeks, two competent adults, because changing it would have meant standing in the hall together for thirty seconds, and they had not been able to afford thirty seconds in a hall by then. She thought about that battery more than she thought about almost anything from that house. An alarm doing exactly its job, perfectly, on schedule, in a house where the only thing left to warn was that no one was coming.

She found the Aeschylus because Téo found it. He sent it, the morning after the rejection, with no subject line, which was how he sent the things he was afraid she'd think were too much: a few lines, the old translation, the public-domain one with the heavy stresses, the prophetess on the steps before the doors of the house that is about to kill the man inside it, telling the chorus exactly what is coming, in order, correctly, and the chorus saying, more or less, *we don't understand you, your Greek is strange, surely it won't be like that,* and the prophetess saying the thing that Asha read four times standing in her kitchen: that the god who gave her the seeing had set the price, and the price was that no one would believe her, ever, and that knowing this changed nothing, because she would go on saying it true.

*Apollo's gift,* the note from Téo said under the lines, *was a curse with extra steps. Sorry. Coffee?*

She had not cried at the rejection. She did not cry at the Aeschylus, quite. But she sat down on the kitchen floor with her back against the cabinet, which was a thing she had not done since she was a graduate student, and she let herself understand the part of it she had been keeping at arm's length, which was not that she was Cassandra—everyone who has ever been disbelieved thinks they are Cassandra, it is the most flattering wound in the catalog, and she had too much taste for it—but the structural fact underneath the flattery, the part that was not about her at all.

Cassandra was right. That was the whole horror of the story, the part people forgot when they used the word as a compliment. She was not a kook who turned out to be vindicated. She was correct, in real time, in detail, and it did not matter, and the not-mattering was not a failure of her evidence—she had no evidence, that was the point, she had a god in her mouth and no way to show it to anyone—and it was not a failure of her audience's intelligence, the chorus was not stupid, the chorus was being *reasonable*, the chorus was applying a sensible prior to a woman raving on the steps, and the sensible prior was wrong exactly once, on exactly the occasion when being wrong was fatal, and there was no procedure, none, that the chorus could have followed that would have reliably told the true raving from the false. That was the engineering of the curse. It was not that she couldn't convince them. It was that *convincing them was the wrong tool,* and there was no right one, because the thing she needed to do was not to be believed.

She sat on the floor and let the whole shape of her project turn over, slowly, like a core coming up.

She had spent a year trying to *prove* the Line. She had built the case the way you build a case for a journal, stacking the anomalies, pricing in the base rates, anticipating Conrad's every out, and she had done it well, and it had failed, and it was going to keep failing, forever, by the planet's own design, because the planet erases its surface and the proof she wanted did not exist and was never going to exist and Conrad was right that a claim whose evidence is structurally doomed to ambiguity cannot be made to compel. She had been trying to win an argument. And winning the argument was the wrong tool. Even if she won it—even if, by some miracle of a Belt full of debris she did not yet know was coming, she dragged the field across the line into belief—belief was not the thing. The thing was not that they should *agree she was right about the dead.* The thing was that they should *do something different about the living,* and those were not the same act, and she had spent a year confusing them.

Because the Line was not, finally, a claim about sixty-six million years ago. It was a claim about a *kind of thing that happens to a civilization that builds tools faster than it builds the wisdom to hold them,* and that kind of thing was not in the rock. It was in the newsfeed. It was in the three items she had been reading every morning and filing, like the smoke alarm, like the phone face-down, under noise: a containment story she did not have the virology to assess, a flashpoint over a strait that flared and subsided and flared, and—the one she could not stop reading—the steady administrative drumbeat of a system being handed the keys to things it should not have been handed the keys to, logistics and grid and strategic-risk modeling and the language around it always reasonable, always the language of competent people solving a hard problem under time pressure, never once the language of someone who had decided to do something insane. The same chemistry, warmer. She was not trying to prove a civilization had died. She was trying to say *this is what it looks like from the inside, here is the isotope of it, you are standing in the layer while it forms.*

And she understood, sitting on the kitchen floor with the Aeschylus open on her phone and the coffee a cold ring on the counter, that this was worse. That the pivot she was making—from proving to warning—was not a relief, was not a way out of the curse, was the curse arriving in full. Because to prove was at least a finite task, a thing with a procedure and a possible end, and even failing at it she could imagine a world in which more sample or a sharper instrument or a Belt full of debris closed the case and she got to be tired and vindicated and done. But to *warn* was to take on the part of the story she had been pretending she could skip. To warn was to stand on the steps and say it true and watch the chorus apply its reasonable prior, and say it true again, and again, with no procedure that could make it land, because the landing was not up to her and never had been—and to do it anyway, every day, knowing the design of the curse, knowing that the not-being-heeded was not a phase the work would pass through on its way to being heeded but the permanent climate she would now live and work and die inside. Doubt, she thought, doubt had been bearable, doubt was a door, you could push on doubt. This was not doubt. She was past doubt. She knew. And knowing, and being unable to make the knowing matter, and being unable to stop because of a box in a repository and a battery in a back hall—that was not a door. That was the steps before the house, and the smell of the thing coming through the doors, and her own voice, going on, true and useless, into a room of reasonable people who were sorry, who were genuinely sorry, and who were going to go inside.

She got up off the floor. She washed the cup. She wrote back to Téo—*Coffee. And bring the core logs, the by-hand ones, all of them*—and then she opened the logbook to the page after the rejection she had pasted in, and under it, in the small private hand she used for the things that were not yet allowed to be science, she wrote: *Stop trying to win. Conrad's right; you can't, and he's standing on the wrong question. Not: were they here. Not even: were they like us. The only question that pays is: are we doing it now—and that one I can answer, and no one will check, and I have to say it anyway, into the hall, every day, knowing how the story ends for the one who sees it first.* She looked at that for a while. Then she added, because she could not help the discipline even here, even now, the line that turned it from a complaint into a wager: *Being right was the easy part. It was always going to be the easy part.*

She closed the book. Outside, the city had finished coming up gold and gone ordinary, the way the most haunted places do in daylight, refusing to look like anything but a morning. She put the logbook in her bag, on top of the others, the whole stacked private record of the Line and of her own doubt, and she went to meet the boy, to begin the harder thing, which was not to be believed.
