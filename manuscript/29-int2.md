# The Recoverability Budget

Let us tell you what sixty-six million years does, so that you understand the size of the silence we are about to ask you to sit inside.

It subducts. The floor of the ocean they sailed has, in places, slid back down into the mantle and ceased to exist — not eroded, not buried, but unmade, returned to the melt, every record on it returned with it to a state in which the word *record* no longer applies. It erodes. The mountains they may have named are gone, ground to the sand that became the stone that is now eroding in its turn. It buries, and the burial cooks: the slow chemistry of pressure and heat that reworks a sediment, that takes the delicate thing you hoped had been preserved and smears it gently into the ambient until the signal and the noise are the same substance. It overwrites. Sixty-six million years is four or five complete reorganizations of the living world, each laying its own dead across the older dead, each generation of fossil-hunters in the deep future — if there are any — having to dig through us to reach them.

Against all of that, here is the budget. Here is the whole of what a civilization can hope to leave, if it leaves anything, to a reader a hundred thousand human lifetimes downstream.

It can leave a chemical anomaly, if the anomaly is global and sharp and made of something the planet does not make on its own. The iridium qualifies, which is why we have the iridium. The transuranics qualify, barely, because their daughters decay on a clock we can read. The shifted isotopes of the lighter elements qualify only as a whisper, deniable, a thing you can hear or not hear depending on what you came hoping to hear.

It can leave, if it was very large and very far from the grinding surface of the Earth, a physical object. Not on the planet — the planet eats its own works — but out in the slow cold, in the belt of stones where erosion is a rumor and a million years is an afternoon. We will come to the object. The object is the only thing in this entire account that you could put your hand on, and a man is about to put his hand on it, and it will change nothing for the people who have decided in advance, because a thing too strange to be explained is, for a certain cast of mind, simply a thing not yet explained, and that mind is not wrong to be that mind. It is only, in this one case, fatally late.

And it can leave bone, marked. A few teeth. The negative impression of a worked edge. The deathbed of a single afternoon, if a flood happened to roll over the dying fast enough to seal them from the air. We have that afternoon. We have it in almost unbearable detail, the fish with the falling sky in their gills, and we have spent a great deal of a science learning to read it, and it has told us everything except the one thing we would trade all the rest to know.

◆

It cannot leave the why.

This is the seam we most need you to see before we go on, because the rest of the book is built across it, and if you do not see it you will think we are being coy at the end when we are only being honest. Intent does not fossilize. A decision leaves the same trace whether it was made in malice, in terror, in error, or in a love so large it could not tell itself from despair. The clay records that a thing happened. It does not record, it cannot record, it will never record, what the thing was *for*.

So when we arrive — and we are nearly there — at the object falling out of the dark toward the shallow sea, we will be able to tell you, to a precision that will frighten you, the mass of it and the speed of it and the angle and the hour and the height of the wave. And we will not be able to tell you whether it was a weapon, loosed to end a war by ending a world; or a shield that failed, a stone they were trying to push aside or mine or master and lost their grip on; or a door, closed deliberately, the considered choice of someone who had looked at what they had built and decided that the only safe number of such civilizations was none, including their own, and reached out and turned off the light on purpose so that no one could ever switch it back on and find the weapons still in the drawer.

All three are consistent with the clay. All three. We have run it every way there is to run it, and the rock holds its peace, and the peace is not neutrality — it is the specific, permanent, structural fact that the difference between those three acts lived entirely in a mind that the planet has long since unmade. The murderer and the suicide and the failed savior all leave the same crater. You are going to want, very badly, to be told which one Iren was. We want it more than you do; we have loved him longer. We are telling you now, while there is still distance, so that you are not ambushed by it: we do not know, and the not-knowing is not a gap in our work. It is the finished shape of the truth.

> *Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair: / Nothing beside remains.*

The traveler in the poem reads a face in the sand and a boast on a pedestal, and from them reconstructs a king, and a sculptor, and a contempt. He has more than we have. He has a face, and words, and the words even confess their own irony, the works gone and the boast remaining to mock them. We have less. We have the iridium and the strange glass and the marked bone and, out in the cold, the turning thing that stopped turning. No face. No words. No boast and no confession. Only the pedestal, and the level sand, and the certainty — the one thing we are certain of — that someone built high enough to fall this far.

We are going to go on now, into the turn, into the war, into the object. Keep the budget in your mind as you read. Everything we are about to show you with such confidence is a kindness laid over a blank. We earned the confidence honestly. The blank is honest too. Hold them in separate hands.
