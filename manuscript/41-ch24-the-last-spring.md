# The Last Spring

The smallest of the four could hold a color now, and she spent the early part of the morning teaching it to be honest, which is to say teaching it nothing, which is to say sitting with it in the apertured light and letting it learn from her body that a throat held was a throat lying and that lying cost more than it bought.

It had begun, in the way of the young, by holding everything. It would feel a thing—a hunger, a startle, the small affront of an older sibling taking the warmer patch of sand—and clamp the flush down out of some instinct she could not name and did not like, and she would watch the effort of it cross the new body like a cramp, and she would let her own throat go openly to whatever she was feeling, plainly, in the light, until the small one's clamp eased and the color came up where it belonged and the world got that much easier to read by exactly one creature. This was the only thing she knew how to teach. She was not sure it was teaching. She thought it might only be company. She had decided, over four seasons, that the difference did not signify.

There were four. She counted them with the side of one finger and the count came out to four, and the morning came in long and broken through the seaward apertures, the river-light crossing the floor and the four small spines in its slow moving grille, and outside, where she could not see it but could feel it in the vestibule of her ear, the day was settling into the particular still high weather of the turn of the season, the air pressure level and generous, a day the body wanted to be out in. She noted this. She had made herself, this spring, note the days that were beautiful, the way other seasons she had made herself note the flocks that were gone, because a record that kept only the losses was a lie of a different kind, and she had sworn the marsh an honest one.

◆

She took the four out to the river, which she had not done before, because the crèche kept the young close and the river was a long uncovered way through the city, and the elders of the crèche would have weighed it and weighed it slow. She did not bring it to them. She had begun, this spring, doing small things without bringing them to anyone, not from defiance but from a kind of economy she did not examine, and she carried the smallest against her breast and let the three older walk and stumble and right themselves between her feet, broadcasting their whole hearts into the light at every new thing, and the city let them pass.

It was a good city. She made herself note that too. It lay along the slow delta in the way it had lain since before her line kept the marsh, the low seaward dwellings and the long causeways and the launch-road running back toward the dark cliff where the Reckoning held its hall, and above the launch-road, pale in the daylight, the ghost of the wanderer the Wheels turned around, so faint you had to know to look. The young did not know to look. They looked at the water and the traders and the river-light coming up off the flood in colors she had no word for and they had not yet been given the words for either, and she watched them not look at the sky and was glad, with the brief uncomplicated gladness the body allows before the mind arrives, and then she did not let the mind arrive. She had gotten better, this spring, at not letting the mind arrive. It was the only discipline she had ever been good at, and she had learned it late, and from the wrong teacher, which was grief.

A trader at the causeway-foot was selling the early shoal-fruit, the soft red kind that came only in the few days of the turn, and she stopped, and the trader read her throat—it was open, it was always open—and read in it the thing about the four and the season, and gave her four, one for each, and would not weigh them against anything, which was a thing that still happened in the city, the un-weighed gift, the choosing-not-to-account, and she stood at the causeway-foot and let the four small loud bodies make a ruin of the soft red fruit and broadcast their whole greedy hearts into the light, and the trader watched them and let its own throat go to the warm color of a thing well given, and for the length of it there was nothing in the marsh that was dying and nothing in the deep hall that could not be brought to anyone, there was only the turn of the season and the red fruit and the four.

She knew what she was doing. That is the part the rock cannot give us and we will not pretend it can: we do not know that she knew. We have only the habit of the woman we have built from a lifetime of her keeping, and the habit says that a creature who had spent four seasons making herself note the beautiful days does not, on the most beautiful one, fail to notice that she is hoarding it. So we have decided to believe she knew, and to tell you that we decided. She stood at the causeway-foot and let the morning be long and did not bring it to anyone and did not write it down, and if there was a grey under the open color of her throat that day, the river did not keep it, and neither can we.

◆

Sael was at the river too, which she had not expected, because the elder did not come down from the hall, and had not in seasons, and she saw the old slow crest first, the settled permanent grey at the throat that the whole of the Reckoning knew and that the young of the crèche feared without knowing why, and then she saw that Sael was sitting on the warm stone of the lowest causeway with the ledgers—the old physical ones, the kept-by-hand kind, the kind the Reckoning had stopped keeping when it built the thing that kept without hands—spread on the stone, and was not reading them.

That was the thing she carried away and could not put down, after, in the little after she had: that Sael had brought the ledgers down to the river and was not reading them. The old one sat in the high still light with a season's weather coming level off the flood, and had the whole long record of the weighing open on the warm stone, every honest deposition the Concord had laid down in a long life of laying them down, and was looking instead at the water, and at the four, who had found the old one the way the young find the oldest body in any gathering, and had arranged themselves at the edge of the ledgers with their hearts in the light, and Sael let them, and did not move the ledgers out of their reach, and put the side of one old finger out, slow, and let the smallest one clamp its three opposed fingers around it the way the young clamp anything offered.

"You brought them to the water," Sael said. The voice was the deposition cadence, everything weighed, nothing retracted, slow as the laying-down of a stratum. "Good. Bring them to the water."

She did not say it was unweighed. Sael would have known. She sat on the warm stone at the edge of the ledgers and let her open throat say the thing she would not, which was that she had stopped weighing the small things, and Sael read it, and did not counsel her, which was itself a kind of counsel, from the one creature in the city whose whole life had been the slow honest weighing.

We want to tell you what Sael did then. We have wanted to, through the whole of this telling, more than we have wanted to recover anything else in the deep past, and we are going to tell you instead that we cannot. This is the place the clay goes silent and we have sworn not to fill the silence with anything but the admission that it is silent. We know the elder existed; we have read the institution Sael belonged to in the long shape of what it built and what it failed to stop. We know there was a last act, because everyone has one, and because the shape of the city's ending bends, very slightly, around a refusal we can almost but not quite resolve—a thing laid down or a thing laid aside, a weighing finished or a weighing finally let go. We can build Iren's fear from the bruise of it and Liss's grief from the long habit of it. We cannot build this. The old keep their last choices closest, and the rock keeps nothing closest of all, and somewhere on a warm causeway in a high still light at the turn of a season sixty-six million years gone, an elder of a vanished accounting looked at the water and the four and the open record and did the most important thing the elder ever did, and we will never know what it was, and we have decided that the honest thing is to stand at the place and say so, and not to give the dead a sentence we dug out of our own need.

She remembered, after, only that Sael let the smallest hold the old finger for a long time, and that when the four grew loud and she gathered them to go on, the elder did not say go well, in the formal mode, the way the Reckoning said it. Sael said something quieter, and shorter, and we do not have it, and she carried it the rest of the day like a stone in the dark, by its weight and not its shape.

◆

Iren came down to the river in the afternoon, which he never did, and that was how she knew.

She did not know what she knew. That is the truth of it, and we have it from the long shape of her, the woman who read a dropped note in a crier's third phrase and a flock's absence in a quiet dawn and her mate's grief in a throat held past holding: she read, in the fact of him coming down to the water in the afternoon with the work-hall still full of light behind him on the cliff, that something was ending. Not which thing. Not the size of it, though she had felt the size of it in the vestibule of her ear the night he reached the bottom of his sentence and could not show her under it. She read only the ending, the way the body reads a floor beginning to tilt by an amount too small for anyone but the frightened to feel, and she did not ask him what, because she had learned over a hard season that the what was a thing he could not make into a session or a study or a grief she knew the shape of, and that to ask was to make him hold a color he could not afford to hold, and she had spent the morning teaching the smallest of the four not to do that very thing.

So she did not ask. She made the choice on the warm stone of the causeway with the four loud around her feet, and it was the tenderest choice of her life, and she has it in common with no one we can name and with everyone we have ever loved across a silence: she chose to spend the day as if it were ordinary, because he had not told her it was not, and because the not-telling was the last gift he had to give and she would not refuse it by being too honest to take it. The marriage of their two honesties had always been this—she greyed in the open and he held in the dark and each forgave the other the half they could not do—and she understood, watching him come down the launch-road in the afternoon light toward the water and the four, that the held color and the open one had finally come to the same place, which was a man who would not say the size of the ending and a woman who would not make him, and that this was not a failure of their honesty but its furthest reach: two records meeting at the one fact neither could set down.

"The smallest holds a color now," she said, when he reached them. She said it the way you say the weather. "I taught it nothing. I sat with it."

He crouched at the edge of the water and the smallest one came to him with its whole heart in the light, and he put the side of one finger out and let it clamp on, the way Sael had, and at his throat the grey was there under the held color and he let it be there, in the open, on the river, where anyone could read him, the second time in their bond she had seen him allow it. He did not say what was ending. She did not ask. The four made a ruin of the last of the red fruit and the light came up off the flood in colors above the colors, and the wanderer hung pale and unseen over the launch-road, and the day was very fine, the air level and generous, the still high weather of the turn that the body wanted to be out in.

"I counted them this morning," he said. "From the hall. I couldn't see them. I counted them anyway."

"There were four," she said.

"There were four." He let the smallest keep his finger. "I came down because the count from the hall stopped being enough. I needed it to be four where I could put a finger on it." He did not say more than that, and she heard the bottom under it, the place with no layer to lift, and she did not press into the silence because she could feel its size, and because pressing would have cost him the held color he had finally let go, and because she had decided, on the warm stone, that she would not buy her own knowing with his last gift.

"Then put a finger on it," she said, and he did, the side of one finger laid along each small spine where it lay against the warm stone, four, in the open, on the river, while the light moved across the four and across his hands in its slow grille, and she counted with him, her finger following his, the same motion from opposite ends of it, and the count came out to four, and was the only count in the whole of the city that day that closed.

◆

They walked the four home in the last of the river-light, the long uncovered way back through the city, and she made herself note it. The low seaward dwellings going warm in the falling light. The traders folding the causeway stalls. A single river-crier, far out on the south shoal where the warmth pooled, fishing the empty water with the folded patient body of a thing that did not know it was the last of its stretch—she did not raise an instrument to it and she did not write it down, it was hers, she had kept it all spring, the one true thing she let herself keep—and the smallest of the four, on her breast, lifting its new-honest throat toward the crier's distant shape and holding, all on its own, the faint flush of a color of its own, *that, and far, and alive,* the first wholly private signal of a new life broadcast into a light no after would read.

She noted the day. She set none of it down. She had decided, that spring, that there were things you kept by living through them, that a record was not the only fidelity and was not even the deepest one, and that the deepest one had no medium the marsh would hold, and was the holding itself, the standing in the light with the four and the man and the open throat and the un-asked question, faith with what was about to be lost, which was not hope, and which she did not, on that fine still evening at the turn of the last spring, mistake for it, or write down, or bring to anyone, or weigh.

He counted them again before they slept. So did she. There were four. The air came level and generous through the seaward apertures, and the river ran up out of the dark in its slow flood, and the wanderer turned somewhere pale and unseen with the Wheels turning around it full of seed-stores and the long ledgers and the hedge against every morning but the one that was coming, and the four were loud and well and very nearly able to hold, each of them now, the smallest flush of a color of their own, and the day had been very fine, and she had kept it the only way it could be kept, which was by spending it, and not being told.
