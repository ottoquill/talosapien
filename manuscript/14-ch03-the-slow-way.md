# The Slow Way

From the south hide she could see the whole long-marsh at low water, and she counted the criers the way she had been taught to count anything that was leaving: not the birds, which moved and would cheat you, but the empty perches, which did not.

There were sixty-one channel-stakes in the near reach, old fishing-stakes the river had not yet taken back, and in a good year a crier stood on every one at the turn of the tide, hunched against the wind off the inland sea with its long throat folded, waiting for the water to drop and the mud to give up its small lives. She had drawn the reach so many seasons that she did not need to look at her own marks to know where sixty-one stood. This morning there were nine birds on the sixty-one stakes, and she made herself look at each of the fifty-two bare ones in turn, slowly, the way you look at a thing so that later you will be able to swear you saw it, because the empty stake was the true count and the bird was only the part that had not happened yet.

She set it down in the journal. Not the number alone—the number was nothing, a number could be argued, she had learned that to the root—but the shape of it: the reach, the tide-state, the wind, the date in their own reckoning of the season, and beside it the thing she had come to write more than any other thing, which was *not the dry.* The dry had not come. The marsh was full to its margins and green to the waterline and the air was thick and sweet with the particular over-ripeness of a wet warm coast, and the criers were dying in it anyway, in the middle of plenty, which was the signature of the thing in the water as surely as a wound is the signature of a blade.

She had stopped, two seasons ago, writing down what she thought it meant. That was the change in her that Iren had not yet found the word for and that she could not have made him feel even if she had handed him the journal, because to him the journal was data and to her it had become the only fidelity she had left to offer. Meaning was for the Reckoning, and the Reckoning weighed meaning honestly and slowly and the criers did not have the time it took to be honest about them. So she wrote down what was, and only what was, the bare stakes and the wet air, in a hand that did not editorialize, the way you write a name and a season on a marker and do not add that the one underneath was loved. The journal was not an argument. She had given up arguments. The journal was a record kept for no one, against the day—and she knew the day was coming and did not let herself know how she knew—when there would be no one to keep it for, and she kept it anyway, because the alternative was to let the criers go down unwitnessed, and she would not. That was the whole of what she had left. She would not let it happen in the dark.

A bird took one of the bare stakes while she watched, dropped onto it out of the grey morning with its throat already folding, and for a moment there were ten, and her own throat went its honest grey at the small mercy of it, and she wrote down *ten* and then, because she was not a sentimentalist and the record did not lie even to comfort her, she wrote down beside it *one new arrival, reach otherwise unchanged,* so that no one reading it—no one, there was no one—would mistake a single tired bird for the marsh coming back.

◆

The thing in the water she could not see, which was the cruelty of it, the thing that made it so easy for the honest to wait.

A drought you could see; a drought wrote itself on the mud in cracks a hatchling could read, and the Concord would act on a thing it could see, eventually, slowly, after the weighing. But the thing they had made and let go—she had traced it now through four seasons and a hundred dissections in the cold-room under the hide, traced it the patient way, the only way she trusted, by following the dead—the thing moved through the *water,* invisible, and it did not kill the criers. That was what she had not been able to make them feel. It killed the small swimming lives in the mud that the criers ate, and the things those lives ate, down at the bottom where no one looked and nothing voted, and the criers starved at the top of it slowly enough that you could tell yourself, every single season, that this season was a bad season and the next would be better, and be honest, and be wrong, and the difference between being honest-and-wrong and being a liar was nothing at all to the birds.

She had opened a crier on the cold-stone two days ago, a fresh one, found stiff on the tide-line with no mark on it, and its crop had been empty and its keel a blade under her fingers, and inside, where the small swimming food should have been, there had been only the clear faint smell of the water itself, the over-sweet smell that meant the bottom of the marsh was wrong all the way down. She had set the blade aside with a care that anyone who knew her would have read as fury. She did not let her color go in the cold-room; there was no one there to read it, and she had found that she could not afford to spend the grey on the dead when she would need it for the living, and that economy frightened her more than anything the dissection had shown, because it meant she had begun, somewhere, to ration grief, and a person who rations grief is a person who has accepted the rate of the loss as a fact of the world rather than a thing to be screamed at, and she had not meant to become that, and she had.

◆

She had brought it to the Reckoning four seasons ago, and they had weighed it honestly, and that was the part she could not get past.

She had not been refused by fools. That was the thing she could not make Iren understand, the few times she had tried, because Iren *was* the Reckoning, Iren believed in the weighing the way you believe in the floor, and to him a thing weighed honestly and laid down was a thing settled, a stratum, load-bearing, done. She had stood in the deep hall with the criers' decline drawn on the read-wall in her own field-marks and she had been heard. They had not laughed at her. They had not been bought; she had watched their throats and the throats had been honest, every one, grey where grey was owed. Sael himself had gone grey at her marsh and had said, in the formal mode, that the loss was real and the cause was likely as she said, and then the weighing had begun, the long honest weighing, the stoppage of the works that fed the thing into the water set against the cost of the stoppage, the certain harm to the marsh against the certain harm to the regions that ran on the works, and every region had laid its color down in turn, honestly, and the honest sum of all those honest colors had been: *study it. Be sure. The stoppage is expensive and the marsh is one marsh and we must be sure.*

And they were being sure. The study was beautiful. It confirmed her to three places and would, in another five seasons, confirm her to four, and somewhere in the fourth place the criers would be a thing the study described in the past tense. She did not blame the regions. She had tried to blame them and could not make it hold; each of them had weighed a true cost against a true cost and chosen the way she might have chosen if she had not stood where she stood, and the thing she had walked out of the deep hall understanding, the thing that had turned her from an advocate into a keeper of records, was that there was no villain in it and no error in it and the criers were going to die anyway. The weighing was honest. The weighing was too slow. Those were both true at once and nothing in the rite was built to hold both, because the rite had been built by a people who had never before been able to kill the world faster than they could agree not to.

She had stopped going to the deep hall after that. Not in protest—protest was an argument and she had given up arguments. She had stopped because she had finally understood what the hall was for, and it was not for the criers. It was for laying down choices the Concord could afford to live on top of, and the choice the criers needed was one that cost more than the Concord had yet decided it was willing to pay, and no honest weighing would ever arrive at it in time, because honesty was the slow way and the loss had found the fast one.

◆

Iren came to the hide near midday, which he never did, and she knew before he had climbed the last of the bank what it meant that he had come, because Iren did not leave the work-hall in daylight unless he had read something he could not sit with alone, and she felt the small mean flare of hope that it might be the criers and put it down at once, because it was never the criers.

He stood in the low doorway of the hide with the marsh-light behind him and held his color the way he always held it, that famous discipline of his that the others read as coldness and that she had learned over their whole life together to read as the opposite, as grief packed down so hard it had become load-bearing, a man holding his throat the way you hold a thing that would spill. She had loved him first for exactly this and resented it now for exactly this, that he could stand in the doorway of a dying marsh and not let his color go, and she had never once doubted that the not-letting cost him more than her letting cost her.

"You opened a bird," he said. He had seen the cold-room, then, or smelled it on her. "What was in it."

"Nothing was in it," she said. "That is what was in it." She did not soften it. She had stopped softening things for him; softening was a kind of lie and they had never lied, it was the one wealth they had, the thing their whole people had once had before they built the thing in the deep hall that had no throat. "The bottom of the marsh is empty all the way down. The criers are starving in a full marsh. I have the reach drawn for nine seasons. I can show you the line."

"I can read the line," he said, and she heard it and her crest came up despite her, because *I can read a system* was the oldest fence between them, the place where his gift became a wall: he could read the line and the line did not reach him, the line was a slope on a wall, and the bird stiff on the cold-stone with its keel like a blade was a thing he had not let himself go and look at, and she did not think it was cowardice. She thought it was worse than cowardice. She thought that if he looked at the bird the way she had looked at it he would not be able to keep building the thing he was building, and that some part of him had decided, honestly, in the only language he had, that the building mattered more than the looking, and that he was probably right, and that she could not forgive him for being probably right.

"You did not come to read my line," she said.

He was quiet. His color did not move, and that was the answer, that the thing he had read in the hall was past the place where even she was allowed, a thing of the Reckoning and not of the marsh, and she understood that there were now rooms in him she was not let into, that the work had grown a deep hall of its own inside him with a door she could read the shape of and not open, and that frightened her more than the empty crop had, because she had spent her life learning to read throats and here was the one throat she had been given to read for the whole of her life going quiet in front of her, holding something down, and she could not tell what, and for the first time it occurred to her that this was perhaps what it was to talk to the thing in the deep hall, this exact helplessness, and that he had been living in it for thirty years and had built her, at last, a small version of it to stand in.

"The clutch is well," he said instead, which was not an answer and was the only true thing he had to give her, and she let it land, because it was true and it was theirs and there were four of them and they were warm. "I counted them this morning."

"I know you did." She came to the doorway and stood with him, not touching, the two of them looking out at the marsh and the nine—the ten—birds on the sixty-one stakes, and she let her color go grey in the open the way she always did and he held his the way he always did, and the gap between those two honesties was their whole marriage and she would not have traded it. "Iren. If the marsh goes—" She stopped. She had not meant to say it. She made herself say it, because the journal did not soften and neither, today, would she. "If the marsh goes, it goes slowly, and we will be able to tell ourselves the whole way down that the next season will be better. That is the thing I cannot bear. Not that it ends. That it ends so slowly we agree to it. I do not want the clutch to hatch into a world we have agreed to."

His color did not move and his crest came down, slowly, the whole flare of it folding flat against his nape the way it did when he had heard a thing all the way to the bottom, and he did not say *bring it to the Reckoning,* which was the only mercy he gave her that day and was a large one, because four seasons ago he would have said it and they both knew now what the words were worth.

"No," he said. Only that. And then, because he was honest even when honesty was the cruelest thing in the hide: "I do not know how to build a thing that makes us decide faster than the loss without building a thing that decides for us." He said it quietly, almost to the marsh, and she did not understand all of it—she would understand it later, too late, the way the rock understands everything—but she understood the grey he was not showing, the thing held down so hard it had gone to stone, and she understood that whatever he had read in the hall that morning lived in the same country as that sentence, and that he had come all the way out to the hide in daylight not to read her line but to be, for an hour, beside the one body in the world he was still allowed to fail to read.

She put the back of one hand against his nape. He let her. They stood.

*(We do not know that she said this. We have a habit—the journal, which we infer from a discipline too consistent to be anything but a life's practice—and we have a marsh that did die the slow way, the bottom up, before the fast way came; and we have given her the words a person keeps such a record in order not to say, and let her say them once, to him, because we cannot prove she did not, and the alternative is to leave her alone in the hide with the count.)*

◆

He went back to the work-hall before the light turned, because the work did not wait, and she stayed, because the marsh did not either.

She worked the cold-room through the long sweet afternoon, and near the turn of the tide a fisher's child brought her a crier still living, found down in the reeds, unable to rise. It was a young one, this season's, fledged late and never fed enough, and she took it from the child's hands and felt the keel under the down, the blade of it, the starving in the middle of the full marsh, and she carried it up into the last of the river-light at the hide's seaward rail where she could see the empty stakes.

It did not struggle. That was the worst of it, that it had not the reserve left to struggle; it lay in the cradle of her hands with its long throat unfolded across her wrist and its tetracone eyes open on the marsh, and it was looking at the water—she would write this down, exactly this, because it was true and because no one would read it and because she had sworn she would not let the criers go down in the dark—it was looking at the water that was full and green to the margin and held nothing it could eat, and its color, what little color a starving bird could still spend, went toward the marsh in the small involuntary signal that meant, in their own bodies and in this one, *here, this, the place,* the homing flush, the one a crier gave when it came down at evening onto its own reach. It gave it to the empty marsh. It could not have known the marsh was empty. It only knew it was home.

She held it while it stopped, which did not take long, and she did not let her color go, because she had begun to ration the grey and would need it, and she hated that she could do this now, hold a dying thing with a dry throat, and she did it.

Then she laid the bird down on the rail and opened the journal and wrote it: the reach, the tide, the wet warm air, *not the dry.* One young crier, this season's, recovered living from the reeds, dead of starvation in full water at the turn of the tide. The homing flush, given to an empty marsh. She wrote the whole of it in the hand that did not editorialize, and she did not write that she had loved it, because the record did not say such things and there was no one to say it to, and she closed the journal and sat in the last of the light with the small cold body on the rail beside her and counted, out of an old habit deeper than her work, the bare stakes—fifty-two, the bird on the rail made it fifty-three—against the day she already knew was coming and would not let herself know she knew, the day she would not be there to count them, the day there would be no one, and she kept the count anyway, because it was the only fidelity she had, and she would not let it happen in the dark.
