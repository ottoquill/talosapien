# Tanis

She had dug the deathbed for eleven years before she understood whose it was, and the understanding did not come in the field, in the cold bright Dakota wind with the surge-bed open under her hands. It came in the night, in the field-lab trailer, with the block on the table and the new framework in her head like a light she could not switch off, and it came the way the worst things come, not as a discovery but as a recognition, the click of a face you have looked at a thousand times resolving, finally, into someone you know.

She had named the place years ago, the way you name a dig, after a word, and the word had outlived its reason. Tanis: a drowned city in an old story, swallowed and preserved. She had meant the fish. The deathbed at Tanis was the most extraordinary preservation of its kind on Earth, a single afternoon caught entire — the impact had thrown a wall of water across the dying inland sea, a surge driven by the shock of a stone falling half a world away, and the wall had rolled over the river delta in the slow hours after the sky burned and sealed everything beneath it so fast and so complete that the creatures were buried still breathing the falling sky, microtektites in their gills, the exact afternoon of the end pressed flat and kept. She had built her name on reading that afternoon. She had loved it the way you love a thing that lets you stand close to enormity and call it work.

She had not let herself think the word *city*.

That was the discipline, and the discipline had held for eleven years, because there was no reason to think it, because the bones in the surge-bed were the bones the surge-bed should hold — fish, a few small ornithischians, the turtles, the churned delta fauna of a Cretaceous river mouth caught in a bad afternoon. And among them, in the third season, a specimen she had catalogued and described and published, a small theropod, a maniraptoran, beautifully articulated, *Troodontid indet.*, remarkable mostly for the wear on its manual phalanges and a set of cut-marks on a nearby long-bone that she had argued, carefully, in a paper no one disputed, were consistent with tool use of a kind already known in the clade. A clever little dinosaur that made and used simple tools. It was a good paper. It was true. It was the smallest possible true thing to say about what was on the table, and she had said it because it was the largest thing the evidence, read in the only framework she had then, would bear.

She had given it a number. And then, two years ago, in the margin of the second logbook, in the small private hand she used for the things that were not yet allowed to be science, she had given it, against every rule she had, a name. She had not chosen the name for a reason she could defend. She had been reading the transuranics, the fission-daughters, the arithmetic of a fire no planet lights on its own, and she had looked at the catalogue photograph of the worn little hands and written, underneath, a name she took from the metal in the clay, the element that first told her a fire had burned here — a coroner's name, a name from the cause of death — and told herself she would erase it.

She had not erased it. And now, in the trailer, with the hull turning in the Belt and the sub-Boundary bed confirmed and the whole impossible careful sentence assembled at last, she sat with the catalogue photograph and the margin name and understood what she had done, which was that she had named the architect.

Not a clever little dinosaur that used tools.

The worn phalanges were the hands of someone who had worked for a living, for a lifetime, at something fine. The framework she had spent a year fighting the whole world to assemble said — said quietly, said in the chemistry, said in a hull a man named Roan Iyer had put his glove against — that the makers of the fire were here, in this delta, that this was a place of theirs, a great place, that the surge had not rolled over a river mouth full of animals but over a river mouth full of *them*, going about an ordinary afternoon, and that the specimen on her table, the small worn careful hands, was not a curiosity preserved among the fish. It was a person preserved among the drowned of his own city. She had been digging, for eleven years, in someone's home.

She put the catalogue photograph down and she went out into the cold and stood at the open surge-bed in the dark with her headlamp off because she could not, just then, bear to look at it lit, and she let herself, at last, after eleven years and a year of war and a marriage and a career she had spent like money on a thing she could not prove, think the word.

City.

The wind came off the badlands the way it always did, indifferent, scouring, and the surge-bed lay open under the stars — the same stars, very nearly; the sky had wheeled but the stones had not all moved; somewhere up there, she knew now, knew the way you know a grave is a grave and not a hill, the dead habitats were still turning — and she stood over a centimeter of the world and understood that everyone she had ever called a specimen had been someone's, had been loved by someone with a body she would never see and a grief she could only infer and a name she would never, ever recover, and that the kindest and least scientific thing she had ever done, the margin name she had told herself to erase, was the truest thing in eleven years of true papers, because it was the only thing she had written that treated him as what he was.

In the morning she went back to the block, and she did the thing she had not done in eleven years, which was to look at the specimen not as a specimen.

She had the associated material, of course; she had catalogued all of it; it was in the description, a line in a table: *associated juvenile elements, NISP 4, taphonomically consistent with the assemblage.* Four. Four sets of juvenile elements, small, very young, found close to the adult in the surge-bed, which she had recorded as four because there were four and had thought nothing of it, because you do not think anything of a number in a table, because a number in a table is the opposite of a person, it is the thing you write so you do not have to write *baby*, so you do not have to write *his*, so you do not have to stand in the trailer at thirty-nine years old with a framework in your head like a light you cannot switch off and understand that the adult with the worn careful hands had been found in the last sealed afternoon of his world with four very young of his own kind pressed close, in the lee of something, against the wall of water, four small ones who had not, the chemistry said, the whole terrible careful sentence said, agreed to any of it.

She counted them. She did not need to count them; the number was in her own table in her own hand; she counted them anyway, the four small associated juvenile elements, with the side of one finger laid along each catalogue drawer, and the counting did not steady her, and she did not know why she had expected it to, and she wrote in the second logbook, under the margin name she had told herself to erase and would not now ever erase, the only thing there was to write, which was not a result, which would never be a result, which she would never publish and never could:

*There were four. He was not alone when it came. Whatever he did or failed to do — and I think he was someone who could have done something, the hands are a maker's hands, the place is a maker's place — he was, at the end, in the lee of a wall, with four of them. I have named him. I know it isn't science. I have spent a year being told that the thing I can see in the rock is not allowed to be a person because the date is wrong, and I have come to think that the date being wrong is the whole of what we cannot forgive, that we would rather he be a clever animal sixty-six million years too early than a person exactly on time, because a person exactly on time is a letter, and we are in it, and there were four of his, and there are how many of ours.*

She closed the logbook. Téo would be up soon; she could hear the generator; you could not let a graduate student catch you naming the dead. She sat for a moment with her hand flat on the cover of the book in the cold trailer, the way you put a hand on a thing you are keeping, and she did the arithmetic she had been not-doing for a year, the only arithmetic that had ever mattered, which was not the half-lives and not the isotope ratios and not the base rates Mauss kept fairly raising, but the other one, the one under all of them: that she had met him. That across a centimeter of clay and sixty-six million years and every law of the unrecoverable, by the one road the record left open, which was to refuse to leave him a number, she had reached down into the dark of the surge-bed and given a drowned maker back the smallest possible piece of what the surge took, which was a name, and a sentence, and someone to say *there were four* over him in the only direction the saying could go, which was forward, which was toward the living, which was toward the hearing in three days where she would stand up and try to make a room full of reasonable people understand that the dead had written us a letter and that the postmark was our own near future and that there was still, for now, by some margin no one could measure, time.

She did not know if the hands had done it. She would never know. The rock would not say; the rock never said; intent did not fossilize; the maker's hands were equally the hands of a man who tried to push the stone aside and the hands of a man who let it fall and the hands of a man who, looking at what he and his had built, reached out and closed the door on purpose so no one could ever open it again and find the fire still in the drawer. All three were the same worn phalanges. All three drowned in the same lee of the same wall with the same four pressed close. She had wanted, for eleven years and especially for the last terrible year, to be told which one he was. She understood now that she would not be told, that the not-being-told was the finished shape of it, that this was exactly the thing she was going to have to walk into the hearing and ask a room to act on anyway: a warning with no proof and a messenger with no certainty and a maker with no recoverable heart, and the four of his, and the how-many of ours, and the door still open, for now, by a margin no one could measure.

She turned off the lamp over the block. She would go in to the hearing with the chemistry and the hull and the bed below the Line, and she would lose, or she would win, and the book would not tell her which because she did not yet know, no one knew, it had not happened yet — but she would go in carrying, under the data, unpublishable, the only thing she was finally sure of, which was that there had been four, and that he had not been alone, and that the least scientific act of her life had been the one that got the truth right.

Outside, the generator coughed, and Téo knocked, and the day that had not yet been decided began.
