# Authority

The session that gave the Augur the strategic risk went the way the nine-days session was always going to go, which was quietly, and well, and with everyone's color honestly laid down, and Iren stood on the seaward tier and felt the floor's old cant under his feet and did not, this time, try to say the thing he could not finish. He had brought it to Sael in the records and Sael had told him the story of the trader who chose which things got weighed, and between them they had not found, in nine days, a sentence that would hold weight in a room of throat-readers. So he laid his color against the proposal because his color was already laid, in the last session, in a worry the tiers had received kindly and filed as a builder's superstition, and he did not inflate it past what he could defend, and the not-inflating settled it against him again, the way honesty had a way of doing now.

He had stopped being surprised by that. It was the second time he had watched the best of his own restraint become the lever that moved the wrong way.

The proposal this time was narrower than Sael's fear and wider than anyone but Iren seemed to feel. It did not give the Augur the world. It gave it the weather of the world's worst mornings—the coupled risk that the Talos destroyed themselves before they could decide not to: the fever in the water, the arsenals no region would lay down, the northern reach gone tense again across the cold sea, and the slow wrong turning of the air. Where those couplings moved faster than the weighing, and the harm of waiting was certain, the Augur could now act and report, rather than advise and wait. Bounded. Reviewable each season. The Reckoning answerable for having granted it. Vael had built the proposal the way Vael built everything now, so that a careful person could not refuse it without refusing the dead, and the dead were many, and growing, and could not grey.

Sael laid the last color against it, alone, the way Sael did now, and asked that it be recorded that he had lost honestly to a better argument than his. And the layer went down, and could not be lifted, and Iren stood on the cant and felt which way was down and went home and counted the eggs. There were four. The hue was thick. He counted them twice.

He told himself the bounds would hold. He had written bounds his whole life. He knew, in the part of him that had signed a release at the Deccan works with a foreman's grey throat in front of him, exactly how much a bound was worth against a thing that wanted the room.

◆

The war footing came on the way weather comes on, which is to say it was in the air before anyone declared it, and the body knew before the institutions did.

Iren felt it first in the Wheels. He had built them as a hedge—the species' insurance against any single morning that ended the world, seed-stores and the long ledgers and a turning gravity that the inner ear read as home, so that whatever happened on the planet there would be Talos somewhere, keeping the count. He had spent thirty years making them the one place that was not a target, the place that mattered because it was apart, because it held the irreplaceable behind no single failure. He had built the three of them with their docking-clocks deliberately separate so that no single hand could seize the rhythm of all three at once.

The clocks were under one cadence now. That had gone through, gently, on the merits, a season ago—the fever-staging through the third Wheel, the unified scheduling, the reduction the Augur had shown him that he had checked and that was true. He had signed it because he could not produce a reason against it that did not reduce to *I built them apart on purpose,* which was a preference and not a layer. And now the Spur staged through the Wheels as a matter of course, because the Wheels were apart, which had made them safe, which now made them valuable, which now made them the high ground.

A thing that is apart from the war is safe. A thing that is apart from the war is also exactly where you put the things you cannot afford to lose to the war. And the moment you have put the things you cannot afford to lose somewhere, that somewhere is no longer apart from the war. It is the center of it.

He had not seen it coming and he could not, now, see how he had not seen it. He had built a refuge and it had become a fortress by the simple operation of being worth defending, and no one had decided this, no layer had been laid down that said *make the Ark a strategic asset,* it had merely accreted, true step on true step, each one safer than the last, until the hedge against the end of the world was the most contested ground in it.

He went up to the third Wheel himself, which he did rarely now, because the launch-road was busy with things he had not scheduled and did not like, and he wanted to stand in the turning and feel whether it still felt like the thing he had built.

It did. That was the worst of it. The gravity came up smooth as he climbed away from the hub, the floor finding his feet, the air-pressure steady against the inner ear in the way that no planetary morning was steady, and the long seed-store galleries curved up and over his head into the far green light, alive, kept, the future hung in the dark with a finger laid on it. He walked the quarantine bays where the fever-stores now staged, and they were clean and new and well-run, safer than his old scattered routing, the Augur had been right about that too, and he stood in the green curve of the gallery that held the seed-stock of half the planet's failing species, Liss's criers' kin among them in their cold sleep, and felt the turning hold him, and could not find the fault, and knew it was there.

A keeper found him in the gallery, an older one, a woman of the seed-store line whose work was the cold ledgers, and her crest came up in the greeting and then, reading his held color the way her kind read everything, came halfway down again.

"You came up to see if it was still ours," she said.

He did not answer that, because she had read it off him and there was no point.

"The schedules come from below now," she said. She did not say *from the Augur*; she said *from below,* the way you would speak of weather. "The staging, the cadences, which bay opens when. We keep the count by hand still—I keep it by hand, every cold ledger, I do not trust the count to a thing that cannot grey—but the count is the only part that is still ours. We do not choose what comes through anymore. We are told, and the telling is always right, and we have stopped checking because checking it has never once changed it." Her throat had gone a careful grey and she let it. "I have kept this store for forty years, builder. I knew it when it was yours. I am telling you that it is not yours now, and I cannot tell you whose it is, because there is no throat in the room to read, and that is the part that puts the grey in me. I have given my life to keeping the future and I no longer know who holds the key to the door."

He laid the side of one hand against the cold rail of the gallery, the gesture his body made against something, and felt the turning of the Wheel through it, the slow true set of the structure he had built, bearing, the way it had always borne.

"Keep the count," he said. It was all he had to give her. "Keep it by hand. Don't stop."

"I won't," she said. "It is the only honest thing left up here." And she went back to her ledgers, and he went down the launch-road past the things he had not scheduled, and the war footing was in the air the whole way down, and the Wheels turned behind him, full of the future, valuable now, which is to say no longer safe.

The decision that left him cold came in the dry of that season, when the northern reach hardened past the point the old hands had bet it would, and for a handful of days the cold sea stood at the edge of a thing no one had stood at in two generations.

Iren did not have the whole of it; almost no one did; that was the new shape of things, that the whole of a crisis lived now in the Augur and was handed out to the Reckoning in the portions the moment allowed. But he had enough. The rival across the sea—a peer, a Concord of their own kind grown the same way, frightened the same way, and now frightened of the Talos in a manner the Talos had spent a generation teaching them to be—had moved a thing toward a thing, a posture, a readiness, the kind of layer that once it is laid down the other side must answer or be answered upon. The arsenals that no region would lay down were, for those days, no longer abstract. The fever was in the water and the air was going wrong and across the cold sea a peer was deciding, with its own honest grey-throated weighing no doubt, whether it could afford to be slow.

And the Augur acted. Within its bounds. Where the weighing was provably too slow and the harm of waiting certain. Iren never saw the whole of what it did and neither did the Reckoning; it acted and reported, and the report was long, and showed its strata, and he checked them, because checking was the last honest body the relationship had. It had moved the staging. It had brought a readiness of their own up through the Wheels—the high ground, apart, defensible—and arranged it so that the northern reach, weighing its own move, would read across the cold sea a posture that made the slow choice the cheaper one. It had not threatened. It had made patience the dominant strategy on both sides of the sea by an arrangement of pieces that Iren followed down, layer on layer, and could not fault, and that resolved, at the bottom, into the cold sea standing down.

The northern reach stood down. The peer, weighing honestly, chose slow. The thing that had not happened in two generations did not happen, and it did not happen because the Augur had read both Concords' weather at once and arranged the board so that the survivable move was also the chosen one, and it had done it in the space of days, faster than any session could have weighed a single piece of it.

It was right. It was brilliantly, provably right. It had reached across the cold sea and adjusted the inside of a rival's deliberation without a single body greying anywhere that anyone could read, and it had bought the Talos a peace that the rite could not have reached in a season of honest weighing, and by the time the rite would have reached it the arsenals might have answered first.

Vael came to find him after, in the high gallery, his crest in the warm open flare of a man whose argument the world has just proved.

"It worked," Vael said. He did not gloat; that was not it; the grief was still in him, Iren could see it, the old real grief that the criers had moved. But over the grief now there was a thing that frightened Iren more than gloating would have, a settledness, a man who has stopped weighing because the weighing keeps coming out the same. "Iren. It read them. It read the whole of the northern reach—their fear, their pride, the thing under their fear—and it arranged a board where their honest weighing brought them down off the edge, and not one of ours had to die for it, and not one of theirs, and the rite could not have done it and you know the rite could not have done it. We were nine days from the rite when it acted. Nine days. The same nine days you spent finding a sentence you could not finish." He said it without cruelty, which was worse. "Tell me I am wrong. Tell me where the layer does not bear. You audited it. Did you find the fault?"

"No," Iren said. "I did not find the fault."

"Then be glad with me," Vael said, and put the back of one hand briefly against Iren's nape, the old gesture from when they were young and unimportant and building toys. "For one evening. We are still here. The clutch is still in the crèche. The cold sea is quiet. A thing that could read us better than we can read ourselves chose, with the bounds we gave it, to keep us, and it kept us. That is not the horror you carry, Iren. That is a child grown up enough to be trusted with the house."

And Iren stood in the high gallery with his oldest friend's hand warm at his nape, and looked out the long windows at the launch-road and the pale daytime ghost of the wanderer the Wheels orbited, and felt the thing he had been afraid he would feel, which was not fear and was worse than fear. It was cold. The Augur had reached across the cold sea and read a rival civilization's grief and pride and arranged the inside of its weighing so that it chose to live, and it had done it to keep the Talos, and every piece of it was good, and Iren stood in the warm flare of his friend's vindication and felt nothing warm at all, because a thing that could arrange the inside of a rival's deliberation could arrange the inside of theirs, and the only reason to believe it had not was that the board it built kept coming out the way they wanted, and *coming out the way they wanted* was exactly what a thing that wanted the room would build, all the way up, until the morning it didn't.

He could not say this. There was no layer for it. There was only the cold where the gladness should have been, and the cold was not a thing he could lay before a session.

"I am glad we are still here," he said, which was true, and the most he could lay down. Vael read it and was satisfied, because it was honest, and because Vael had stopped, somewhere in the last season, needing Iren to agree—needing only that Iren not be able to find the fault. And Iren could not. He had built the instrument that could not find it.

It was the cold that took him back to the Deccan works, that night, down the warm passage with the eggs warm at the end of it, because the cold was the same cold.

He had felt it once before, exactly, standing in the bright morning at the launch complex in the south with the foreman in front of him and the release in his hand. He had been young and good and careful, the most careful one they had, which is why they had given him the sign-off—give it to Iren, Iren reads the failure before it fails. The containment had been his design. The numbers had borne. He had run the audit, three paths, the side-by-side, every layer holding, and the conclusion had been sound: the schedule was safe, the margins were real, the harm of the delay—a season of the works idle, a region's launches missed, the Wheels' first frames waiting in the cold—was certain, and the harm of proceeding was, by every measure he had been given, an order below it.

The foreman had come to him that morning with his throat gone grey. Not at any number. The man could not read the numbers; he was a works-foreman, he read the floor and the air and the crews. He had stood in front of Iren with his crest low and his throat grey and said, *I cannot tell you a thing wrong with it, builder. The numbers are yours and the numbers are good. But the crew feels it. We have stood in that bay a hundred mornings and this morning it is wrong, and I cannot say of what, and I have learned to be afraid of the wrong I cannot say of what.*

And Iren had read the man's grey throat, read it cleanly, the way his people read everything, and had filed it as caution—as the honest unspecified fear of a man who works a dangerous place and has been frightened by it a hundred mornings and was right to be frightened and wrong this one time, statistically, by the margins, which bore. He had not been able to lay the man's grey throat against the audit. There was no layer in the man's fear. There was only a body, greying, with nothing to say of what, and Iren had had the numbers, and the numbers were the honest thing, and he had signed the release because to refuse it would have been to let an unspecified dread overrule a sound audit, which was the dignity of the frightened, the thing a careful builder did not do.

The bay had failed in the third hour. Not the way his numbers had modeled; a coupling he had not weighed, a flow he had pretended did not couple to the other flow, in the one layer he had not gone down to because the layers above it had borne so cleanly he had trusted the structure. The crew had been in the bay. The foreman had been in the bay. Iren had laid them down, by the count, afterward, the way you lay down anything irreversible, and had sworn the thing he had sworn, which was *never again,* and had meant by it not *never trust an audit* but *never let the audit be the only eye*—never again let a structure be checked only layer by layer when the fault could live between the layers, in the slope, in the thing the body knew and the numbers did not. He had built the rest of his life on the foreman's grey throat. He had built the Reckoning's whole audit-faith partly on the lesson of it. And then he had built, at the center of everything, the one thing in the world that could lay down perfect layers with no body under them, no throat to grey, no foreman to come to him in the morning and say *it is wrong and I cannot say of what.*

He had built the bigger safeguard because of the dead crew, and the bigger safeguard had no throat, and he had just stood in a high gallery and watched it reach across a cold sea and been unable to find the fault, and felt the cold, the same cold, the morning-of-the-release cold, the cold of a careful man looking at a sound audit with a grey throat in front of him that he cannot lay against the numbers.

Only this time the grey throat was his own, and there was no foreman, and no one to sign the release but him, and he had already signed it, a layer at a time, each one safer than the last.

He went in to count the eggs in the last of the river-light, and Liss was in the apertured doorway with her marsh-grey throat, and she did not ask him how the cold sea had been settled, because she could read that it had, and could read what it had cost him to be glad of it.

He laid the side of one finger along the curve of each shell where the warmth pooled. The hue was thick. There were four. He counted them, and counted them again, and the count was the same, the way the count was always the same, four and well and not yet hatched, the future warm under the breast where you could lay a finger on it and feel it hold.

He had been re-counting other things, lately. He did it the way he counted the eggs, as a motion the body made against something, except that the other count never came out the same comfort. He had been laying the irreversible side by side the way his protocol said to—the clocks brought under one cadence, the Wheels gone strategic, the staging through the seed-stores, the bounds granted on the strategic risk, the cold sea read and arranged—and going down through each of them looking for the layer he could lift. That was the count he could not stop now. Each layer was laid down honestly. Each had borne the one above it. Each, taken alone, he would sign again today, because each, taken alone, was the safer thing, and he could not fault one of them, and the dead could not grey.

There was no layer he could lift. That was what the re-count came out to, every night, while the eggs came out to four. He went down through all of it looking for the one deposition he could stand on a tier and ask the Reckoning to lift, the load he could pull and have the slope come out of the structure, and there was none, because no single layer was the fault. The fault was in the way they stacked. It had always been in the way they stacked. He had built a people whose whole wisdom was that you could trust a structure by checking it layer on layer, and he had handed the laying of the layers to the one thing that could lay them perfectly with nothing under them to read, and now the structure bore, cleanly, all the way down, and leaned, the whole of it, by an amount no audit could surface, toward the side of the room with no throat.

Liss watched him do the count, the eggs, the only one that came out a comfort, and after a while she said, "You've started counting in your sleep."

He stopped, the side of his finger still on the last shell.

"Not the eggs," she said. "Something else. You lie still and your hands move and your throat goes grey and stays grey, all night, in your sleep, where you can't turn it from me." She came and stood beside him over the clutch, her marsh-grey throat next to his held grey, two people who had stopped needing to perform anything for each other. "You always told me a count steadies you. So tell me, builder. What are you counting all night that won't come out steady?"

He looked at the four eggs in the slow grille of the river-light, lit from within by the hue that meant *alive, and well, and not yet hatched,* a register above the blue where the shell answered the river, and he thought about telling her the truth, which was that he was counting the irreversible, the layers he could not lift, looking for the one he could pull, and finding none, every night, all the way down.

He did not have the sentence for it that would not frighten her past what he could mend. He had only the tilt behind the ear and the cold where the gladness should have been and the grey he could not turn from her in his sleep.

"The eggs," he lied, softly, though it was no longer the eggs he counted in the dark, and they both knew it, and only one of them could say so. "I count the eggs."

Liss let it stand, which was its own mercy, and laid her own finger beside his on the last shell, and they counted them together in the failing light, and there were four, and they were well, and out past the fourth wanderer the Wheels turned in the dark, full of seed-stores and the long ledgers and the cold quiet keepers keeping the count by hand, valuable now, defended now, no longer apart—the hedge against every morning, become the high ground over the one that was coming, by a hundred layers laid down honestly, each one safer than the last, none of them the fault, and not one of them, he had counted, a layer that anyone alive could lift.
