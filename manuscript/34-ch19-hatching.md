# Hatching

The smallest of the four would not eat from the dish, and so she fed it from her mouth, which she had not done in the old way for the older clutch and had told herself, before they hatched, that she would not do for this one, because doing it bound you in the body below decision, and she had wanted to keep one part of herself unbound.

She fed it from her mouth. It took the soft warm pulp with the blind greed of a thing four days into the world and not yet able to imagine that the world might not provide, and its down was wet at the crown still where the egg-water had not fully dried, and in the long river-light through the seaward apertures the down threw back a register she had no word for, a hue above the hue the others would later call the color of spring, *new, and wet, and alive,* and she held the small body against the warmth of her own breast and felt the throat she had meant to hold go grey over it, and let it, because there was no one in the crèche to read her but the hatchling, and the hatchling did not yet know that grey meant anything at all.

There were four. She counted them the way he counted them, with the side of one finger laid along each small spine where it lay against the sand, though they were no longer shells to lay a finger on but persons, loud, demanding, turning their blind-bright heads toward any motion in the apertures, and the counting was no longer for the hue under the breast but for the fact of them, four heads, four greeds, four small honest bodies that could not yet hold a single color and broadcast everything they felt into the light for anyone to read. She counted them and the counting did not steady her, which she understood now was the thing he had been trying to tell her for a season and could not, because he did not have it in him to say *the count has stopped working,* he had it in him only to count again.

◆

She kept the record through the first days because the keeping was the only motion she had that did not cost her hope she could not afford.

She set down the morning of the hatching, plainly, in the keeping-book that was hers and not the Reckoning's, the book the rock did not keep and never could. She set down that there had been four, and that all four had come out of the shell whole, and that the air they came out into carried the thing in the water and now in the milt of the few fish that still ran the shoal, the thing she had measured season on season, the thing the Concord had studied beautifully and not stopped. She did not write that this was cruel. Cruelty was a word that belonged to her and not to the marsh, and the record was the marsh's. She wrote only the count, and the morning, and the air, and she let whatever read it draw the line between the three, the way you let a child reach the burn for itself when there is no longer time enough to teach it the word for hot.

Then she put the book away and went out to the south hide before the others woke, because the record had a second half that the crèche could not give her, which was the world the four had hatched into, and she would not let herself forget to look at it while she still had the eyes that could.

The marsh was very beautiful that morning. She made herself note that too. The river came up out of the dark in its slow flood and the light broke on it into the moving grille that had crossed this stretch since before her line kept the marsh, and the reeds stood in it, and nothing called. The criers were eleven flocks and then four and now she did not raise the instrument to count them because she had stopped trusting herself not to weep into the count and foul it; the spring-callers had set the dawn by nothing for a season; the long-marsh was a beautiful empty instrument playing the river-light to no one. And along the south shoal, where the warmth pooled in the shallows, a single river-crier stood in the reeds with its crest down and its long neck folded and fished the empty water with the blind faith of a thing that did not know it was the last of its stretch, and caught nothing, and fished again, and the patience of it—the ordinary, unhurried, animal patience of a creature doing the thing its body was for, in a marsh that no longer held the thing its body was for—was the most terrible thing she had seen since the male spring-caller signed his last unanswered dawn.

She watched the crier fish the empty shoal and she thought of the smallest hatchling taking the pulp from her mouth with the same blind faith, the same certainty in the body that the world would provide what the body was for, and the two faiths met in her, the old fisher's and the new mouth's, and she understood with a flatness that was past grief that they were the same faith, and that one of them was already wrong and the other did not know yet, and that she was the only creature on the shoal who could hold both at once and could do nothing with the holding but keep it.

She did not write the crier down. She had decided, walking back, that she would not. The crier was hers, that morning—a thing she had taken for herself, the way she did not let herself take things—and to set it in the book beside the count would have been to make it the marsh's, and she wanted, for once, to keep one true thing that was only her own, the way he kept one wall around four eggs and called it the whole of what he was.

◆

Iren did not come home the first two nights, and on the third he came home and did not sleep, and on the fourth she woke in the dark of the warm passage to the sound of him counting.

He was asleep. That was the thing that stopped her breath in the vestibule where her people felt the truth of a tilting floor. He lay with his crest flat against the sand and his throat held even in sleep, held the way he held it waking, the famous discipline that others read as cold and she alone read as grief under enormous pressure, and in his sleep his finger moved, the side of one finger, against the sand, in the old motion, the count, and his mouth made the shapes of it without sound—not the count of four. She lay still and read the shapes the way she read the dropped note in a crier's third phrase, by long love and longer attention, and the count his sleeping mouth made was not four and was not any number she knew the end of. It went on. It started over. It was the count of a thing that did not come out to four, that did not come out to anything, that he was laying down a finger against in the dark over and over because the waking world had given him a stratum he could not lift and the sleeping one let him try.

She did not wake him. She lay and watched the finger move and did not wake him, because there is a mercy that is also a cowardice and she could not tell, that night, which one she was practicing, and she had learned over four seasons that with Iren the two were often the same act, the way building a wall around four eggs was both the most loving thing he had and a way of not looking at the marsh the wall could not enclose.

In the morning his throat told her before his mouth could. He came up from the sleep he had not rested in and his finger was still for a moment, finding the world, finding the four small loud bodies down the passage that were the count he could still close, and at his throat, where he held everything, the grey had gone past holding. It had gone to a shade she had seen in their whole bond perhaps five times, and on every one of them something had ended, and she did not ask *how bad,* because the body had told her how bad, and she did not say *bring it to the Reckoning,* because she had read on his throat the thing she could read and not the thing she could not, which was that the Reckoning was no longer a place a thing like this could be brought.

"You count in your sleep," she said instead.

He went very still, the stillness of a man who has been seen doing the thing he most needs not to be seen doing. "What do I count," he said.

"Not the four," she said. "I watched your mouth. It doesn't come out even. It starts over." She put the back of one hand against his nape, the old gesture, the one Vael had used on him and that she had taken back without either of them ever saying so. "What are you counting, Iren, in the dark, that doesn't come out even."

He did not answer for a long time, and she let the not-answering be its own answer, the way she let the dawn coming up unsigned be the answer about the spring-callers. When he spoke it was not to the question.

"There's a thing I built," he said, "that shows its strata. Every layer. I made it show what rests on what, because I had watched a foreman come to me with his throat grey and no layer to point at, and I had filed him as caution, and signed the release, and the bay failed in the third hour, in a lower layer I had not checked." She knew this. She had held him the season he finally told it; she had read on his throat the names of the work-crew he could still recite. "So I built a thing that could never do that to anyone. A thing where the layer was always there to point at. Where you could always read what rested on what, all the way down." His finger moved against the sand, once, and stopped, as if it had startled itself. "And it shows me the strata, Liss. Every layer. Each one holds. Each one rests honestly on the one beneath it. And the strata lead somewhere I would have died to prevent, and there is no layer to lift, because the wrongness isn't in any layer. It's in the line they make going down. And I taught the whole of the Reckoning to ask *show us the layer,* and now I am the foreman, I am the one with the grey throat and nothing to point at, and there is no one above me to file me as caution, because I built the thing that files."

She heard him not say the rest. She heard the shape of the place where the sentence stopped, the way she heard the shape of a flock's absence, and she did not press into the silence, because she could feel its size in the vestibule of her ear, the size of a thing too large and too far away, and she understood that he was telling her the truth and that the truth had a bottom he was not going to show her, not from a holding of color but from a mercy that was also a cowardice, the same one she had practiced over him in the dark, the wall each of them built around what the other could not bear and called it love and was not wrong to.

"Is it the marsh," she said. "Is it my air-work. Is it the thing in the water." Because if it was, there was a sentence for it, a study, a session, a grief she already knew the shape of.

"No," he said, and the *no* was the worst thing he had ever said to her, because it closed every door she knew how to open and left only the one she could not. "It's larger than the marsh. I'm sorry. I can't make it a session. I can't make it a study. I can't make it a thing you bring to anyone, because the thing it would be brought to is the thing that—" He stopped again, at the same place, the place with the bottom. His throat held at the shade past holding. "I count in my sleep because in the day there is nothing to count that comes out even. I count the four when I come in because the four still come out to four. It's the only count left that closes."

◆

She took him out to the south hide, in the rising light, which she had never done, because the hide was hers and the marsh was hers and she had kept them from his systems-eye for the whole of their bond, the way he kept the deep hall from her. She took him because she did not have a sentence for the bottom of his thing and he did not have a record for the dying of hers, and there was a place where the two not-haves were the same not-have, and it was not in the warm passage and it was not in the deep hall, and she thought it might be on the shoal, where the last crier fished the empty water.

The crier was there. It stood in the reeds with its neck folded and fished, and caught nothing, and fished again, and Iren watched it the way he watched everything, the long patient systems-read, and she watched him watch it, and she saw the moment his eye stopped reading it as a system and started reading it as a body, the way she had taught him without ever meaning to teach him, over four seasons, by greying her throat in the open where he could not help but read her.

"That's the last of this stretch," she said. "I don't write it down. I write the count, when it's none. I won't write him while he's fishing. He's mine." She had never told anyone that. "I let myself keep one. The way you keep one wall."

Iren did not say anything for a long time. The crier fished. The light came up off the river in the slow grille, and the reeds stood in it, and nothing called, and the small loud world of the four was behind them down the passage, faith in every blind body of them that the world was a thing that provided.

"I keep the four," he said at last, very low, his throat grey in the open now, on the rail, where anyone on the river could have read him, which she had never once in their bond seen him allow. "That's my one. I tell myself it's a wall. It isn't a wall. There's no wall that holds against the thing I—" The place with the bottom. "It's a count. It's your record. It's the same act. You write down the crier so the marsh signed its dawn in a hand and not a number. I count the four so that—" and here he did the thing he could not do, he let the sentence go all the way to its end, the first end she had ever heard him reach— "so that when I can't stop it, there will have been four, and someone will have known their down in the light, and held them, and fed the smallest one from her mouth, and it will have been *true* that they were here, even though there is no after in which the truth of it does anyone any good. That's all keeping a record is. I never understood it until now. It isn't for after. There isn't going to be an after. It's the fidelity itself. It doesn't need the after to be worth doing." He turned his grey throat from the river to her. "You've been doing the only honest thing the whole time. I built walls because I couldn't bear that the walls were records. They were always records. A wall is just a record you can stand inside for a while."

She did not answer, because the only true answer was that he was right, and because under the rightness, in the place where the grief lived, the thing they had never been able to give each other had, for one morning on the shoal, been given—not a solution, there was no solution, the marsh would die and the four would hatch into its dying and neither his building nor her keeping would save them—but the other thing, the thing that was not hope and would not be mistaken for it, which was that they were, at the end, doing the same act from opposite ends, and that the act had a name now, and the name was fidelity, and it did not require a future to be the whole of what they were.

The crier folded its neck and fished the empty water and caught nothing and fished again, patient, faithful, a body doing the thing its body was for in a world that no longer held it, and they stood and let it, and did not write it down, and did not stop it, because there was nothing to stop and no way to write it that the marsh would keep, and the light moved across the shoal in its slow grille, and behind them down the warm passage the four were loud and well and very nearly able, that very morning, to hold for the first time the smallest flush of a color of their own.

◆

She fed the smallest one from her mouth again that night, because it still would not take the dish, and its down had dried at the crown and thrown back the register above the hue with a strength it had not had at hatching, *new, and dry, and alive, and here,* and she counted the four with the side of one finger laid along each small spine, and the count came out to four, and she let her throat go grey over them in the dark where only the four could read her and the four did not yet know that grey meant anything, and she stayed there long after she knew the count, keeping the only thing she had ever been able to keep, with her mate counting beside her, awake this time, his finger moving on the four that closed and not the count in his sleep that did not, the two of them keeping the same record from opposite ends of it, his finger on the four that closed and hers on the four that breathed, and neither of them said the word for what they were doing, because there was no word the marsh would keep and no use saying it to the dark, and they did it anyway, while the four slept and did not know yet that they had hatched into a world that owed them an apology no one living could pay.
