# Base Rates

The departmental email came in while she was teaching, and she read it standing at the front of an emptying lecture hall with her thumb over the screen the way you cover a wound before you have decided to look at it. The committee thanked her for her *continued contributions*. The committee noted that the Rendel core program's external funding had *not been renewed at the anticipated level*. The committee looked forward to *discussing realignment of resources* at her earliest convenience. There were four signatures, and one of them was Mauss's, and his was the only one she trusted to have meant it kindly.

She did not feel anything she could name. She felt the specific deadness of a thing she had already rehearsed so many times that the real one arrived as a repetition. She put the phone in her bag and gathered the cores of the lecture—she still lectured on the iridium, on the Alvarez father and son and the global spike, the clean story, the only story the room had paid to hear—and she went down the corridor to the lab to find Téo, because there was a conversation she had been postponing for a month and the email had just made it not-postponable.

He was at the bench with the new spherule mounts, and he had the look he got when the work was good, the look she was about to spend.

"They cut the core program," she said.

He set down the forceps. "How much."

"Enough that I can't pay you past the spring."

◆

She had wanted to tell him in a way that gave him an exit. She had planned the sentences on the walk over—*you should think about Hwang's lab, you should think about the Chicxulub group, your transcript is clean and you are not on any of my papers yet and there is still time to not be the student of the woman who claimed the dinosaurs had reactors*—and she had meant to deliver them as a supervisor and not as a drowning person reaching for the nearest warm thing, and she could already feel that she was going to fail at the distinction, because she needed him, and need made a liar of the part of her that knew better.

"You don't have to decide anything today," she said, which was the first lie, because of course he did, the email had decided the calendar for both of them.

"Okay," Téo said. He had gone still in the particular way of someone doing arithmetic he didn't want to be seen doing. He was twenty-six. He had a fellowship application due in November and three letters he needed and two of them were supposed to come from people in this building who had just signed a document with the word *realignment* in it. None of that was on his face. What was on his face was that he was trying to keep it off his face, and she had taught him to read rock and he had learned, somewhere, to read her, and the symmetry of it was unbearable.

"Téo. I'm going to say the thing, and I need you to actually hear it and not be loyal at me. If you stay on this, your name gets attached to the Line. Not the iridium. The other thing. The transuranics, the nitrogen, the—the claim. And the claim is going to follow you. When you apply for things, somebody on the committee is going to type your name and my name will come up next to it and there will be a little flag, an invisible little flag, that says *this one worked on the K-Pg civilization thing,* and you will never see the flag and you will never be told it cost you anything and it will cost you things for twenty years. I know how this works. I am the cautionary tale they tell. I would be doing you real harm to let you walk into that without saying it out loud."

"You're saying I should leave."

"I'm saying you should be allowed to leave. There's a difference and I'm trying very hard to be on the right side of it."

He picked up one of the spherule mounts and turned it under the lamp, not looking at it, just needing his hands to be doing the thing they trusted. "Can I ask you something that's going to sound like I'm on Mauss's side."

"Please. God. Be on Mauss's side. Somebody should be in the room who is."

"What are the base rates," Téo said.

She waited.

"That's his whole thing, right? Not contamination, not diagenesis, those are the retail arguments. The wholesale argument is the base rate. He says: take every scientist in history who was *certain* they'd found something that overturned the consensus, that everyone else was too blinkered to see, that the establishment was suppressing or just too dim to grasp. Take all of them. The cold fusion people. The faces-on-Mars people. The ones who were *sure*. And then take the tiny, tiny fraction of them who turned out to be Alvarez—who were the lonely right one. The ratio is brutal. It's like a thousand to one, ten thousand to one. So if you're a young person and you're standing in front of someone who is *certain* and *unproven* and *fighting the whole field*, the rational thing—the actually rational thing, not the cowardly thing—is to bet they're in the thousand and not the one. Because that's what the numbers say. That's what they say about *anyone* in that position. Including you." He set the mount down. "Including me, if I become you. So tell me why the base rate is wrong here. Not why you're special. Why the rate is wrong."

It was the best thing anyone had said to her in a year. She felt the awful gratitude of the cornered—that he had loved her enough to hit her with the real blade and not the soft one.

"It's not wrong," she said.

He blinked.

"The rate's not wrong, Téo. That's the part nobody who's *on my side* will admit, and it's the part you have to hold or you're just another crank with a graduate student. Mauss is *right* about the base rate. If you sort the entire population of confident-and-unproven-and-against-the-field people, you should bet against almost all of them, and you should bet against me, and a sane person who knew nothing but my position in that population *should* walk out of this lab today. I'm not going to stand here and tell you the prior's stupid. The prior is the accumulated wisdom of every science that ever cleaned a crackpot out of its bloodstream. It is the immune system. It is *supposed* to reject me. If it didn't reject things that look like me, it would believe everything, and a field that believes everything knows nothing." She pulled the lab stool out and sat, because her legs had decided the matter. "So that's the honest floor. I'm starting from there. Down at the bottom, where he's right."

"Then—"

"Then here's where I think the field is making a mistake, and it's not a mistake about *me*, it's a mistake about a category. Sit down, this is the only thing I have." He sat. "The base rate is the right tool for one kind of question. *Is this claim true?* For that question, prior probability is king, and my prior is in the gutter, and it belongs there. But that's not the only question on the table, and the field is acting like it is. They've collapsed two questions into one, and the collapse is invisible, and it's killing us."

"Which two."

"*Is it true,* and *what does it cost to be wrong in each direction.*" She held up one hand, then the other, like a balance, and hated the gesture even as she made it because it was Mauss's gesture, the reasonable man's gesture, and she had caught it off him like a cold. "Take cold fusion. Say you're on that committee in 1989 and someone hands you cold fusion. Base rate says: reject, almost certainly wrong. And what does it cost you to reject it if it turns out you were wrong? You missed out on free energy for a while. Embarrassing. Recoverable. The world keeps making energy the old way for another decade, no one dies of your skepticism. So in that case the base rate isn't just the right answer about the *truth*, it's the right answer about the *action*, because the downside of disbelief is small. Disbelief is cheap there. You can afford it."

"And here it isn't."

"Here," she said, "if I'm in the thousand, you've wasted your twenties and I've burned my career and a couple of grad students got flagged and a foundation spent some money it could've spent on something publishable. That's the whole cost. That's it. It's a *career* cost. It's the cost of being the kind of wrong that lands on a CV." She found she had picked up the spherule mount herself, the little glass bead in resin, the one she didn't trust herself with. "And if I'm in the one—if the reading is right, if there was somebody here before us who fixed nitrogen and split atoms and built something and then was *gone*, gone so completely that sixty-six million years filed them as a chemistry anomaly—then the claim isn't a claim about the past, Téo. It's the only data point we will ever have on the question of whether a civilization like ours *survives the part we are currently in.* One data point. And the data point is a grave. The cost of disbelief there isn't a wasted career. The cost of disbelief is that we ignore the one example we've got of exactly the thing we're walking into, because the example didn't come with a receipt."

He was very quiet now. The lab's chiller cycled, hummed, settled.

"So the base rate's right about the truth and silent about the stakes," he said slowly, working it the way she'd taught him, out loud, testing each joint. "And the field's treating a high-stakes asymmetric bet like it's a low-stakes symmetric one. Like it's cold fusion. Like disbelief is free."

"Disbelief is *never* free," she said. "It just usually looks free, because the cost lands somewhere you're not standing." And then, because honesty was the only currency she had left and she was not going to short him on it, "But hold the other side too. Don't let me off. The asymmetry argument is also exactly what every crank uses. *You can't afford to be wrong about my perpetual motion machine, think of the stakes.* The stakes argument is a lever long enough to move any belief, and a careful person is right to be suspicious of it, because it's how you get smuggled past the evidence. Pascal mugged whole governments with it. So you can't *only* have the stakes. The stakes only matter if the claim is live—if the prior is low but not insane, if there's real evidence even if it's deniable, if a serious person looking at the actual numbers feels the floor move a little. You have to earn the right to invoke the stakes by first having a case. I think I have a case. Mauss thinks I have a yearning that's wearing a case as a costume. And here's the thing I can't get around, the thing that keeps me up: from the inside, those two feel *identical.* There is no test I can run on myself to tell which one I am. None. I've looked."

◆

She had not meant to tell him about the other core. She told him about the other core.

"Ten years ago," she said, "before Tanis made my name, I was second author on a sediment study off the Carolina margin. Pliocene. And I saw a thing in the trace metals that didn't sit right—a little excursion, the kind of thing that's almost certainly nothing, the kind of thing you flag and somebody senior tells you is reworking or a hot particle or a smear, and they tell you in that voice that means *you are early in your career and this is how you stay in it.* And I had a postdoc coming up that depended on the senior person's letter, and I filed it as contamination, and I was probably right. Probably. It was probably nothing. I've told myself it was nothing for ten years." She set the mount down very carefully, in its slot, square. "I don't know that it was nothing. I know that I never checked, because checking was expensive and agreeing was cheap, and I read the warning sign in my own data and called it noise because noise was the answer that kept my career. That's not the only time I've done that, either. I've done it in places that weren't a lab." She did not say Daniel's name. She did not have to; the silence after *weren't a lab* had a shape, and Téo, who was kind, did not reach into it. "So when you ask me why I won't do the sane thing now and write it up as *interesting unresolved anomalies, further work needed* and keep my job—it's because I already know exactly who that woman is. I've been her. She's very sensible. She's right almost every time. And the one time she's wrong, she's wrong about the only thing that ever mattered, and she finds out too late to do anything, and she has to live in the house with it. I would rather be the crank. I have done the math on which one I can survive being, and I would rather be the crank."

Téo looked at the cut cores stacked along the wall, the long photographic logs, the centimeter of clay repeated down the whole array like a held note.

"That's not an argument," he said gently. "That you'd rather. That's a wound."

"No," she agreed. "It's a wound. I'm not pretending it's an argument. I gave you the argument first. The base rate, the asymmetry, the case, the part where you don't let me off. That's the argument and it stands on its own and you should weigh it cold. The wound is just why I can't put it down. Those are different things and you deserve to have them handed to you separately, because you're being asked to decide with the argument, not with my wound. The wound's mine. You don't have to carry the wound."

◆

She told him to take the weekend. She told him to talk to Hwang, who would take him in a heartbeat and never think of the Line again, and who did clean reproducible work on foraminifera that would feed him a career like a smooth road. She told him she would write the strongest letter of her life and not put a single fingerprint of the K-Pg claim on it. She told him she would not think less of him—and she meant it, she found, with a completeness that surprised her, because she had spent the morning building him the exit and somewhere in the building she had stopped resenting that he might use it.

That was the part she hadn't expected. She had braced to need him so badly that she'd be ashamed of how relieved she'd be if he stayed. Instead she stood in the doorway of the lab and discovered she wanted, cleanly, separate from her own survival, for him to walk out and be safe—wanted it the way you want it for the people who are going to be alive after you are not, which is not the same as wanting them near you. She thought: this is what the warning is *for*. Not for me. For the one standing where I won't be. If the whole point of reading the grave is that someone who comes after doesn't end up in one, then the first someone, the nearest one, the one with the most future and the least scar, should get to choose his own road with clear eyes and a clean letter and no debt to my needing him. That was the entire content of the thing she'd been fighting for, reduced to one boy and a fellowship deadline, and she had almost missed it because it was too close to see.

She went home. She did not sleep. She got up at the grey end of the night and took out the logbook, the paper one, and under the day's runs she wrote, in the small private hand: *Told him the base rate is right. Told him the stakes are asymmetric. Told him I'd been the careful woman who filed the alarm as noise. Gave him the door and meant it. The argument is sound. The argument was always sound. Soundness isn't the thing that makes anyone walk through fire. I don't know what is. I've never been able to make it on purpose. I can only ever be the smoke alarm. You can't make someone smell the smoke.* Then she closed it, because the argument was over and there was nothing to do with the rest of the night but wait to learn what a twenty-six-year-old decided about a future neither of them would get to check.

◆

He came in Monday before she did. When she got to the lab the spherule mounts were already out, all of them, not the three she'd flagged—all of them, the whole tray, and beside them in his neat hand a fresh log sheet headed with the date and, under it, a numbered list that began at one and was clearly meant to go past a hundred: a protocol for measuring the internal structure of every glass bead in the box, blind, scored by two readers, the way you do a thing when you want a number that embarrasses your enemies.

He was at the bench. He did not turn around. "I talked to Hwang," he said. "She's great. She offered."

"Good. Téo, that's—that's good."

"I told her not yet." He adjusted the lamp. "I worked the asymmetry over the weekend on my own, without you in the room, because I didn't want to be deciding with your wound. And the argument holds. It holds when you're not looking at me. So." A small sound, then, that she felt rather than heard—the click of something seating, a decision laid down where it would have to bear weight. "If we're going to be cranks, we should be the kind they can't dismiss without doing the work. Twenty readers. Blind. I want the number to be obscene."

She stood in the doorway and looked at the back of his head and the long array of cores and the centimeter repeated down the wall, and she was grateful past speech, and under the gratitude, exactly where she had known it would be, was the shame—that she had built him an honest door and still, somewhere she could not reach, had needed him to refuse it, and that he had refused it, and that she would never now be able to tell whether he'd done it because the argument was sound or because she had failed, in the end, to keep the wound out of the room.

"Twenty," she said. Her voice held. "And then do twenty more."

He almost smiled. She heard, behind it, the patient mechanical breath of the instrument starting its cycle, indifferent to what it found or who it freed or ruined, and she went to put down her bag and pick up the work, and let her face do, for one unwatched second with the boy's back turned, the thing she never let it do where anyone could see.
