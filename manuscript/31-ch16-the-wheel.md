# The Wheel

The board had answered him by the time he came back, and the answer was a kind of silence he could read.

He had expected the message that does not come, the open board going quiet around a filing that the working frontier did not know what to do with, no claim contests, no jeers, no rock-broker's flag, just the assay sitting up there in the pool drinking attention the way the object drank his lamp. What he had not expected was the one private line, routed long, stripped of header, that said only: *Have you been inside.* No question mark. Someone careful had typed it, far down the gradient, and had taken the punctuation off on purpose so it would not look like a thing he could answer with a yes or a no.

He had not been inside. He had chipped a flake off the outside of it in the dark and run from what the flake meant for the better part of a season, and then he had spent money he did not have to come back, and now the *Margin* hung off the long axis of the thing again with the loop ticking on the bad grease, and the question sat on the good screen where the file used to sit, patient in the same way.

He went inside because the alternative was to keep not going inside, and a man could only carry that for so long. He had learned this once already. He suited up by the numbers, checked the loop twice and the rebreather three times because Devi was in the cabin telling him to, and he did not argue with her this time, because she was right and the arguing was over.

◆

He had been treating it as a fragment. That was the thing he understood, going out the lock—that the word he had been using was *fragment*, a broken piece, a chunk off a parent body, and that the word had let him keep the thing small. You could hold a fragment in a glove. A fragment had a worth and a registry line and a shape that ended.

The headlamp found the surface a hundred meters out and the surface did not end.

It curved. He had logged the long axis as too long and the corrugation as not-noise, and he had carried in his head, all season, a picture of a broken thing—a slab, a shard, a hull section tumbling. What the lamp showed him as he came up on the tether was that the curve did not stop where a fragment's curve stopped. It went on. It went out of the cone of the light and he tracked it with the lamp and it kept going, the same regular swell of it, the corrugation running away into the dark in both directions like the staves of something too big to be a thing, and he understood with the part of his body that had flown for thirty years and read scale off a hull at a glance that he was not looking at a piece of an object. He was looking at the inside of a curve whose center was somewhere off in the dark behind him, and the radius of that curve was not meters and was not hundreds of meters.

He stopped on the tether. He let himself hang. He turned the lamp slow along the corrugation and counted the staves into the dark until counting them stopped meaning anything, and the cold thing he had felt in the cabin a season ago came back, not the cabin's cold, the other one, the one the body let in at its own rate.

It was kilometers. The thing was kilometers.

He had ranged it on radar and the radar had told him this and he had not believed the radar, had filed the long axis as an error of his processing, because the number the radar gave was a number that belonged to a moon, to a small captured rock, not to a made thing, and a made thing was salvage and salvage had a size you could tow. He believed it now, with the lamp, with his eye, with the regular swell of the corrugation running off both ways past the reach of the light. Made, and kilometers of it. The two facts sat in his chest the way the other two had sat there—made, and old—not reconciling, just both present, both true, the floor gone out under them.

There was a seam ahead where the corrugation broke into a darker geometry, a recess, an edge of shadow that the lamp could not flatten. He went to it hand over hand and his pulse was loud and he made himself breathe the way you make a panicked man breathe, by counting, and he came up on the recess and it was a way in.

◆

It had been a port once. He knew it the way you know a thing your own trade has taught your hands—not by reading it, by recognizing it: a frame, a sealing surface, a place where something had mated and unmated under pressure, the same problem he solved every time he cycled a lock, solved by someone else, solved differently, solved long ago. The closure that had filled it was gone, blown or rotted or simply opened and never shut, and the dark inside was a different dark than the dark of space, a held dark, a dark with walls.

He went in.

The lamp threw its circle on the inner surface and the inner surface was a floor.

He knew it was a floor the way he had known the port. Not by a sign. By the grammar of it—by the way it ran flat under his boots for a stride and then began, very gently, to rise, to curve up and away ahead of him and behind him and to either side, the whole of it tilting up out of the lamp's reach into a vault he could not see the top of, and his trade-trained eye and his vestibular nothing, his inner ear that had nothing to read in free fall, both reported the same thing in their separate languages: that this was the inside of a wheel, and that the floor was the floor because the wheel had turned, once, and thrown a world's worth of weight outward against it, and held the weight there the way the renderings held it, the way the green cylinders held the rivers running up the walls.

He stood on a ground that had been a ground. He turned the lamp up the curve of the world and let it run, and the floor went up and over, far overhead, past where the beam died, and came down the other side, and was a floor there too, upside down to him, waiting for a weight that was not there to hold it.

The dread came in proper then. Not fear—he knew fear, fear was a fast clean thing, decompression, a loop that failed, a tether that parted, and you fixed it or you died and either way it was over quick. This was the slow one. This was the one that arrived at the rate the body allowed, the understanding edging in, that he was standing inside a place that had been made to be lived in. Not a hull. Not a ship you rode through the dark with your couch and your loop and your one empty seat. A place with a ground and a sky's worth of vault over the ground, a place with the geometry of a morning in it, a place where the weight you felt under your feet was the weight a turning world made for you so that you would feel at home in the dark, so that the body that had not been built for the dark would have a down to stand on and a ground to lie on and a curve overhead to call a sky.

Somebody had built a sky. Out here. Far enough out and long enough ago that the building of it was sixty million years cold, and they had built it because they were coming out here to stay, and they had wanted, when they got here, to stand on something that felt like home.

He had thought, all season, that he had found a thing. He understood now, standing on the ground that had been a ground, that he had found a place. And a place that had been built to be lived in, and was empty, and had been empty a long time, was not a wreck.

It was a tomb. The size of a city.

◆

He did not say it out loud. There was no one to say it to and the recorder caught everything and the habit held even now, even here. But he caught himself wanting to, caught the want rising the way it had risen in the cabin a season ago—*Dev, look at this, Dev, tell me what I'm standing in*—and there was no one in the cockpit and no one on the tether and no one anywhere in the kilometers of curved dark but him, a working pilot a hundred meters and then a long way more from a forty-year hull, standing on a floor that a vanished weight had pressed flat, and the want went nowhere, the way it always went nowhere now, and that was the worst of it, the small absence inside the enormous one, the no-one-to-tell pressing up under the no-one-here.

He made himself work. Work was the thing you did instead. He walked the floor—you could walk it, the spin-down had left enough of a tumble that the lamp swung, but the floor was the floor and his boots found it—and he walked along the curve documenting, because the documenting was the procedure and the procedure was the only thing standing between him and a man who stands in a tomb the size of a city and lets it take him apart.

He photographed the floor. He photographed the curve of the floor going up, with the *Margin*'s ranging laser laid across it so the radius would be in the frame, so that no one down the gradient could call it a slab. He photographed the staves of the corrugation from the inside, where they were ribs, structure, the bones of a thing built to hold pressure and spin and a world's weight, the alloy he had massed a season ago laid up exactly the way he would have laid it up and could never have laid it up, against a problem he knew the shape of. He found, where a section of the inner skin had peeled, a layering behind it, soil—a depth of it, a made ground laid over the structural floor, the way you would lay a ground if you meant for things to grow in it, and he photographed that, the centimeters of it, the dark line of it against the bright structure, and he did not let himself think the word that wanted to come, which was *field*.

He found the run of the light.

It was high up the curve, a long recess that ran the axis of the wheel, the length of it lost in the dark both ways, and along the recess the inner skin was scorched and crazed in a way the rest was not, baked, sun-faced, the surface of a thing that had had light run along it for a long time, hour on hour on hour, the way the renderings ran a line of false sun down the spine of the green cylinders so that the people inside would have a day. A sky's light had run there. The ghost of where it ran was burned into the skin of the world, a long bright scar of all the mornings the place had made for whoever stood on the ground he was standing on, and the light was sixty million years out, and the scar was still there, baked into the alloy, the fossil of a sun that had never been a sun.

He stood under the ghost of the light and he held the lamp on it and his trade-trained eye read the geometry of it the way it had read the floor and the port, and what the geometry said was the thing the body had been letting in all along at its own slow rate, the cold arriving now all the way to the bottom of him: that this had been a morning, every day, on purpose, for a long time, for someone. That the curve over his head had been a sky someone woke up under. That the soil under the skin had grown something. That a place had been made out here in the dark, against the cold, to keep something alive that the cold would otherwise have ended—a hedge, a redundancy, the eggs in more than one basket, the next place, the thing everyone agreed was coming—and that the someone who built it was sixty million years gone, and the place they built to outlast the ending had outlasted them, and was a tomb, and he was standing in it.

◆

There was a thing on the floor.

He almost did not see it. It was small against the kilometers and the lamp had passed over it twice, and it was only the third pass, when he had stopped walking and stood still under the scar of the false sun to let the cold finish arriving, that the beam caught it where it lay against the rise of the curve, a small made thing fallen on the made ground.

He went to it. He crouched—you could crouch, the floor held him in its slow tumble—and he put the lamp on it and did not touch it, because you did not touch a thing before you photographed the geometry, and he photographed the geometry, and then he could not help it and he touched it.

It was a vessel. A small one. It fit the cup of his glove the way a thing fits a hand it was not made for but could have been—a container, a hollow made thing, the wall of it thin and worked, with a structure to it, a way it had been meant to be held and tipped and set down. It had been set down. That was the thing his hands reported, the thing that went up his arm and into the cold place and broke something in him that the kilometers had not broken, that the vault had not broken, that the scar of the false sun had not broken. The kilometers were too big to break a man. This was the right size. Someone had carried this small worked hollow thing across the ground that had been a ground, under the sky that had been a sky, in the light of the false sun while it still ran, and had set it down, on the floor, the way you set a thing down meaning to come back for it.

They had not come back for it.

He held the small vessel in the cup of his glove in the kilometers of dark and the want came up again, enormous now, the want to turn to the empty couch a hundred meters and a long way off and say *Dev*, and there was no Dev, and there was no one who had built the vessel, and there was no one who had set it down, and there was no one who had failed to come back for it, and there was no one in the whole turning dark but a working pilot crouched on a dead floor holding a cup that a hand sixty million years cold had held, and the no-one-to-tell and the no-one-who-made-it and the no-one-who-came-back all pressed up together into the same place under his breastbone, and he knelt there and let it, because there was nothing to fix, because for once in his life there was nothing to fix.

He set the vessel back down where it had lain. He photographed it again, the context, the geometry, the relation of the small made thing to the made ground and the structural rib and the scar of the light, so that no one down the gradient could say it was a rock, so that no one could call it a smear or a ghost or the eye finding structure in noise, because it was not noise, it was a cup, set down on a floor, by someone who meant to come back, in a place built to outlast the end of the world and standing empty sixty million years past the end of theirs.

◆

He took nothing from inside. He had meant to take a sample—the procedure, the chip and the context photo and the geometry—and he stood on the floor with the bag in his glove and he did not chip the floor, because the floor was a floor, and you did not chip a floor, and the part of him that had run the broker's arithmetic in the cabin a season ago, the part that had let itself live for a minute in the world where the debt was gone, that part was quiet now, all the way quiet, because there was no figure with zeros that fit on the inside of a tomb the size of a city, and he knew it, and the knowing was a relief the way the cold was a relief, the way a true thing was always a relief once you stopped running from it.

He had the photographs instead. That was the thing he carried out, hand over hand back along the tether with the kilometers of curved dark closing behind him and the scar of the false sun going out of the lamp's reach and the small vessel left where its hand had left it: the run of the false sun baked into the skin; the made ground over the structural floor; the curve laid out against the *Margin*'s laser so the radius would be a number a careful person could not argue with; the rib, the port, the geometry of a place built to be lived in. Pictures of a sky someone woke up under. Pictures that were not a chip you could call contamination and not a ratio you could call a measurement near the detection limit and not a smear from a careless glove. A place. A whole place, photographed against a ruled laser line, kilometers of made world with the grammar of a morning in it, and the cup on the floor for scale.

You could deny a number. He understood that now in a way he had not understood it a season ago, filing the isotopes to the open board, thinking the number would do the work. A number was a thing a man with a reason could hold at arm's length. He had read the careful private line on the board—*Have you been inside*—and he understood now that the someone down the gradient had known, before he had, that the number was not enough, that the isotopes were a handwriting and a man could always say a handwriting had no hand. You could not deny a floor that curved up into a vault. You could not deny a scar where a false sun had run. You could not deny a cup set down by a hand that meant to come back. Not the way you denied a number. You could try. But it would cost you something to try, in front of the photograph, the way it had cost him something to stand on the floor, and that cost was the thing the number had never had.

◆

He got back inside and out of the suit and he sat in the webbing in the cold cabin with the loop ticking on the bad grease and the photographs filling the good screen, the curve and the scar and the cup, and the radar shadow he had carried all season finally meant what it had always meant, and he let it.

He did not file it to the open board. He had thought, going out, that he would do it the same way he had done the assay—straight, by the procedure, into the pool for anyone to read. He sat with his thumb over the key and he did not press it, and this was a different not-pressing than the season before, because the season before he had pressed it, had paid the zeros, had put the thing where the frontier could read it and call it whatever it called things. He understood now that the frontier did not have a use for this. The frontier had a use for tonnage and registry lines and rates that did not exist yet. The frontier would index this and search it and dredge it for reasons of its own and none of those reasons would be the right size for a cup set down on a floor.

There was one person the photographs were the right size for. He knew it the way he had known the floor was a floor—not by reading it, by recognizing it. The careful private line had come from her direction, down the long gradient, stripped of its punctuation so it would not look like a thing he could shrug off. Someone who kept a standing net out for the heavy daughters with no natural parents. Someone who had been told by everyone whose advice was worth having that the thing she was reading could not be there, and had not looked away, the way he had not been able to look away from the seam that came apart clean. Someone who had a handwriting and had spent a long time unable to find the hand, and here was the hand: kilometers of it, with a morning built into it, and a cup on the floor.

He did not have her name. He had a routing he could answer down, long, stripped, the way it had come. He composed it the way he composed everything now, plain, no editorial, because the moment you editorialized an extraordinary return was the moment they got to stop reading you, and this was the most extraordinary return there had ever been and he was not going to give anyone the out.

*Been inside,* he wrote. *It's not a fragment. It's a wheel. Kilometers of it. Built to be lived in—floor, soil over the floor, a burn down the axis where a sun ran. Spin's decayed. It's been empty a long time. Sending pictures, not samples; I didn't chip it, you don't chip a floor. Laser's in frame for the radius so nobody can call it a slab.* He stopped. He thought about the cup, and he did not know how to write the cup, and he wrote it anyway, plain, because she of all people would know what to do with it. *There's a cup on the floor. Somebody set it down. They didn't come back.* He attached the photographs, all of them, the curve and the scar and the rib and the port and the cup, and he wrote, last, in the plainest words he had: *Your number's right. I'm standing in what made it. I think we need to talk, and I think it can't wait for the lag, and I think you already know that or you wouldn't have asked if I'd been inside.*

He looked at the second couch, once, and put it away.

He sent it down the long gradient toward the inner system, toward the woman with the standing net, toward the centimeter of clay he did not know about and the deathbed city he had never heard of, the two halves of a sentence written sixty million years apart drifting at last into the same line, and outside the hull the wheel turned, slow, the floor coming up and going over and coming down empty, the scar of the false sun running its dead morning around and around in the dark, and the cup lay on the made ground where the hand had left it, and the cold finished arriving, all the way to the bottom of him, and stayed.
