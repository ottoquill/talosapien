# Contamination

She ran it again, which was the eleventh time, and which she had promised herself the tenth time was the last, because running it again was not science anymore, it was the thing you did instead of believing the science, and she knew the difference and did it anyway. The mass spectrometer did not care how many times she asked it. It gave her the same numbers it had given her at two in the morning and at four and now at the grey end of the night when the field camp's generator was the only sound and the Hell Creek badlands outside the trailer were going from black to the color of old bone, and the numbers said what they had said every time, which was that there was something in the Boundary clay of the Rendel core that the falling rock could not have put there.

The iridium was fine. The iridium was wonderful, in fact, textbook, a clean global spike exactly where it belonged, the Alvarez signature that every undergraduate could draw from memory, the fingerprint of the day the sky fell. Nobody argued about the iridium anymore. Asha could have published the iridium in her sleep and no one would have looked up.

It was the things sitting next to the iridium that would not let her sleep.

She had a ratio of plutonium isotopes that had no business existing. Not the bare presence of plutonium — you could argue contamination for that, fallout from the last century's tests had gotten into everything, into the ice and the coral and the teeth of people born after 1945, and a careless reading of a deep core could pick up a modern smear and call it ancient if you wanted it badly enough, and Conrad Mauss would say she wanted it badly enough, and the awful thing was that she did. But the *ratio* was wrong for fallout. The ratio was wrong for anything that had been made in the twentieth century, and it was wrong in a specific direction, the direction of a great deal of decay, of daughters that had been quietly turning into other daughters for a length of time that was not decades and was not centuries and was, if the ratio meant what a ratio like that could only mean, about sixty-six million years.

She had a nitrogen isotope excursion — the δ15N going somewhere the carbon cycle alone does not send it — that looked, to her, who had spent her career reading the chemical handwriting of dead environments, exactly like what you would see if something had been pulling nitrogen out of the air and fixing it at industrial scale and laying the byproduct down in the mud. She had a carbon excursion bigger than the Deccan volcanism wanted to explain. She had spherules — the little glass beads that rained out of the impact plume, that everyone agreed were impact glass — and three of them, under the good microscope, had an internal structure, a regularity, that impact glass does not have and manufacture does, and she had stopped showing people the three spherules because the way their faces changed when they looked and then changed back was a thing she could not afford to keep watching.

Each one alone was deniable. That was the part she had finally understood, sometime around the eighth run, the thing that had turned the work from exciting into the cold flat dread that was keeping her in the trailer at dawn. Each anomaly, by itself, had an out. Contamination. Diagenesis — the slow chemical reworking of buried sediment, the catch-all that could explain almost any inconvenient number if you invoked it with enough confidence. A volcanic source. A lab error. A core handled by too many hands. Take any one of her impossible numbers to Mauss and he would, kindly, give her the out, and he would be right that the out existed, and she would not be able to prove it was the wrong out, because *the wrong out cannot be disproven; it can only be made to look unlikely,* and unlikely is not a thing you can stand on in front of a man who has decided in advance what the rock is allowed to say.

It was only when you put them together that they stopped being a list of deniable anomalies and became a sentence. And the sentence was the most extraordinary sentence in the history of her science, and the evidence for it was a handful of numbers each of which had an innocent explanation, and that, she was beginning to see, was not a temporary state of the investigation. That was the permanent condition of the thing. She had read the old paper, the one from the twenties, the two physicists who had asked the question as a thought experiment and answered it with a kind of beautiful bad news: if there had been an industrial civilization before us, deep enough in time, *we almost certainly could not prove it,* because the planet recycles its own crust and erases its own surface and sixty-six million years is long enough to subduct a continent and grind a city to a chemical smear. The most you could hope to find was exactly this: ghosts in the isotopes. A handwriting with no hand. A case that was real and could not be closed.

She had thought, reading it years ago, that it was a charming piece of provocation. She had not understood that it was a prophecy about her own life, and specifically about this hour, in this trailer, with the eleventh run finishing and the generator coughing and the badlands going the color of bone, and her thumb hovering over the key that would start a twelfth.

She did not press it. She had promised. Instead she opened the logbook — the paper one, the one she kept by hand because she did not trust herself, in this, to a file that could be edited without leaving the kind of mark that shamed you — and she wrote the date, and she wrote the run number, and then she sat with the pen and did the unprofessional thing she had started doing three weeks ago and had not told anyone about, not even Téo, especially not Téo. She wrote, under the numbers, in the small private hand she used for the things that were not yet allowed to be science: *Whoever you were, you were not careless. Careless doesn't leave a ratio. Careless leaves a mess. This is the opposite of a mess. This is the most careful thing I have ever read, and you laid it down on purpose, or your death did, and I cannot tell which, and I am beginning to think you couldn't either.*

Then she closed the logbook, because the light was up, and Téo would be awake, and you could not let a graduate student catch you writing letters to the Cretaceous.

"You didn't sleep," Téo said, which was not a question, when she came out into the cold blue morning with two coffees and her face arranged into the face of someone who had slept. He was already at the screen-side of the camp's worktable with the core photographs spread out under the rising sun, the long stacked images of the Rendel core annotated in his neat hand, and he had the particular brightness of a young man who had slept eight hours and believed the day would contain a discovery, and she loved him for it and could not afford it.

"I ran it again," she said.

"Asha."

"I know."

"That's eleven."

"I know how many it is, Téo, I'm the one who—" She stopped. He was not the thing she was angry at. He was never the thing she was angry at; he was twenty-six and brilliant and had hitched his entire near future to a supervisor who was about to do something that could end both of their careers, and he did not know that yet, and the kindest thing she could do for him was to make sure he had a way to unhitch before the rest of the field worked out what she was claiming. "It's the same," she said, more gently. "Eleven times the same. The plutonium ratio holds. The nitrogen holds. It's not drifting, it's not a calibration ghost, it doesn't move when I move the standard. It's just *there*, Téo, it's been there for sixty-six million years waiting for somebody to be stubborn enough to run it twelve times, and the only reason no one found it before is that no one runs a Boundary sample for transuranics, because why would you, there's nothing there to find, everyone knows that, it's just the iridium and the impact and the dinosaurs and the cleanest story in all of geology."

Téo was quiet. He picked up one of the core photographs, the close one, the centimeter of dark clay with the pale beds above and below, the most photographed centimeter in the history of the planet, and he looked at it the way she had taught him to look at things, which was to assume it was lying and find out how.

"Okay," he said. "Walk me through what would have to be wrong. Because you taught me that. Before you tell me what it is, tell me everything it could be that's boring."

She felt something in her chest unclench, just slightly, because this was the good part, this was the part that was still science, and as long as they were doing this part they were not yet the kind of people the field laughed at. "Contamination," she said. "Modern plutonium from weapons testing, smeared into the core during recovery or handling. That's the first and best one."

"And the ratio kills it."

"The ratio makes it very unlikely. The isotopic ratio looks like old plutonium, decayed, not like fresh fallout. But Téo — a careful skeptic doesn't have to believe my ratio. He has to believe his own mass spec, and if he thinks my instrument is mis-measuring a tiny quantity near the detection limit, then a weird ratio is exactly what a measurement error would also produce. I can't prove the ratio's real without more sample than the Boundary will give me, and the Boundary's a centimeter, and it's the same centimeter everywhere, and I can't make more of it."

"Diagenesis."

"For the nitrogen and carbon, sure. Burial chemistry can push isotopes around. I don't think it pushes them *this* way, this much, in this pattern, but 'I don't think' is not a result."

"The spherules."

She didn't answer for a moment. The sun had cleared the ridge and the badlands were turning gold and ordinary, the way the most haunted places do in daylight, refusing to look like anything but rock. "The spherules I can't explain," she said quietly. "And that's the one I trust least, because I want it most. When you want a thing, Téo, your eye finds structure in noise. That's not a flaw you can train out. That's just what an eye is. The three spherules might be exactly what I think they are, or they might be three times my pattern-hungry brain doing to glass what it does to clouds. I've stopped looking at them. I don't trust myself with them anymore."

Téo set the photograph down. He was not bright anymore, not in the morning-discovery way; he had gone somewhere older, somewhere she had not wanted to send him this early. "Then why do you believe it," he said. "If every piece has an out. If you can't prove any of it. You taught me extraordinary claims need extraordinary evidence and you just told me your evidence is a stack of ordinary-looking anomalies that only mean something if you let them lean on each other. So why do you believe it, instead of doing the sane thing and writing it up as 'interesting unresolved anomalies in the K-Pg boundary, further work needed,' and keeping your job?"

It was the right question. It was the exact question Mauss would ask, gentler in Téo's mouth but the same blade, and she had been not-answering it for three weeks, and the dawn and the coffee and the boy's honest face pulled it out of her before she could file it down into something safe.

"Because of the shape," she said. "Each anomaly is deniable on its own. But they're not a random scatter of deniable things, Téo. They're a *set*. Industrial nitrogen fixation. Transuranics — which means fission, which means either reactors or bombs. A carbon spike like burning. Glass that looks made. If I handed you that exact set of signatures in a layer from forty thousand years ago, you wouldn't hesitate for a second; you'd say, that's a technological civilization, obviously, what else fixes nitrogen and splits atoms and makes glass. You'd say it before lunch. The *only* reason it sounds insane is the date. The only thing making this an extraordinary claim is that it's sixty-six million years too early for the one species we've already decided is allowed to have done it. Take the date off and it's the most boring interpretation in the world. So I have to ask myself a question I really, really don't like, which is: am I refusing the obvious reading of the evidence because the evidence is weak, or because I've decided in advance that we were first?"

Téo looked at her for a long moment in the gold light. Behind him, down the slope, was the dig — the Tanis bonebed, the deathbed deposit, the place that had made her name a decade ago, the unbearable lucky preservation of a single afternoon sixty-six million years gone, fish that had inhaled the impact debris in the hour it rained down, a whole drowned world recorded in a flood-surge like a photograph of the exact moment everything ended. She had built a career on reading that one terrible afternoon. She had not yet understood — she would not understand for the better part of a year, and we are not going to rush it, because she did not get to rush it — *whose* afternoon it was.

"Okay," Téo said at last, and she heard him decide something, heard the small private click of a young person choosing the harder of two futures, and she wanted to tell him to choose the other one and did not, because she was not strong enough, and because she needed him, and she would hate herself for both of those for a long time. "Okay. Then let's make it stronger. Tell me what evidence would make even Mauss flinch. Not prove it — you've convinced me we can't prove it. Just make him flinch. What's the thing that doesn't have an out?"

She did not know yet. That was the honest answer, that morning, in the spring, at Tanis. She did not yet know that the thing without an out was not in the clay at all, that no amount of the most careful chemistry on Earth would ever close the case from the ground, that the only piece of evidence in the entire solar system that could not be waved away as a smear or a ghost or a hungry eye was at that moment turning, slow and silent and impossibly old, in the cold dark between Mars and Jupiter, where a man named Roan Iyer was about to do a routine survey of an orbit that did not behave, and find, riding it, the one thing the dead had built large enough and far enough away that even sixty-six million years had not been able to grind it down to a number you could deny.

But that morning she only had the clay, and the boy, and the eleven runs, and the letter she should not have written. So she said the true thing, which was the smaller thing: "I don't know. But I'm going to take the clay to Mauss anyway, because that's the procedure, and the procedure is the only thing standing between me and being exactly the crank he's going to call me." She picked up her cold coffee. "Run it a twelfth time while I pack," she said. "Not because I don't believe it. Because when he asks how many times we ran it, I want the number to be obscene. I want it to be a number that embarrasses him."

Téo almost smiled. "How many is obscene?"

"Keep going till the answer stops being interesting," she said, "and then do twenty more." She went to break down the trailer. Behind her she heard the spectrometer start its cycle again, the small patient mechanical breath of an instrument that did not care what it found or who it ruined, and she stood for a second in the gold morning with the deathbed below her and the impossible centimeter in its case and the letter folded in the logbook, and she let her face do, in private, where the boy couldn't see, the thing she never let it do in front of anyone — the thing that, in another body, on the other side of a centimeter of clay, a man holding down the grey at his throat would have recognized instantly, and never could.
