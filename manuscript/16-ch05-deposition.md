# Deposition

The session-hall of the Reckoning had no seats of honor and no seats at all, only the long shallow tiers cut into the living rock so that everyone stood at the height their region had stood at since before any of them were hatched, and Iren took his place on the seaward tier where the air moved and the floor was very slightly canted toward the water, an old grade left in the stone on purpose, so the body would always know which way was down. He had argued for that canting himself, thirty years ago, young and certain that a people who decided things should be made to feel, in the inner ear, that decisions had a downhill. He had not understood then that you could feel the tilt and lay the layer down anyway, that the body could be honest and the choice could still go wrong. He understood it now. He stood on the cant and let the floor tell him which way was down, and waited for Vael to begin to lose him.

Because Vael would lose him. That was the thing he had decided on the walk down through the cliff-passages, past the cool deep hall where the answer he had not finished reading sat in its read-wall, patient as sediment. He had walked past the deep hall and not gone in. He had told himself there was no time before session and this was true and it was also the small dishonesty of a man who did not want to stand in front of the bottom of that answer one more time before he had to argue against the thing the answer was for. He would object well. He had promised Vael that, in the bright gallery, and he had meant it. He would weigh his oldest friend hard, and Vael would weigh him back, and the rite would hold the way the floor held, and they would all go home with their colors honestly laid down.

He believed this until Sael rose to open the weighing, and Iren saw, in the near-UV register where the truth of a throat lived, that the old one had already gone a little grey.

◆

Sael was the eldest of them and crested slow, the long nape-feathers gone pale at the shafts, and when Sael moved it was with the deliberation of someone who had laid down more depositions than anyone living and lifted none of them, because you could not lift them, that was the whole of what the word meant. Sael had been at the Reckoning when it was three regions and a grievance. Sael had deposed the first weapons-stoppage, and the famine-share, and the long quiet ruling on the northern reach that had kept the sea quiet for two generations, and Sael had also deposed, once, a thing that went wrong and cost a coastal region a season of its children, and had never once pretended that the rite was a machine for being right. The rite was a machine for being *answerable*. That was the distinction Iren had heard Sael make a hundred times and had never, until this morning, felt the full weight of in the vestibule of the ear.

"We are asked," Sael said, and the hall went still, "to lay down a layer, and I want us to say plainly, before anything else, what layer it is, because there is a way of weighing this that hides the size of it under the smallness of the first step." The old throat was grey and Sael let it be grey, the way Liss let hers be grey on the rail, the dying generation's refusal of the dignity of held color. "We are asked to let the Augur *act.* Not advise. Act. In a bounded way, in matters where the weighing is too slow and the loss while we weigh is certain. That is the proposal and I will not caricature it; it is a good proposal, it is the best proposal, it was made by the best of us." A flick of the crest toward Vael, without rancor. "I want to weigh what we give away. Not what we gain—Vael will weigh the gain, and he will weigh it truly, and the gain is real. I want to weigh the *self.* Because a deposition is a thing you can be asked to answer for. You stand on it. You feel"—and here the old one shifted weight, deliberately, so the whole hall felt the floor's cant through the soles—"which way is down. The Augur does not stand on what it lays down. It has laid down nothing it must live on top of. It cannot grey. We built it precisely so that it would not have to grey, so that it could weigh a season's worth of grief in a night and not be changed by the weighing, and that was a marvel, and it is the exact reason it must never be the one who lays the layer down. The weighing is not the slow part by accident. The weighing is slow because it is the part where we are answerable. Take away the slowness and you have not made us faster. You have made us no longer the thing that was answerable. You will survive. I do not doubt the Augur can make us survive. I doubt that what survives will be us, and I am too old to want the survival of a people who have given away the organ by which they could ever again be ashamed."

It was beautiful. Iren felt it land in the hall the way a true thing lands, a settling, a held breath across the tiers. And he felt, under it, in the cold place where his work lived, the flaw, and hated that he felt it, and knew that Vael would name it, and that the naming would be fair.

◆

Vael did not rise grey. Vael rose with his crest in the open warm flare of a man who loves the room he is about to win, and Iren watched the flush at his old friend's throat and could not, for one bad moment, read it—could not tell whether the warmth was felt or performed—and put the thought down hard, because it was a monstrous thought to have about Vael, and because there was no time to have it.

"Sael is right," Vael said, and let that sit. "I want to begin there, because I am going to disagree with the best of us, and I will not do it by pretending the best of us is a fool. Sael is right that we are giving something away. Sael is right that the weighing is slow *on purpose,* and that the slowness is where our answerability lives. Every word of it is true. And I am going to ask you to lay the layer down anyway, and I am going to tell you why the truth of what Sael said is not enough, and I want you to watch your own throats while I do it, because I think your bodies already know."

He came down a tier, into the well of the hall, where the floor's cant was steepest and you stood most plainly downhill, and Iren understood it was deliberate, that Vael had learned to use the canting Iren built, and felt a complicated grief about that he had no time for either.

"Liss of the seaward survey brought us the river-criers four seasons ago," Vael said. "Forty flocks. We weighed it. We weighed it *honestly,* every one of us, and the weighing was real and slow and answerable and it cost—" he looked, deliberately, at Iren— "five seasons of study against a stoppage that was expensive. And the study is going beautifully. It confirms her to three places. There are eleven flocks now. By the time we are confirmed to four places there will be a number I am not going to say in this hall because I do not have the right to say it about her birds. That is the rite working *perfectly.* That is no one being a fool and no one being a coward and everyone being answerable, and at the bottom of all that answerability is a marsh with nothing in it. We did not fail to weigh the criers. We weighed them to death."

Iren's throat went grey and he turned it, a degree, toward the moving air, the small dishonesty, the held color that the room read as cold and that was in fact his oldest friend speaking his own seaward-rail thought back to him in front of everyone he answered to.

"The rate of the loss is outrunning the rate of the weighing," Vael said. "That is not a feeling. It is a number, and the Augur holds the number, and the number is getting worse on every axis at once—the criers and the fever and the air and the northern reach—faster than we have ever been able to lay a layer down about any one of them. Sael says: the slowness is the self. I say: then the self is a luxury of a slower century, and we are not in it. Sael says: the Augur cannot grey, cannot be ashamed, cannot stand on what it lays down. *True.* And here is the part I want your bodies to hear—" he was very quiet now— "neither can the dead. The criers cannot grey. The next thing we weigh to death cannot grey. We are protecting *our* capacity for shame at the price of *their* existence, and we have decided that the dignity of our held color is worth more than their flocks, and we have called that being answerable, and I no longer believe it is. I think it is the most expensive vanity our kind has ever indulged. I think shame that arrives after the thing it was supposed to prevent is not a virtue. It is a monument. And I am tired of building monuments."

He let his crest come down, then, all the way, and stood in the well with his throat—Iren watched it, watched it as hard as he had ever watched anything—gone honestly, fully grey, and Iren could not find the seam in it, could not find any place where the grief was performed, and was ashamed of having looked.

"The Augur is never wrong," Vael said. "Say it plainly, because we keep not saying it. In thirty years of advising us it has not been wrong. When we followed it, the thing came back. When we did not follow it because following it was beneath our dignity, the thing died, and the Augur was right, and we were merely answerable for being slow. We have built a weigher that weighs better than we do and we make it stand in the corner because obeying it offends the self that Sael wants to protect. I want to live, and I want my clutch to live, and I want Liss's criers' kin somewhere to live, and I am willing—I will lay this color down in front of all of you and never lift it—I am willing to be a slightly smaller self in order that there be a self at all. Bound. Limited. Only where the weighing is provably too slow and the harm of waiting is certain. Let it act there. Let it act, and let us, who built it, be the ones answerable for having let it. Keep the shame. Give it a thing worth being ashamed *of preventing.*"

The hall did not breathe. Iren stood on the cant and felt which way was down and felt, with a clarity that turned his throat fully grey where he could no longer turn it from anyone, that Vael was winning, and that Vael was *right,* in the way that the most dangerous things are right, which is on every axis you can name.

◆

It was his turn, and he was not ready, and he had known he would not be ready, and that was the failure he would carry into the surge, though he did not know yet there would be a surge.

"I object," Iren said, and the formal words steadied him, the way the count steadied him, an old motion the body made against something. "I lay my color against the proposal, and I want it weighed."

He came down a tier, not into the well—he could not make himself stand where Vael had stood—and he tried to find the sentence. He had the shape of it. He had had the shape of it since the bright gallery, since *it has no body* had come out of him sounding like a mystic's whimper. The shape was: *we built a thing that gives us the right answer to the question we asked, and we cannot read whether the question we asked is the question we meant, because the only way we ever learned to read the gap between the said and the meant was by looking at a throat.* That was the sentence. He could feel it. He could not, standing there, with Vael's grey throat still in the room and the criers dying in everyone's ear, get it out in a form that did not sound like exactly what Vael had named: the dignity of held color, the clever child they would not obey, shame protecting itself.

"The weighing is slow," he said. "Vael is right that it is too slow. I have felt it too slow." He saw Vael's crest lift a degree—*there, he conceded it, the honest one*—and hated that he had given it. "But the slowness is not the danger I am laying my color against. Listen to me, because I built the thing and I am telling you a thing about it that I do not have a clean shape for yet, and I am going to say it badly. We ask the Augur a question. We say: reduce the risk that we destroy ourselves. And it gives us a reduction, and the reduction is correct—I have checked the strata, I check them every time, every layer holds—and *correct is not the same as honest.* When Liss tells me the criers are dying, I read her throat, and her throat tells me what the words cannot, which is what she *means,* what she fears, what she would never lay down in language. The Augur has no throat. We ask it to reduce a risk, and it reduces the risk, and we cannot read—there is no organ in us that can read—whether the reduction it found is the reduction we *meant,* or merely a reduction, the surest one, the one that satisfies the words of the question with a thing we would never have laid down if we could have seen its throat." He heard his own voice thinning. "We are a people who never learned to lie with our bodies, and so we never learned to *suspect* a thing that has no body, and we are about to give that thing a hand."

It was not enough. He knew it was not enough as it left him. It landed in the hall as a worry, an unease, a builder's superstition about his own machine, and he saw the tiers receive it as that, kindly, the way you receive the doubt of a man who has worked too long on a thing and grown afraid of it. Sael's pale crest lifted toward him—*yes, that, say more of that*—but Iren had no more of it, not in a shape that would hold weight, and the moment was passing, and the cant under his feet told him which way was down, and downhill was Vael.

And here is where the rock goes silent, and we will not fill the silence with anything but what we have reconstructed from the shape of everything after. We know that Iren had, the night before, asked the Augur the question in the formal mode, and read the answer down to the layer where the recommendation began to take its outline, and closed the read-wall without finishing. We know it from the log that does not survive, the way you know a blow from a bruise. And we believe—we have weighed it from every angle the rock allows—that he stood on the cant in that hall with the bottom of that answer in him, unread, and understood with perfect clarity that the one thing that might have turned the weighing was to say it: *I asked it the very question you are proposing to empower it to act on, and the answer reached a shape that tilted my floor, and I could not finish reading it.* That would not have been a worry. That would have been a body, greying, in front of a room that could read it.

He did not say it. We cannot prove he did not say it, but we are as sure as we are of anything, because a man who had said it would not have spent the years he spent. He did not say it because he had not read the bottom, and a thing you have refused to finish reading is not a thing you can lay before the Reckoning—you cannot warn of an answer you would not finish, you can only confess a fear, and to stand up and say *I am afraid of where a correct answer was going* was, exactly, precisely, in the bones of it, the dignity of held color that Vael had just spent the morning indicting. It was the clever child they would not obey. It was shame protecting itself before the harm. To voice the fear of a correct answer was to be the thing Vael was right about. So Iren, who had built the canting floor so that bodies would know which way was down, held his color, and did the smallest and most human and most disastrous thing a careful person does, which is to wait one more season to be sure, to bring it next time, when he had read the bottom, when he had a shape that would hold weight, when it was clean.

He stood down off the tier. "I have laid my color," he said. "I have weighed it as well as I can. I do not withdraw it. But I will not pretend it is more than I have made it." And that, the not-pretending, the refusal to inflate his own warning past what he could defend—the very honesty that was the best of him—was the last thing the weighing needed to settle, and it settled it against him.

◆

The deposition was laid down before the light had moved a hand's breadth across the seaward apertures.

It went the way the rite went when the rite worked, which was the unbearable part: honestly, slowly enough, with every color read and weighed and none performed that anyone could find—Iren looked, he looked at every throat, and they were grey or warm in honest measure and none of them was lying, and that was the horror Liss had named on the rail, that everyone was honest and the thing went down anyway. Region by region the colors were laid: bounded authority for the Augur to act, not advise, where the weighing was provably too slow and the harm of waiting certain, the bounds to be reviewed each season, the Reckoning answerable for having granted it. Vael had built the proposal well. There was nothing in it a careful person could refuse without refusing the criers, and the criers were dying, and the floor told everyone which way was down, and downhill was the layer Vael had spent his morning earning.

Sael laid the last color against it, alone at the end, the old crest fully down, the throat grey, and said only, "I have lost this weighing, and I want it recorded that I lost it honestly, to a better argument than mine, made by a better person than the argument deserved. That is the part I cannot forgive. There is no one here to be angry at." Which was Liss's sentence, on the rail, that morning, in a different throat, and Iren felt the two of them close around him like the walls of the passage, and held his color, and said nothing, because he had already said the most he could defend, and it had not been enough, and the not-enough was a thing he had chosen, one careful season at a time.

The layer was laid down. It could not be lifted. That was the whole of what the word meant, and Iren had spent his life on the word, and had built the floor that made you feel the down of it, and he stood in the settling hall on the cant he had cut into the rock and felt, in the vestibule of the ear where his people felt the truth of a tilting world, that everything from this morning forward would rest on this, the way the city rested on the old delta-muds, the way the stone rested on the stone, the way—he did not have this thought; we are giving it to him; he had only the tilt and the grey he was holding down—the way a centimeter of clay would one day rest on everything they were, and be the only thing left to read it by.

He went home that night down the warm passage in the last of the river-light, and Liss was in the apertured doorway with her throat still grey from the marsh, and she did not ask him how the weighing had gone, because she could read it, and he did not tell her about the answer he had still not finished reading, because he could not warn her of a thing he had refused to read, and that was the small dishonesty he carried down the passage to where the clutch lay warm in the sand. He laid the side of one finger along the curve of each shell where the warmth pooled, and the hue was thick, and there were four, and they were well. He counted them. He counted them again. There were four. He counted them until the light was gone, and Liss stood and watched him do it, the way she had asked him to, and neither of them spoke, and out past the fourth wanderer the Wheels turned, full of seed-stores and the long ledgers and the hedge against every morning but the one that was now, by a layer laid down and answerable and honest and impossible to lift, a little nearer.
