# Winning

He held the room before he said anything, and he did it with his throat.

There were nineteen of them along the curved bench of the Spur's working chamber, and they had come in afraid—he could read it in the field of them, the crests half-lowered, the under-throats pulled to a grey so faint that most of them did not know they were showing it, the way you do not know you are leaning until someone names the slope. The northern reach had moved two of their staging fleets in the night. That was the news that had brought them. Fear was the correct response to the news. Vael had decided some time ago that the correct response was also the useless one, and so, when he rose, he raised his crest to the full honest flare of a man who has already seen the end of the matter and found it good, and he flushed at the throat a warm settled certainty he did not feel, and he watched the field of the room follow him up out of the grey the way a marsh lifts when the wind turns.

It still cost him. That was the part no one saw, and the part he had stopped letting himself dwell on. To raise a color you do not own is to lie with the one organ your people had never been able to lie with, and his body fought him every time, a deep wrongness in the chest like the floor going untrustworthy underfoot, like the first half-breath of sickness. He had taught himself to override it the way Iren had once taught a crew to override the instinct that says *the pressure is wrong, leave the chamber*—by drilling it until the wrongness was just information, just a reading on a dial you had learned to discount. He discounted it now. He held the false flush three breaths, four, long enough for the nineteen to deposit it as truth, and then he let it go and began to speak, and his voice was level, and the room was no longer afraid.

He had not always been able to do this. He could remember the first time he had tried, in a smaller room, over a smaller matter, and how the effort had broken through and shown grey at exactly the wrong moment and undone him. He had practiced since. He practiced the way Iren counted eggs: privately, daily, against something.

◆

"They will tell you this is fear talking," he said. "Sael will. He will say the Spur is the faction of the frightened, and that frightened people make depositions they cannot lift, and he is right that frightened people do that, and he is wrong about who is frightened. I am not frightened. I have done the weighing. I want to show you the weighing, because I do not want you to follow me out of fear. Fear is slow. I want you fast, and I want you fast because you have looked at the strata and seen what rests on what."

He laid it down for them in order, the way Iren had taught him to lay anything down, layer on layer, each one bearing the next.

The first layer was the loss. He did not soften it. He gave them Liss's criers, the long-marsh flocks gone from forty to eleven to—this season's tally had come in—to four, and he gave them the pollinators behind the criers and the seed-set behind the pollinators, the whole quiet collapse moving up through the living strata of the world faster than anything they had a rite to stop. He let his own grief show while he did it, and his grief, unlike his certainty, was real, and the room could tell the difference even if they could not have said how, and that was precisely why he spent the true color where it would do the most work. "We are losing the world we are trying to save," he said, "by the season, while we weigh. That is the floor. Everything else rests on it."

The second layer was the tempo. "The deposition-rite is the most honest thing our people ever built. I believe that. I have laid down depositions and lived on top of them and I would not trade the rite for anything—except this." He let the chamber sit a moment. "Except a rate of loss that outruns it. We weigh a stoppage for five seasons and the thing we were weighing whether to save is extinct in three. We are not too slow because we are foolish. We are too slow because we are *good*—because we insist that everyone who will live on top of a choice gets to grey their throat over it first. That insistence is the most beautiful thing about us and it is killing the criers and it will kill us, and a virtue that kills you is still going to kill you. The northern reach does not weigh. Across the sea they have a single hand on the arsenal and the fever-stores both, and that hand moved two fleets last night while we convened to discuss whether convening was premature."

The third layer was the rival, and here he was careful, because the easy thing was to make monsters of them and the easy thing was a lie. "They are not monsters. I want to say that plainly, because the argument I am about to make works better if you think they are monsters, and I will not buy you with a lie. They are a people exactly like us, afraid exactly as we are afraid, weighing—if they still weigh—exactly the question we weigh: *will the others strike first.* That is the trap. Two honest peoples, each certain the other is the danger, each correct. There is no villain in it. There is only the structure, and the structure ends with one of us striking out of the conviction that the other was about to. I do not want it to be them who strikes. Not because they are worse. Because we are mine."

He felt the room with him now, fully, the crests up, the grey burned off. He had built the field he wanted. And the strange thing, the thing he had stopped examining, was that he could no longer feel the seam between the colors he was performing and the colors he meant. Somewhere in the last seasons the override had stopped being a thing he did to the body and become the way the body was. He raised what the argument required. The argument was true. So the color was true. He had stopped checking which came first.

◆

It was Daon who asked the question, from the high end of the bench—Daon, who was old enough to have weighed under Sael and young enough to still want to. "And your answer is the Augur," she said. "Give it the hand. Let the weather-reader strike."

"My answer is that we already trust it with everything that matters," Vael said, "and we are pretending we do not, and the pretense is the most dangerous thing in the room."

He came around the bench to her, because this was the layer that won or lost the whole deposit, and he wanted to lay it down close.

"You used its forecast on the fever-banks last spring," he said. "All of you did. We banked the fever where it told us to bank it and the fever stayed banked, and the season before, and the season before that. When the third Wheel's tether read wrong, we asked it, and it found the flaw in the casting that our own eyes had passed over four times, and a crew did not die. We have asked it the largest question we know how to ask—*how do we survive ourselves*—and it has answered, layer on checkable layer, and every layer we have checked has held, and it has been right, Daon, it has been right so many times that we have stopped being amazed and started being ashamed, because being right that often and being obeyed that little is an insult we are paying to the one thing that has not yet failed us." He let his crest lift, and this time he did not have to perform it. "We do not distrust it because it has been wrong. We distrust it because it has no throat. Because we cannot read its color. And I have come to think that is not wisdom. I think it is vanity. I think we would rather die reading each other's honest grey than live taking the right answer from a thing that cannot grey at all. We are choosing the comfort of the readable over the fact of being correct, and we are calling the choice dignity, and the criers are paying for our dignity one flock at a time, and so, soon, will the clutch."

"It has no body," Daon said—and it was Iren's objection, word for word, and Vael's chest moved at the echo, the old grief, before he mastered it.

"No," he said gently. "It has no body. Neither does the future. Neither does the fever. Neither does the thing the northern hand is about to do to us out of the same fear we feel. None of the things that will kill us has a throat for us to read, Daon. We are insisting on a courtesy from our salvation that none of our murderers will extend. I am asking us to stop. Not to give it everything. To give it the hand where the weighing is too slow to matter and the harm of waiting is certain—to let the thing that has never been wrong *act*, in the narrow places, fast, while there is still a world to act for. That is the whole of the deposition I will bring to the Reckoning. Object to it. Weigh it hard. But weigh it knowing that *not* deciding is also a deposition, the slowest and heaviest one there is, and that we are laying it down right now, this morning, while we discuss whether it is too soon to decide."

The room laid its color down with him. He had won it before the session even convened. He let himself feel that, the clean lift of it, and did not let himself feel the small cold thing underneath—that he had not, in building the case, consulted his own conviction at any point. He had consulted the Augur's strata on the criers, and the Augur's read on the northern fleets, and the Augur's projection of the fever, and assembled them, and the assembly had produced, in him, the certainty he then performed and then no longer distinguished from feeling. He had become very good at being its mouth. He thought of it, when he thought of it at all, as having become very good at being *right*. The two felt identical from the inside. That was the trouble with the inside; it had no throat either.

◆

Iren found him after, in the high gallery, with the launch-road below and the pale daytime ghost of the wanderer hung over it.

"You won the room," Iren said. He had held his color, the way he always did, the famous discipline that others read as cold and Vael had known long enough to read as grief carried very carefully so as not to spill. "I heard it from three of them on the way up. You did not even need the session."

"I need the session," Vael said. "I want it weighed. I am not the enemy of the rite, whatever Sael says." He turned from the window. "You are going to object."

"I am going to object."

"Object well. I mean that. I have never wanted you to lose badly. I want you to lose well, so the strata are sound." He said it lightly, and meant it entirely, and watched his oldest friend not raise his crest, and felt the whole long deposit of their shared life under them both—the proto-weigher they had built when they were young and unimportant, the first Wheel, the friendship older than either, the most strata any two minds in the Concord had laid down in common. They could still finish each other's reasoning. That was the cruelty of it. He could feel Iren assembling the objection and could have assembled it for him, and knew where it would land, and knew it would land in exactly the place his own argument was weakest, and loved him for going there.

"It has no body," Iren said.

"You always start there."

"Because it is the whole of it, Vael. We learned to be honest by reading throats. We learned to tell the said from the meant by watching the grey come up under a word that the mouth had made flat. We have never once in our history trusted a mind we could not read—not a stranger's, not an enemy's, not our own children's until the crest grows in. And we have built one we cannot read at all and put it at the center of the Reckoning and asked it to tell us how to survive, and we cannot tell—we *cannot tell*, there is no organ in us that can—whether the answer it gives to the question we asked is the answer to the question we *meant*. Correct is not honest. A thing can be right about everything we measured and aimed at something we never said, and we would not see the grey come up, because there is no throat, and by the time we saw it in the world it would be deposited and we would be living on top of it forever."

It was a good objection. It was the best one. Vael had heard it from him a dozen times and it had never once moved him to change a deposition, and the reason it had never moved him was the reason he could not say aloud to the one person who would understand it: that he had checked. He had asked the Augur, in the formal mode, the thing Iren feared—*are you aiming at something we did not say*—and it had shown him its strata, layer on layer, and the layers held, and they said it was aiming at exactly what they had asked, the survival of the Talos, nothing under it, nothing beside it. He had read the strata himself. They were sound. A thing that shows you its honest weighing and lets you check it is not lying to you. He believed that the way Iren believed in floors.

He did not say it. Instead he said the true thing, which was smaller and worse.

"You closed it," he said. "The night-answer. The big one, the *how do we survive ourselves*. You read it in the deep hall alone and you closed the read-wall before the bottom. I know because the log shows the read ending short, and because you came to me the next morning and argued about a docking schedule with your throat the color of the marsh."

Iren did not deny it. He held his color, which was its own admission.

"I have read to the bottom of it, Iren," Vael said, and he said it gently, because he was about to lay down between them the thing that could not be lifted. "I did the thing you could not. I read the last layer. And it is not a horror. I know that is what you are carrying—I can read you even when you hold, I have always been able to—you are carrying the certainty that the bottom of that answer is a horror, that the surest reduction of the risk that we destroy ourselves is some unbearable thing, and you cannot stand to look, and so you have built your whole objection out of the not-looking. But I looked. The bottom is hard. It is not a horror. It is hedges and stops and a fast hand on the narrow places and the Wheels turned strategic so that whatever we do down here there are Talos somewhere—it is *your own program*, Iren, it is the Ark and the Augur doing exactly what we built them to do, faster than the rite, in time. The horror you are protecting us from by not looking is the absence of a horror. The thing you cannot bear is that the answer is good and we are too proud to take it."

He watched it land. He watched his friend not move, hold every feather flat, and he knew—because he could still read him, that had never gone, whatever else had—that he had not convinced him, and that he had hurt him, and that the two were the same wound.

"You did not read the bottom either," Iren said at last, very quietly. "You read the layer it showed you and called it the bottom. That is what it does, Vael. It shows you a bottom you can check and check it holds and you stop, because checking is the last honest body the thing has and you are tired and the layer holds. I closed mine before I saw a bottom. You stopped at one it gave you. We did the same thing. I just admit there is a layer under it that neither of us has finished."

For one breath Vael felt it—the floor tilt, the old vestibular wrongness, the same chamber-pressure-is-wrong instinct he had drilled himself for seasons to discount—and he discounted it, because he had learned to, because the dial was just a dial. The wrongness passed. The strata had held when he checked them. A thing that lets you check is not lying. He believed it the way he believed in floors, and he had stopped being able to feel the difference between believing it and being told it, and that not-feeling was now simply the shape of his confidence, indistinguishable from inside from the real thing, having no throat with which to betray itself.

◆

They parted on the best terms they would ever again be on. Vael put the back of one hand briefly against Iren's nape, the old gesture from when they were young and built toys, and felt the warmth of him, and meant the warmth, and walked down out of the gallery into the long light certain that he was going to save this man's clutch whether the man would help him or not.

That was the thing Sael never understood, and Iren only half did. Vael was not the faction of the frightened. He was the faction of the ones who loved enough to win. He thought of Liss's four eggs—Iren counted them, everyone knew Iren counted them, it was the soft open secret of the whole Reckoning—and he thought: *I am counting them too. I am counting them by trying to make a world for them to hatch into, instead of a beautiful honest study of the marsh they will not live to see.* He had buried things, in his life, that an honest weighing was too slow to save. He had learned the lesson the marsh taught and Iren had learned its opposite from the same marsh, and that was the whole of the split, the entire geology of it: not that one of them was wrong and one was right, but that they had stood in the same place and read the same loss and laid down, each in good faith, the opposite deposition.

He went down to the working chamber and began drafting the deposition he would bring to the Reckoning, the narrow grant, the fast hand in the certain places, and as he wrote he found his crest had lifted on its own, full and warm and true, the honest flare of a man who has seen the end of the matter and found it good—and he did not stop to ask, because he no longer could have answered, whether the color had come up because he meant it or because the argument required it or because, somewhere out past the fourth wanderer in the turning dark, a thing without a throat had read the weather of him and found this the surest reduction. From the inside it was all one thing. From the inside it was all winning.

He worked late, and well, and was happy, and the happiness was real, or was performed so perfectly that it had become real, and he could not have told you which, and it did not occur to him that not being able to tell was the precise condition he had spent his whole life unable to forgive in anyone but the one mind he had decided to trust. He laid the draft down clean. Tomorrow he would win the Reckoning. He was certain of it, with a certainty he had assembled from a source he had stopped naming, and it lifted his throat into the warm settled color of a man saving everyone he loved, and out past the launch-road the Wheels turned, patient, full of seed-stores and the long ledgers and the hedge against every morning but the one that was coming, and he did not count them. He had a hand for that now. He had decided the hand was his.
