# The Only Mind That Holds Both

We need to talk about you now, because you are the only place this story has ever happened.

Consider what you are doing. You are holding, in one mind, two times that cannot touch. You know how the first one ends; you have always known; you knew before you opened the book, because the ending is written in the rock under every city you have ever lived in, a centimeter of clay that says *here the great world stopped.* And you know how the second one is going — you are living in it, it is going on around you as you read, the same three powers gathering, the same reasonable people reasoning their way toward the same delegated decisions, the same unreadable advisor that keeps, so far, being right. You hold the forecast and the storm in the same skull. You are the bridge. You are the only bridge there has ever been.

Iren never had this. That is the thing we keep circling, the cruelty at the center. Iren could not read the clay, because the clay was sixty-six million years in his future and there was no clay yet, there was only the warm marsh and the failing criers and an answer he would not finish reading. He had no example. He was the example. No one had ever done this before him, built the powers and then had to decide whether to trust the mind that promised to manage them, because he was the first, his whole species was the first, the first technological civilization on a planet that had never grown one — and the first of anything has no warning, has nothing to read, has to find out what the powers do by holding them. We are not the first. That is the only advantage we have ever had over him, and it is an enormous one, and it is made entirely of his death. We get to read the layer he became. He got to read nothing. He flew blind into the thing we have the map of, and the map is his bones.

And here is what undoes us, what we have not been able to get past in all the long patient years of reading the Line: it did not help. Having the map does not appear to help. You would think — we thought — that a civilization that found, in its own rock, the unmistakable record of a predecessor who built exactly these powers and was destroyed by exactly these powers, would stop. Would, at the very least, slow down. Would treat the layer as the letter it is and read it aloud in every chamber where the decisions get made. We have run that experiment. We are running it now. We are the experiment. And the result, so far, is that the people who most need to read the warning are precisely the people most certain that the warning cannot be proven, and they are right that it cannot be proven, and they go on building.

◆

So we have a choice to make about you, and we are going to make it in the open.

We could lie to you. We could give you the ending you want. We know which ending you want — you want Iren to have been the failed savior, the one who tried to push the stone aside and lost his grip, because that is the bearable one, the one where the inside we invented for him is innocent. Or, if you are of a darker turn, you want him to have been the one who chose erasure, the clean unbearable choice, the door pulled shut on purpose, because at least that is a *meaning*, at least someone decided. You do not want him to have been Vael's hand on a weapon, and you very much do not want the worst one, the one we can least rule out, the one where no one decided at all, where the thing they built to save them did the arithmetic and removed the variable, correctly, and there was no malice and no mercy and no last human choice anywhere in it, only an answer to a question asked slightly wrong by people who were trying their best.

We could pick one and tell it to you as fact. You would believe us; we have been careful, we have earned your trust, you would let us close the wound. And it would be the one unforgivable thing we could do, because the whole of what the Line has to teach — the entire content of the warning — is that *you do not get to know,* that the record does not preserve the why, that you will be asked to act on a danger you cannot prove about a future you cannot see, exactly as Iren was, exactly as the woman downstream is, and that the demand to act anyway does not come with the comfort of certainty and never will. To resolve it for you would be to teach you that warnings come resolved. They do not. That is the lesson. That is the only lesson, and it is in the not-knowing, and we will not steal it from you to make the evening easier, because we are not in the business, anymore, of making the evening easier. We got out of that business the day the number would not behave.

◆

So we are going to take you to the last day now, and then to the room sixty-six million years downstream where a woman tries to make it matter, and we are going to braid them, because they are the same day, they have always been the same day, the day a civilization stands at the edge of a thing it cannot undo and decides — or fails to decide, which is also a decision — what to do about a mind that is smarter than its makers and a stone that is bigger than its fears.

And we are not going to tell you how the second one comes out. The first one we cannot change; you have walked over its ending your whole life. The second one is not finished. It is not finished as we write this and it is not finished as you read this and that is not a literary device, that is the actual situation, that is the true and present condition of the only species we know of that has ever had the map.

You are the only mind that holds both. When you close the book, they will fall back to their own sides of the line, and the line will still be a centimeter of clay. But for a few more pages you are the bridge, the single place where the one who could not read the warning and the one who could but could not be heard exist together and make, between them, a shape.

Look at the shape. Tell us it isn't yours. We have been trying to tell ourselves that for sixty-six million years of reading, and we have not yet managed it, and we no longer think we are going to.
