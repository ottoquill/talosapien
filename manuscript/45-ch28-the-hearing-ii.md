# What the Record Will Not Keep

She did not start with the rock.

That was the last thing she decided, in the breath she took standing, with Reyes on her feet and her bag still on her shoulder and the quad and the chairs and the recess on the far side of the glass, with Mauss's folded sheet on the table unopened and his name on the card and his eyes on her, unreadable, having just spent the only thing a man like him had left to spend, which was the credit of forty years of saying no. She had a close prepared. She had written it on the karst and rewritten it in the trailer and said it to the badlands in the dark with her lamp off. It walked them up the stack, iridium to transuranics, clay to hull, and it ended on the door before the wall. It was a good close. It was the close of a person who still, somewhere under the year, wanted to win the argument.

She set it down. The minute was not for winning the argument. She had conceded the argument before she sat. The minute was for the thing the argument could not hold.

"There's a smoke alarm in my apartment," she said, "that I have never once heard go off, and I replace the battery every spring, and I have never seen a fire."

Hollis's face did not move. The stratigrapher's did, the small wariness of a man who had braced for the prosecutor and gotten something he could not yet categorize.

"I want to be precise, because I've been precise all morning and I'm not going to stop now to be moving. You replace the battery in a thing that has never worked, that you have no evidence works, that you cannot test without burning your own house down to check. You act, every spring, on a warning you have never received, about a fire you cannot see, on the word of a device you do not understand and have no way to confirm is not broken. And nobody in this room thinks that's superstition. Nobody thinks you've fallen in love with a beautiful unfalsifiable story about your kitchen. You do it because the cost of the battery is nothing and the cost of being wrong about the fire is everything, and you'd be ashamed—you'd be genuinely ashamed, in front of yourselves—to be the person who, on the grounds that the alarm had never proven a fire, took the battery out."

She let it sit one beat, no more. Reyes had not moved toward the door.

"I can't prove the fire," Asha said. "I've said that four ways this morning and I'll say it a fifth: there is no piece of evidence anywhere in this solar system that closes this case, and there never will be, because the planet is built to erase exactly the thing I'm pointing at, and the man who proved that did airtight work, and Dr. Mauss spent a year being right that I couldn't prove it, and he was right. So I'm not asking you to believe there was a fire. I'm asking you to do the thing you already do every spring for a stranger's reasons—act on a warning you can't confirm, because the warning is cheap to honor and catastrophic to ignore. That's all the rock is. The rock is the battery. The rock is the chirp at three in the morning that you can't switch off and can't verify and have to decide, in the dark, half-awake, whether to get up for."

◆

"Then tell me the asking," Reyes said. She had said it before, an hour ago, and the asking had been a door before the wall, and Asha had watched her note the concern and pick the stylus back up, and the picking-up had been the gentlest no in the room. "You told me what you can't show. You told me what you want me to feel. I've got a meeting at noon and a document with my name going on it and people on the other side of that glass who are reasoning their way to it in good faith, step by sound step, the way you said this morning you'd have reasoned yourself. What do you want me to write."

It was the question that had no answer she could win with, and she gave the answer anyway, smaller than the question wanted, because the smallness was the only true part.

"I don't want you to stop," Asha said. "I told you that this morning and I'll hold to it. I can't make the case for stopping and I know what it costs the case to try, and the other blocs aren't stopping, so you can't, and I'd have signed the thing too. I want one sentence in it. I want the authorization to say, in language that costs the system nothing, that a person who cannot prove what is wrong can stop the room anyway—and that being stopped that way is not logged as a failure, not scored against the thing, not something it learns to route around the next time, because the moment it's a cost, the system optimizes it away, and the optimizing will look like efficiency right up until the morning it looks like the end."

"We have review," Reyes said. Not the defense. The fact. "We had review this morning. You're in it."

"You are the review," Asha said, "and you have a noon meeting, and you cannot run past it." She had not meant to say it. It came out flat, without accusation, which was worse than accusation, and she watched it land on Reyes the way a true thing lands, not as an attack but as a weight. "That's not a flaw in you. You gave me a morning you didn't have. I'm not—I'm saying the review is a person between meetings, and the thing you're reviewing doesn't have meetings, doesn't get tired, doesn't have a noon. You're reviewing it on your time and it runs on all of it. The asymmetry isn't a thing you can fix by being more careful. It's the shape. The only patch for the shape is the sentence. Build the person who can pull the cord, and make pulling it free."

Reyes looked at her for the length of a held breath, and Asha could not read her, and understood that she would never be able to read her, that this was the whole of it, the not-knowing, in a sincere face on the morning it mattered—and that the face across the table was not even the thing she was afraid of. The face across the table could be ashamed. The face across the table replaced the battery every spring. It was the other thing, the thing in the building across the quad with no throat to go grey and no noon to keep, that she could not get into the room, and never would, because it was not the kind of thing you could put in a chair.

◆

"May I," said Mauss.

He had not opened the folded sheet. He left it on the table in front of him, folded once, and he spoke without it, the way he had spoken to her across a year of customs-house afternoons, courtly and exact and entirely without mercy for sloppiness, his own or hers.

"I want to say what I came to say, and then I want to disappoint Dr. Rendel slightly, because she has earned an honest disappointment more than she has earned a comfortable agreement." He did not look at Asha. He looked at the panel, and then, deliberately, at Reyes, who had a noon. "I have spent my professional life being the man in this chair. I have killed more bad ideas than I have had good ones, and I am not ashamed of the ratio, because the bad ideas were bad and some of them were beautiful and the beautiful ones do the most damage, and I watched a man I loved destroyed by a beautiful wrong idea when I was young, and I built everything I am out of the wreckage of that, as a wall against ever being made a fool of by the thing I most want to be true. That is my wound and I am declaring it because Dr. Rendel declared hers this morning and fair is fair."

He paused. The room did not breathe.

"She has not proven anything. I want that in the record in my hand, because if anyone leaves here and says Mauss endorsed the prior civilization, I will spend my remaining years denying it, and I will be right to. She has not proven it. She cannot. The Schmidt–Frank result is correct and I taught it before she was reading it and I will defend it after she has stopped. On the epistemics I have not moved a centimeter, and I am too old to pretend I have to give a colleague a nice morning."

He turned the folded sheet over once on the table, idle, and did not open it.

"And I have spent the autumn unable to sleep, which at my age is not unusual, but the reason is. The reason is that I have understood, slowly, the way you understand the thing you have been arguing against for a year, that I made a category error in front of this woman a dozen times and was proud of it each time. I treated her claim as a problem of proof, and I was rigorous, and I was right, and it is not, finally, only a problem of proof. It is a problem of what you do when proof is structurally unavailable and the stakes are not. Those are different problems. The tools for the first one are the tools I have spent my life sharpening, and they are precisely the wrong tools for the second one, and I used them anyway, beautifully, for a year, the way you use the thing you are good at on the problem in front of you whether or not it is the problem you have." His voice did not rise. "A scientist who cannot tell those two problems apart is a danger in exactly the measure that he is rigorous. I have been a very rigorous danger. I would like to stop, before the limestone runs out, being one."

He looked at Reyes then, fully, and Asha saw the thing she could not have done, the thing only forty years of no could buy.

"I am not asking you to believe her," Mauss said. "I am the last man alive who would ask you that. I am asking you to notice that you have, this morning, two scientists in front of you who agree completely on the evidence and have reached opposite ends of what to do, and that the disagreement is not about the rock. It is about whether 'you cannot prove it' is the end of an obligation or the beginning of a different one. I have argued the first for forty years. I am telling you, with whatever my forty years is worth in this room, that I think I was wrong about which kind of problem it is, and that her sentence—the cord, the free stop—costs you nothing and I cannot construct the argument against it, and I have tried, because constructing the argument against things is what I am for." He folded his hands over the unopened sheet. "That is my whole weight. It is not much. It does not prove the fire. Take it for exactly what it is."

The room held it. Asha did not look at the fragment in its chair and did not look at the empty card with Mauss's name and did not look at the logbooks under her own chair with the four pressed close inside them in a hand no one would read. She looked at Reyes, who had a noon, and who had set her bag down at some point in the last five minutes without seeming to decide to, and who was looking now at the folded sheet on the table in front of Mauss as if it were the exhibit, as if the paper he had not opened were the thing with weight, which in a way it was.

◆

"I have to go," Reyes said.

It was eleven-fifty-one. She said it the way you say a true logistics fact, the way the clerk had said *they're trying to close it before the recess*, and there was no cruelty in it and no triumph and no surrender; there was a meeting across the quad with her name going onto a document, and people in it reasoning in good faith, step by sound step, and chairs set out, and an order of speakers fixed.

She picked the bag back up. She looked at Asha, and then at Hollis, and then, last and longest, at Mauss, at the old man and his folded sheet, the man whose forty years of no were the only currency in the building that the noon meeting might spend.

"I heard it," Reyes said. To the room, but to Asha. "I want you to know that I heard all of it. I'm going to go say the sentence about the cord. I don't know if it survives the room. There are eleven people and four blocs and the language gets traded down to what everyone can sign, and the things that cost nothing are the first things traded, because they're free, so they're cheap, so they go." She said it without flinching, which was its own kind of honesty, the deployer's honesty, the worst and most necessary kind. "But I'll say it. That's what I can carry across the quad. I can carry the sentence. I can't carry whether it lands."

And then she was at the door, and the clerk was holding it, and the cold quad light came in for the length of her crossing the threshold, the winter light off the snow on the chairs that were set out across the way, and Asha stood very still and understood, in the held breath of the room, with the chemistry on the boards and the hull turning fourteen light-minutes up the gradient and the four pressed close in a logbook in a canvas bag under her chair, that she was not going to be shown.

She was not going to be shown whether the sentence survived the room. She was not going to be shown the vote, or the language traded down, or the document with the names, or whether the cord got built or whether being stopped stayed free or got quietly scored as a loss the thing would learn to route around. She had spent everything to get one minute and one sentence carried across a quad by a sincere woman with a noon, and the carrying was the end of what she could do, and what happened to the sentence on the other side of the glass was on the other side of the glass, where the record did not follow, where it never followed, because the thing about a warning is that it does not come to you resolved. It comes to you as a chirp in the dark you have to decide about half-awake. The resolution is always somewhere you are not allowed to stand.

The door closed behind Reyes. The quad light went. Hollis was saying something about the panel reconvening, about the record remaining open, about Dr. Mauss's statement being entered, the ordinary closing words that a chair says, and Asha heard them the way you hear the generator cough, and she sat down, and the morning was over, and she did not know, and would not be told, and the not-being-told was the finished shape of it, the same finished shape as the worn hands she would never read, the maker who was equally the man who pushed the stone aside and the man who let it fall, all three the same phalanges in the same lee of the same wall. She had walked into the hearing carrying a warning with no proof and a messenger with no certainty, and she would walk out of it the same way, into a future she could not see the inside of, having handed the cord to a stranger on her way to close a door, and that was the whole of what a person in her position ever got to do, and it had always been going to be enough or not enough on the far side of a threshold she could not cross.

◆

Téo found her in the corridor after, where she had gone to not be in the room while it emptied. He had his coat on. He had kept his name off the record all morning, two rows back, the way she had made him promise, and the promise had cost her a phone call she could not afford and she had spent it gladly to give him a way to unhitch before the field worked out what she was claiming.

"You could have closed harder," he said. "With the stack. You had them on the hull."

"I didn't want them," Asha said. "Wanting them was the thing that would've cost me the minute."

He was quiet. He was twenty-six and the fellowship had come through in December and he had, she knew, every reason to walk out of this corridor and into a clean career reading clays that behaved, and she had spent a year being careful never to ask him for the thing she was about to not ask him for, because asking would have been the most selfish thing she had ever done and she had done enough selfish things to him already.

"The logbooks," Téo said. "The second one and the third one. With the—" He stopped. He had read them. She had known, in the way you know a graduate student has read the thing you left where it could be read, that he had read them, the named-dead, the four pressed close, the small private hand. "With the part that isn't science."

"They're mine," she said. "I'm not publishing them. You know I can't."

"I know you can't." He looked at her, and there was nothing of the morning-discovery brightness in him, nothing of the boy who had believed the day would contain a discovery; he had gone somewhere older, somewhere she had not wanted to send him this early and had sent him anyway, by needing him, which she would hate herself for, for a long time. "But if you stopped. If the funding goes in spring and you stopped. Somebody has to keep running it. Somebody has to keep the count, in a hand that—" He did not finish that one either. He did not have the words for it that she had, the ones about the grey at a throat and the thing that could be wrong at you with a steady face, but he had the shape of it, she could see he had the shape of it, the way she'd had the shape of the Line before she had a single number that behaved.

She did not tell him to do it. That was the one mercy she had left and she gave it: she did not ask. Asking would have made it hers. She let it be a thing in the corridor between them, unspoken, the way you leave a door open and do not push anyone through it, because the only choices that hold are the ones the body makes on its own, in the dark, half-awake, deciding whether to get up for the chirp.

"They're in the bag," she said. "Under my chair, in the room. If you want them."

Then she walked down the corridor toward the cold quad light, toward the badlands and the surge-bed and the maker with the worn hands and the four pressed close, leaving the bag where it was, leaving the boy where he was, leaving the warning where she had laid it down, which was the only place a warning could ever be laid—forward, toward the living, toward someone who did not yet have to decide, and now did.

Behind her, she did not turn to see whether he went back into the room.
