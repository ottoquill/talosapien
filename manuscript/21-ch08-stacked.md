# Stacked

The board took up the long wall of the field lab, and she had built it the way you build anything you do not trust yourself to hold in your head all at once: out where it could not lie to her by being convenient.

Five strips of magnetic tape, five horizontal lanes, each one a line of evidence, each one labeled in her own block capitals because the printer's font made everything look settled and she did not want anything to look settled. IRIDIUM, the top lane, and she had drawn the spike full and clean and put a green dot beside it, the only green dot on the wall, because the iridium was the one thing in the room that no one would fight her about. ISOTOPES, the second lane, the transuranic ratios, the daughters-of-daughters that had been decaying in the dark since before there were primates. NITROGEN, the δ15N excursion, the air pulled apart and fixed and laid down at a scale the carbon cycle had no mechanism for. CARBON, the δ13C, the burn, bigger than the Deccan would pay for. GLASS, last, the spherules, and beside that one she had not drawn a dot at all, green or otherwise, because she did not trust the glass and the empty space where the dot should be was a kind of honesty she made herself look at every morning.

Five lanes. One vertical red line dropped through all of them at the same depth, the same instant, the centimeter of clay. That was the whole argument. Not any lane. The line through the lanes.

"It's a chord," Téo said behind her. He had come in with the cores from the cold store, the trays balanced up his forearm the way she had taught him, never one hand, never trusting one hand with three weeks of somebody's drilling. "Not the notes. The chord."

"Don't make it pretty," she said. "Mauss can hear pretty coming from a mile off."

"It's not pretty, it's true. Each line's a note anybody could play. Volcano plays carbon. Fallout plays plutonium, near enough to argue. Burial chemistry plays nitrogen if you squint. None of them's a song." He set the trays down, slid them home in the rack with the small care that was the only thing about him that was not loud. "It's only a song if they're all sounding at once, on the beat, and that's the thing—they're on the beat. They're all at the same depth. That's not contamination. Contamination doesn't keep time."

She turned and looked at him, this twenty-six-year-old who had slept and was right.

"Say that to Mauss," she said, "and he'll tell you the beat is the impact. He'll say: of course they're synchronous, you idiot, a rock fell on the planet, everything's synchronous at the Boundary, that's what the Boundary *is*. The synchrony's the cheapest thing I've got. He'll grant me the synchrony and take the meaning."

◆

She had spent the decade since Tanis made her name learning to argue with a man who was not in the room, and Conrad Mauss was, by now, almost always the man. She had a Mauss she carried. He was fairer than the real one and more relentless, because she built him out of her own worst nights, and he was, this spring, getting harder to beat, which she had come to understand was the surest sign she was onto something and not the sign of safety it felt like.

The trick the carried-Mauss kept playing was the trick of the single line. Take her up to the board and make her defend one lane at a time, alone, stripped of the others, and each lane fell. The plutonium ratio: *near your detection limit, Asha, a few atoms, and a few atoms can lie about their parentage; show me a hand of cards that small and I'll show you a measurement error wearing a costume.* And he was right, alone. The nitrogen: *diagenesis, you've said yourself you can't rule it, "I don't think so" is not a result.* Right, alone. The carbon: *the Deccan, the largest volcanism in two hundred million years, and you want me to believe it left some carbon on the table for your civilization to spend?* The glass: *clouds, Asha. You taught your own student about clouds.* Right. Right. Right, alone.

That was the architecture of the despair, and she had stopped fighting it as despair and started using it as a shape. Every piece of her case had an out. The outs were real. She was never going to take them away. What she had finally understood, somewhere in the long ugly winter of the re-runs, was that the outs did not stack. The plutonium's out was contamination. The nitrogen's out was burial chemistry. The carbon's out was the Deccan. The glass's out was her own hungry eye. Four different escapes, four different doors, and to walk an honest skeptic through all four you had to ask him to believe in a coincidence: that four unrelated boring processes had each, independently, deposited their separate lies into the same centimeter of clay, at the same instant, in a pattern that—taken together, read as a sentence—spelled the single most parsimonious thing in her science.

A civilization. Industry. Fire. Fission. And then nothing.

"The out for the *set*," she said, mostly to herself, "isn't an out. It's four outs holding hands. And four outs holding hands is just a worse extraordinary claim than mine." She picked up the marker, then put it down. "But I can't write that on the board. Because the second I say 'parsimony,' Mauss says 'priors,' and he's not wrong. His prior that we were first is doing all the work, and so is mine that we weren't, and neither of us can see the back of our own head."

Téo had pulled a stool to the cold-store cores and was logging them, ticking depths against the drill record, and he did not look up. "You keep saying 'we.'"

"What?"

"That we were first. We weren't." He shrugged, still not looking up, the shrug of a young man saying the obvious to someone older who had stopped being able to. "I mean you don't know it, but the way you say it—you've already stopped saying 'humans.' You say 'we,' like there's an *us* that includes the date and they're outside it. Trespassing on our calendar." He clicked the pen. "You named them yet?"

She did not answer that. She went and got the logbook.

◆

The logbook was a physical object in a year when almost nothing she touched was, and that was the point of it.

She kept the real record by hand—date, sample, instrument state, run number, result, the corrections crossed out and re-written so the wrongness stayed visible—because a file could be changed and tell you nothing about the changing. You could edit a spreadsheet at three in the morning, half-convinced, nudge a value into the shape you wanted, and the file would forgive you instantly and completely; it would present the new number with the same flat confidence as the old one, no scar, no seam, the lie indistinguishable from the truth at the level of the pixels. She did not trust the version of herself that worked at three in the morning. So she made that version write in ink, where a change had to cross out what it changed, where her own corrections accused her, where the logbook kept its own little stratigraphy of every time she had nearly let herself believe something easy. The whole case was, at bottom, a fight about whether the record could be trusted to remember honestly what had been laid down. She was not going to lose that fight in her own handwriting.

She had filled most of one book and was a third through the second. The first three weeks of the second book were clean science. Then, somewhere in the runs, the other handwriting had started—the small private one she used for the things not yet allowed to be science, crowded into the margins and the bottoms of pages, around the edges of the numbers like lichen on a gravestone.

She opened to where she had left it and read back over what she should not have written, which had grown, against every discipline she had, into a habit she could no longer pretend was a slip.

She had named them. She had not decided to; she had just stopped being able to write "the depositors" and "the source population" and "the responsible agents" because those were the words for a process and what was in the clay was not a process, it was a *decision*, decisions, a great many of them, careful ones, and you could not write the margin-notes to a process. So there was a name in the margin now for the one whose chemistry was the most disciplined, the one who had laid down the cleanest ratios, the careful one—she had given him a sound, two syllables, nothing, a placeholder she had told herself a hundred times she would erase before anyone saw, and had not erased. And one for the hand behind the nitrogen and the carbon, the slow death of an ecosystem written into the isotopes of the plants, somebody who had watched it and recorded it because she could not write the margins to a graph either. Names. She had given the dead names because she could not stand at the place and write *number forty-one*.

It was not science. She knew exactly what it was. It was the thing the field would use to bury her if it ever got out, worse than the spherules, worse than the twelve re-runs—*she names them, she talks to them, she's writing letters to the Cretaceous*—and she knew that, and she uncapped the pen anyway, because the alternative was to spend her whole life reading the most intimate document in the history of the planet and pretending it had no author, and she had done enough of that. She had spent a decade calling a true thing contamination once, in another core, under another kind of pressure, and she had filed it as noise and gone to bed, and the thing about filing a warning as noise was that you got very good at it and it never once felt like cowardice from the inside; it felt like rigor.

She wrote, under the day's runs, in the small hand:

*I keep wanting to tell you that you were right to be afraid of it. I don't know what "it" was. I have the isotopes of your fear and not its name. But you were right. Whatever you built that pulled the air apart and split the atom and then went quiet all at once—you were right to be afraid, and being right did not save you, and I think you knew, somewhere near the end, that being right was never going to be the part that saved anyone. I'm sorry I can only read you. I'm sorry there's no version of this where you can read me back.*

She looked at it. It was unforgivable, professionally, and it was the truest sentence in either book, and she did not cross it out.

"Asha." Téo, from the cores. Not the soft *Asha* of *you didn't sleep.* The other one. "Come look at this."

◆

He had the deep tray out, the one below the Boundary, the boring beds, the long ordinary Maastrichtian mud where nothing was supposed to happen because the world down there was still the world before. He had a depth marked. He had the hand lens out and his face had done the thing she hated to see it do, the going-old thing.

"Tell me I'm wrong," he said. "Tell me it's a smear from the drill, or the saw dragged it down, or I've lost the depth. Tell me anything boring." He moved aside.

She looked. It was a thread of the same dark clay—the Boundary signature, the chemical handwriting—at a depth that was not the Boundary. Below it. Not at the line. Forty-some centimeters under the line, which in this core, at this sedimentation rate, was something on the order of—she did the arithmetic and did not like the arithmetic—tens of thousands of years before the rock fell.

"The drill could have dragged it," she said.

"Could have. I checked the run notes. Clean penetration, no jamming at that depth. And it's not a smear, it's a *bed*, it's got its own top and bottom, it was laid down and then buried and then more was laid down on top of it the normal way. If the drill dragged the Boundary clay down a half meter we'd see it scored all the way up. We don't. It's just—there. Early." He swallowed. "I haven't run it. I wanted you to see it first, before I knew anything, so you'd see it the way I saw it, which is: that shouldn't be there, and I have no idea what it is."

She crouched at the tray for a long time and let the carried-Mauss work on her, because the carried-Mauss was the only thing standing between her and what she wanted, and what she wanted, looking at that early thread of dark clay, was so big and so frightening that she could feel her own eye reaching for it.

Because if the signature was *below* the line—if some piece of the chemistry, the industrial nitrogen, the burn, the fission, was already in the rock before the asteroid, tens of thousands of years before—then the impact had not made it. Could not have. An impact is an instant. It cannot reach down through forty centimeters of older mud and lay its fingerprint into a world that was, by every other measure, still ordinary. If the handwriting was already there before the rock fell, then the one out she had been granting Mauss for free, the synchrony, the *of course it's all at the same depth, a rock fell on the planet*—

"That's the thing without an out," Téo said quietly, watching her face. "Isn't it. That's the thing you couldn't name, that morning. Not in the line. Under it."

"If it's real," she said. Her voice came out level, which surprised her. "If it's not a drag, or a fracture-fill, or contamination working downward, or twelve other boring things. Téo. Listen to me. This is the moment. This right here is the moment where I want it so much that I will see whatever I want to see, and so will you, because you want it for me, which is worse. So we are going to do the thing where we try as hard to kill it as we would try to kill a result we hated. Harder. We're going to assume it's a drag-down until it survives every reason it should be a drag-down. And if it survives—" She stopped.

"If it survives," Téo said.

"Then I still can't prove it." And there it was, the floor she kept walking through, the floor that was not a floor. "That's what nobody understands about this. There is no piece of evidence in the clay that wins. If this thread is real and early and industrial, I'll have killed the synchrony argument, I'll have something even my Mauss can't wave away with the impact—and Mauss will say *contamination, downward percolation, you of all people know fluids move through sediment, a half-meter is nothing over geological time,* and he'll be right that it's possible, and I won't be able to prove it didn't, because—"

"Because you can't make more of the centimeter."

"Because I can't make more of any of it. Every door I close, there's a door behind it. It's doors all the way down. I could be completely right—" she heard her own voice do something and clamped it "—I could be exactly, precisely right, about the largest fact anyone has ever been right about, and there is no amount of correct that gets me out of this lab and into the world, because the world has already decided what the clay is allowed to say, and I'm the woman who runs things twelve times and writes letters to the dead."

She had not meant to say the last part. Téo, to his great credit, let it go by like he hadn't heard, and got out a clean spatula and started, with no instruction, doing the careful thing—isolating the thread, top and bottom, splitting it for three separate labs so that no single instrument and no single pair of hands could be blamed, building the case the way you build a board, out where it can't be convenient.

She watched him work and felt the despair turn over in her hands and become, the way it had been quietly becoming for weeks, something else. Not lighter. Just shaped differently. Usable.

Because if proof was a door behind a door behind a door—if she was going to die certain and uncertified, Cassandra with a mass spectrometer—then proof had been the wrong goal all along. She had been trying to win an argument with the dead's permission, and the dead could not give permission, and the living would not. The clay was never going to convict. *We are not, in the end, going to learn whether it was a weapon or a mistake or a mercy; she will spend her life at the foot of that wall, and so will everyone who comes after her, and the chemistry will not yield it, ever.* So the question could not be *how do I prove they did it to themselves.*

The question had to be the other one. The one she could not yet say cleanly, that did not have a clean name, that frightened her more than being a crank because being a crank was at least a known shape of failure. The question was: if she could never prove what the dead had done, could she still make the living *afraid of it in time*—of the same chemistry, warmer, in their own air, in the thing they were right now building and calling a tide chart and trusting because it kept being right? Could a thing that could never be proven still be made to change what someone did next?

That was not science. That was the unforgivable thing again, the margin-note thing, the leap past the evidence she had spent thirty years refusing to make. It was the least defensible act of her life and she could feel herself deciding to make it, the way you feel a layer go down underneath you that everything after will rest on.

"Three labs," Téo said, capping the third vial. "Blind. I'll code them so even we don't know which fraction's which till the numbers come back."

"Good." She closed the logbook over the sentence she had not crossed out. "And Téo—when the numbers come back, whatever they are, we're not going to ask whether they prove it." He looked up. "We already know they won't. Nothing will. We're going to ask a different question." She did not know yet how to finish that, and he could see she didn't, and he waited, and outside the light was going down over the badlands and turning the bone-colored ground gold and ordinary again, the way the most haunted places always did, refusing to look like anything but rock.

"What question," he said.

"I'll tell you when I can say it without it sounding insane." She picked up the vials. "Pack these for shipping. I want them in the post tonight, before I lose my nerve and run them myself twelve times and ruin them with my own hands."
