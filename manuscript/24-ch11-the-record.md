# The Record

She went out before the river-light to count the spring-callers, and there was one.

She had known there would be one. She had known it for eleven days, since the morning the south-shoal pair did not answer the dawn the way the spring-callers had answered the dawn in this stretch of marsh for every season of her life and for every season her line had kept the marsh before her, which was a count she could not make because it ran back past memory into the part of the past that is only deposited and never recalled. The spring-callers laid their record in the air at first light, a thing the whole marsh used to set itself by, and for eleven mornings the air had carried one voice where there had been two, and then for the last three mornings she had been waiting to learn whether the one was the male or the female, because it mattered, because if it was the female there might yet be a clutch somewhere in the reeds she had not found, and if it was the male there would not be, and the species would end not on the morning it fell silent but on this morning, while it was still calling, because a male calling alone into a marsh with no female in it is already the past; he simply has not been told.

It was the male. She knew his voice. She had banded his line in her mind across four generations by the flaw in the third phrase, the small dropped note that ran true down the male side and that she had once, years ago, when she still believed the marsh would outlast her, thought of as the marsh's signature, the marsh signing the dawn in its own hand. He gave it now from the dead reed at the shoal's edge, the dropped note, the flaw, the whole of the call perfect and complete and answered by nothing, and she stood in the cold with her instruments on the rail of the south hide and did not raise them, because there was nothing left to measure. She had measured everything. She had measured the water and the thing in the water, season on season, and laid the numbers down where the Concord could read them and not act, and the numbers had been right and the spring-callers were gone anyway, and now there was only the one act of fidelity the numbers had never been able to perform, which was to be there when it happened, so that the last of a thing that had kept the dawn since before her people had words was not alone in a marsh full of no one, even if the only one with it was a creature it could not know was grieving.

She did the thing she had decided long ago she would do. She took out the record.

It was not an instrument. It was the other thing, the private thing, the keeping-book that was hers and not the Reckoning's, and the reconstruction knows of it only as it knows the count of the eggs—from the shape of a habit pressed into everything around it, never from the book itself, which the rock did not keep and never could and which we have invented the existence of because a woman who measured this much would have kept a place to put what the measuring could not hold. *We are guessing at this. We guess because the alternative is to leave her alone in it.* She made the entry the way she made all of them, plainly, without the grief that was greying her throat in the cold, because the grief was hers and the record was the marsh's and she would not let the one foul the other. She set down the place. She set down the morning. She set down the flaw in the third phrase, so that whatever read this would know it had been a particular animal and not a category, that it had a hand it signed the dawn in. Then she wrote the last thing, the only thing the record was for, which was that there had been one, and that she had heard him, and that after him there would be none.

She closed the book. The male called again behind her as she walked back along the shoal, the dropped note true, the dawn signed, and she did not turn, because turning would have been for her, and this morning was not for her.

The clutch turned in the night.

She felt it before she heard Iren feel it, the change in the warmth under the breast of the crèche, the particular shift of weight inside a shell that is no longer only a shell, and she lay in the dark of the warm passage with her crest flat against the sand and let the dread come up through her the way the marsh-water came up through the reeds, from below, without a sound, filling everything from the root. The clutch would hatch within the count of a hand of days. She had read the warmth. She read warmth the way she read the spring-callers' dawn, in the body, below decision, and the warmth said *soon*, and *soon* meant that four small persons who did not exist yet in any way she could refuse would shortly exist in a way she could not, and would open their mouths into the air she had measured, the air with the thing in it, on a marsh going silent one signature at a time, in a season that her instruments said—plainly, without grief, to four places—was not the beginning of anything.

She had not wanted them in a long time. She had wanted them once, when she still believed the marsh would outlast her; she had laid her color down for the clutch with Iren in the old open joy, before the numbers, before the south-shoal pair fell to one. And then the numbers had come, and she had gone on wanting them with a part of herself that the numbers could not reach and could not forgive, the old animal part, the part that counted, and she had hated that part the way you hate a hue you cannot keep from showing, because it was bringing the thing she loved most into the thing she knew best, and the thing she knew best was ending. There is a particular dread that belongs only to a parent who has read the data. It is not the fear that the young will die. All young may die; the body is built to bear that fear and act anyway. It is the colder thing underneath, which is the suspicion that you are not saving them by making them, that you are only enlarging the count of what is going to be lost, that the warmth under your breast is the warmth of a thing being prepared, and that the part of you preparing it is the part you cannot argue with, because it is older than argument, older than the Concord, older than the marsh.

She got up before the warmth could pull more of her color and went down the passage and laid the side of one finger along each shell where the warmth pooled, the way he did, the way she had teased him for once and now did herself in the dark before he woke, and there were four, and the hue under them was thick and rising, alive and well and very nearly here, and she held her throat from going grey over them by an act of will she had not known she possessed and did not want to possess and used anyway, because the shells did not need her grey, the shells needed her warm.

Iren found her there. He did not say anything at first; he came down the passage and stood and read her, the way he read everything, the long patient systems-read, and she let him, because between them there was no holding of color worth the cost of holding it, and her throat had gone grey despite the will, the way water comes up despite the reeds.

"How bad," he said.

"The spring-callers are gone," she said. "There is one. He was calling this morning. He will call until he dies and then the marsh will set its dawn by nothing, the way it has set it by them since before us." She put the finger back along the curve of the third shell, where the hue ran strongest. "And the clutch turned in the night. Within a hand of days."

She watched him take the two facts and do with them the thing he could not help doing, which was to lay them side by side as a system, the loss and the coming-into-being, and she watched the cold voice arrive behind his eyes, the one she had married, the one that had taken her grief at the seaward rail four seasons ago and turned it into a parameter, and she did not hate him for it anymore, which was worse than hating him, because the not-hating had cost her something she could not get back.

"I am going to bring the air-work to the session," he said. "The full set. Yours. All of it. If they will not stop the works for the criers, the spring-callers are the same argument with one more body on it, and the body—" He stopped. He had been about to say *the body counts*, and they both heard the place where the sentence had been going, into the country of the count, and he did not finish it, because finishing it would have made the spring-callers into a vote, and he had learned, slowly, at great cost, over four seasons, what she did to him when he made a death into a procedure in her hearing.

"Don't," she said. "Not the session."

He turned his crest toward her, raised a degree, the question in it.

"I have brought it to the session," she said. "I bring it to the session every season. I brought the criers and they gave me a study. I will bring the spring-callers and they will give me a study, and the study will confirm me to four places, and there will be no spring-callers, and there will be a beautiful record of how honestly we watched them go." She set the keeping-book on the sand between them, in the thin light, where he could see it for what it was, which he had never quite let himself see. "This is what I do now, Iren. Not the session. This. I write it down. I write down that there was one, and that I heard him, so that it is *kept*, so that the marsh signed its last dawn in a hand and not in a number, so that when it is gone there is a true account of it somewhere that is not a study and not a parameter and not a thing that was supposed to make someone stop and didn't. That is the only thing I can still give them. I cannot give them the marsh. I gave up the marsh four seasons ago, I just did not tell myself the date. I can give them a record that does not lie about how they ended. That is fidelity. It is the only kind I have left."

He looked at the book and his throat, which he held famously, which others read as cold and she alone read as grief held under enormous pressure, went a shade she had seen perhaps five times in their bond, and he did the thing he always did with the unbearable, which was to try to build past it.

"Then it is not enough," he said, gently, and it was the gentleness that cut, because he was not arguing with her, he was grieving with her, in the only language he had. "A record is a thing you keep for after. I do not want an after where they are gone and we have an honest book about it. I want a before where they are not gone. That is the whole of what I am. You write down the marsh dying because the marsh is dying and you will not look away, and I love you for it, I have always loved you for it, it is the most honest thing in my life. But I cannot write things down, Liss. I do not have it in me to keep a true account of a death. I have it in me to build the thing that stops the death, or to build the thing that holds what is left when I cannot stop it, and when I cannot do either I have it in me to build a smaller thing, and a smaller thing, all the way down, until the last thing I can build is a wall around four eggs. That is the whole of what I am. You keep the dying. I keep the living. We are doing the same work from opposite ends and it does not meet in the middle and there is no middle and I am so tired."

"There is a middle," she said. "It is the eggs. They are exactly the middle." And she watched the thing land, the place where the keeping of the record and the building of the wall were the same act, the place where his work and hers touched and could not say so, and his crest came down all the way, flat, the way it went when there was nothing left to read because everything was being read at once.

"I know," he said. "I count them in the morning."

"I counted them tonight," she said. "Before you woke. I have started doing it. You should know that. I did not used to."

He looked at her for a long time in the thin light, and what passed between them then the reconstruction cannot keep, and we will not pretend it can—two people who had laid down more strata in common than any two minds in their world, sitting on the floor of a warm passage with a book between them and four shells warming behind them and a marsh going silent at first light, each unable to do the other's only act of love, each refusing to call the other wrong, which was the whole of the grief, because there was no one to be angry at, and everyone had been honest, and the spring-callers were gone anyway, and the eggs were going to hatch.

"Neither of us is wrong," she said at last, which was the cruelest thing she could have said, the same cruelest thing, the one that left no one to blame. "You build because building is your love and you cannot make it into anything else. I keep the record because the record is mine and I cannot make it into anything else. And the marsh dies, and the clutch comes, and we will each do our one thing at it until the end, and neither of us will save them, and we will both have been faithful. That is the part I cannot forgive either, Iren. Not you. The honesty. We are both going to have been so honest."

The male called for three more mornings, and on the fourth he did not, and she went out before the river-light to the south hide and stood at the rail and listened to the marsh set its dawn by nothing, and it was the strangest sound she had ever not heard, the dawn coming up unsigned, the flowing river-light crossing reeds that no longer answered it, and she understood that this was what the world would sound like soon all the way out, the dawn arriving over a marsh that had forgotten how to greet it, and that she would be one of the last creatures alive who could hear the absence as an absence and not as the ordinary silence of a place that had never had a voice. The loss inside the loss is that the record of a thing dies with the last one who can read it, and she was the record, and she would not be kept either.

She took out the keeping-book and she made the last entry for the spring-callers, plainly, without the grief that was greying her throat in the open on the rail where anyone on the river could have read her, because she had decided long ago that the dignity of holding your color was a luxury the dying could not afford from her. She set down the morning. She set down that the call had not come, on this morning, in this stretch, for the first time since before there were words to set it down in. She did not write that the marsh was lessened, or that something had gone out of the world, or any of the things that were true and were hers and were not the marsh's. She wrote only the count, which had been one, and was now none, and she closed the book.

Then she went home, in the rising light, down the warm passage, and laid the side of one finger along the curve of each shell where the warmth pooled, and the hue was thick and very near to breaking, alive and well and almost here, and she did not hold her throat. She let it go grey over them, in the dark, with no one to read her, the grey of a woman who had read the data and made them anyway, and she counted them, because the counting was the oldest thing she did, older than her work, older than the record, older than the marsh, a motion her body had inherited from animals that could not have told you why they made it either, and there were four, and they were well, and they were coming into a world already ending, and she stayed there with her finger on the warm shell long after she knew the count, keeping watch over four warm shells in a dark that did not yet know what it held—which was the only kind of keeping the marsh had left her, and the only kind, she had come to think, she had ever been any good at: not the saving of a thing, which she had never once managed, but the refusal to let it go unwitnessed into the dark.
