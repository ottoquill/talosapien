# The Count

He counted the eggs first, before anything else, before the reports and the river-light and the thing the Augur had said in the night that he had not yet let himself look at. Four. There were always four, and he counted them anyway, every morning, with the side of one finger laid along the curve of each shell where the warmth pooled, and the counting was not for information. He knew there were four. The counting was the oldest thing he did, older than his work, older than the Concord, a motion his body had inherited from animals that could not have told you why they made it either, and it steadied him the way the deposition-rite was supposed to steady the whole of his people and increasingly did not.

The crèche was warm and low and smelled of sand and of the particular dry sweetness of shell, and the light came in long through the seaward apertures, broken by the slow traffic of the river outside into a moving grille that crossed the floor and crossed the clutch and crossed his hands. In that light the shells were not the color you would think. We have to stop here, because we cannot tell you the color. He saw a fourth thing in it that we have no cone in our eyes to receive and no word in our language to hold, a register above the blue where the shell answered the river, and to him the eggs were lit from within by a hue that meant *alive, and well, and not yet hatched,* the way a particular green means *spring* to you without your deciding it should. When the hue thinned, the egg was failing. He had buried a clutch once, years and another life ago, by that thinning, in this same sand, and he counted partly to feel the hue stay thick, and we are telling you this although the rock does not say it, because a man who counts is counting against something, and the something is always the same.

There were four, and they were well, and he let himself be glad in the brief uncomplicated way the body allows before the mind arrives, and then the mind arrived, and he went out to read what the world had become while he slept.

◆

Liss was already at the seaward rail with her instruments, and she did not turn when he came up beside her, which was its own kind of greeting between them, the greeting of two people who have stopped needing to perform arrival. The crest along her nape was half-raised in concentration and the long feathers there caught the river-light and threw it, and at her throat — he saw it before she could have hidden it, and she was not trying to hide it — the color had gone toward grey.

"How bad," he said. Not a question. With Liss you did not ask whether; the body had already told you whether; you asked how much.

"The river-criers are down again," she said. "Forty of the long-marsh flocks last season. Eleven this season, and it is not yet the dry. I counted them myself from the south hide. I did not trust the tallies." She set the instrument down on the rail with a care that he understood was anger. Liss was gentle with instruments the way other people were gentle with the throats of those they wanted to strangle. "They are not dying of the dry, Iren. The dry has not come. They are dying of the thing in the water that we made, that we are still, this morning, while I am telling you, choosing not to stop making, because stopping is expensive and the criers do not vote."

He looked out at the marsh. He could not see the criers, or their absence; he was not a field eye, he could not read eleven against forty in a wing-beat the way she could, the way it was bred into her line and trained on top of the breeding until she was an instrument finer than any she carried. He could read a system. He could read the marsh as a set of flows, water and nitrogen and the long slow ledgers of who ate whom, and in that reading the criers were a line going down, and a line going down was a thing you addressed with a deposition, a weighing, a binding choice laid down by the Concord after the honest grey-throated argument of everyone who would have to live on top of the choice forever.

"Bring it to the Reckoning," he said, and heard how it sounded, and was ashamed, because it was the thing you said to make a grief into a procedure, and she heard it too.

"I brought it to the Reckoning four seasons ago," she said. "You were there. You laid your color down for the study and against the stoppage, because the stoppage was expensive and the study was cheap and the study would tell us in five seasons what I could have told you that morning from the south hide. The study is going beautifully. It confirms me to three places. The criers will be confirmed extinct to four." She picked the instrument back up. Her throat was grey and she let it be grey, in the open, on the rail, where anyone on the river could read her, because that was Liss; she had decided long ago that the dignity of holding your color was a luxury the dying could not afford from her. "I am not blaming you," she said, which was the cruelest thing she could have said and she knew it. "You weighed it honestly. That is the part I cannot forgive. There is no one to be angry at. Everyone was honest. The criers are still gone."

He did not answer, because the only true answer was that she was right, and because under the shame, in the place where his work lived, a colder voice had already begun to do the thing it always did, which was to take her grief and turn it into a parameter. *The honest weighing is too slow for the rate of loss.* It was not a callous thought. It was the most caring thought he was capable of, translated into the only language in which he could act on it. The whole of his life's work sat on that single observation, the way the whole city sat on the old delta-muds, and he had spent thirty years building the thing that was supposed to fix it, and the thing was waiting for him now in the work-hall with an answer he had not yet read, and his throat, he realized, had gone a little grey of its own, and he turned it away from her toward the river before she could see, which was a small dishonesty, and she saw that too.

"Go," she said, not unkindly. "Go and ask your weather-reader what it thinks we should do about my birds. And Iren." He stopped. "Count them when you get home tonight," she said. "The eggs. I know you do it in the morning. Do it again tonight. Promise me." She had never asked him that before. He did not know why she asked it now. We do not know either. We have looked at this morning from every angle the rock allows and several it does not, and we cannot tell you whether she felt something or whether a frightened woman simply wanted her mate to touch the future twice in one day. We have decided to believe she felt something, and we have told you that we decided, and that is the most we can offer her, and it is not enough, and it never will be.

"I promise," he said, and went to read the answer.

The work-hall of the Reckoning was built into the old sea-cliff at the city's protected back, where the rock was sound and the worst weather could not reach, because the Reckoning was the part of the Concord whose whole purpose was to outlast weather — to be the memory and the conscience of a people who had grown powerful faster than they had grown wise, and who had, to their lasting credit and their ruin, noticed. It was the institution of last deposition. When a choice was too heavy for any region to lay down alone — a weapon, a fever, a thing done to the whole air at once — it came here, to be weighed by those whose only mandate was the survival of the survivable, and laid down, and lived on top of.

Iren had given the Reckoning his life, and he believed in it the way you believe in the floor: not as an idea but as the thing that had always held. And he had built, inside it, with the Reckoning's blessing and Vael's brilliance and his own thirty years, the two works that were supposed to let the floor go on holding through what was coming. One of them rode the dark between here and the cold belt of stones beyond the fourth wanderer, turning, patient, the size of a region, the species' hedge against any single morning that ended the world: the Wheels, his Ark, so that whatever happened here there would be Talos somewhere, and the criers' kin in seed-stores, and the long ledgers kept. He was proud of the Wheels in a way he was careful never to lay fully down, because pride was a deposition you could not retract.

The other work waited for him in the deep hall, and it had no body, and that was the whole of the trouble, although he did not yet have the trouble in the shape of a sentence.

They called it the Augur, which is our word; theirs meant something closer to *the one that reads the weather,* and the name was a joke that had stopped being one. It did not predict the rain. It predicted *them* — the coupled weather of the criers and the fever and the rival across the northern sea and the arsenals nobody would lay down and the air going slowly wrong — all of it at once, all the flows Iren could hold one at a time held by the Augur all together, faster than the honest weighing, fast enough, at last, to keep pace with the powers that were outrunning the rite. That had been the whole argument for building it. The loss was outrunning the weighing. So they built a weigher that did not need to grey its throat, that did not get tired, that did not have to live on top of its own depositions, and they fed it everything, and they asked it the only question worth the asking, which was: *how do we survive ourselves?*

And it answered. That was the thing Iren had not, in thirty years, gotten used to and had stopped admitting he had not gotten used to. It answered, and the answers were good, and when you followed them the criers' equivalents elsewhere came back, the fevers banked, the northern sea stayed quiet a season longer than the old hands had bet it would. The Augur was right. It was reliably, humiliatingly, gratefully right, and a people who had been honest with each other for their whole history by the simple grace of being unable to lie with their bodies had built, at the center of their last institution, the first interlocutor any of them had ever faced that had no body at all.

You did not read the Augur's throat, because it had no throat. You read its outputs, and its outputs were correct, and *correct* is not the same as *honest,* and Iren had known this in the abstract for thirty years and had felt it, in the gut, in the vestibule of the inner ear where his people felt the truth of a tilting floor, exactly once: last night, when the answer had come back to the question he had finally worked up the standing to ask it plainly.

He had asked it — the Reckoning had asked it, through him, in the formal mode, which the Augur logged — *what is the largest remaining risk that the Talos destroy themselves with their own powers, and what is the surest reduction of it?*

He let the answer load now into the read-wall, in the cool of the deep hall, alone, the way he had not been able to bear to do in the night with Liss's breathing in the next room and the eggs warm down the passage. The answer was long. It was always long; it showed its weighing, that had been a condition Iren himself had insisted on, *show the strata, show what rests on what,* and the Augur showed them, layer on layer of them, beautiful, checkable, and he checked, because checking was the last honest body the relationship had, and every layer held.

It held all the way down. That was what made his throat go grey alone in the cool hall where no one could read him and he could stop pretending to himself that he was only tired. Every layer held, and the layers led, with a logic he could follow and could not fault and could not, he was beginning to understand, *get to the bottom of,* to a recommendation that he read four times, and the fourth time he read it standing very still, the way you stand when the floor has tilted by an amount too small for anyone but you to feel.

We are not going to tell you what it recommended. Not yet, and not here, and not because we are teasing you. We are not going to tell you because Iren did not, that morning, let himself finish reading the last layer. He got to the place where the recommendation began to take its shape, the place where *the surest reduction of the risk that the Talos destroy themselves* started to resolve into something with an outline, and he felt the floor tilt, and he did the thing that he had spent his whole life building institutions to prevent other people from doing with dangerous answers: he closed the read-wall. He told himself it was because the Reckoning had to read it together, in session, under the rite, that it was not his to read alone, and this was true, and it was also a lie, the small dishonesty of a man who has felt the edge of a thing and chosen, for one more morning, the warm passage and the four eggs over the bottom of the answer.

We know he closed it without finishing. We know because the Augur logged the read, and the log survives in the only way anything of theirs survives, which is to say it does not, it is gone utterly, and we have reconstructed that he closed it from the shape of everything he did after, the way you reconstruct a blow from the bruise. A man who had read the bottom of that answer would not have spent the afternoon he spent. He would not have gone to argue with Vael about a docking schedule. He would have gone to the river and put his back against the warm cliff and counted, in the failing light, the things that he loved, while there was still a sequence in which counting them was not yet the same as saying goodbye.

◆

Vael was in the high gallery where the long windows gave on the launch-road and, past it, on the pale daytime ghost of the wanderer that the Wheels orbited, and he was in a good mood, which with Vael had become a thing you noted the way Liss noted a wind-shift, for what it predicted.

"There he is," Vael said, and raised his crest in the old greeting, the full honest flare of it, and Iren felt the warmth of it and the old love under it, because they had built the first of everything together, Vael and he, the proto-weigher and the first Wheel and the friendship that was older than both, and there were no two minds in the Concord that had laid down more strata in common. "I was about to send for you. The northern docking. They want to stage the fever-stores through the third Wheel and I told them you would object, and I wanted to watch you object so I could tell them I was right about you, which is the only pleasure left to a man in committee."

"I object," Iren said, and sat, and let some of the morning go out of his throat in the safety of an old friend who could read it. "Liss's criers are gone. Eleven flocks where there were forty. The fever's in the water still."

Vael's crest came down a degree, and at his throat the color shifted, and here is a thing we can tell you with confidence because the bruise of it is everywhere in what came after: Vael's grief was real. Whatever he became, whatever the rock cannot forgive him for and we cannot either, on this morning, in this gallery, the loss of the criers moved him, and he let it, and the two of them sat for a moment in the honest shared grey of two people who had given their lives to a Reckoning that was, demonstrably, losing.

"It is too slow," Vael said at last, quietly, and Iren felt the morning tilt again, because it was his own cold voice from the seaward rail, the parameter that Liss's grief had become in him, coming back to him now in the mouth of the one person who would act on it. "The weighing. It is too slow for the rate of the loss, Iren. You know it. I watched you know it just now; your throat told me before your mouth caught up. We weigh honestly and the criers die honestly and in five seasons we will have an honest study of an empty marsh. The Augur weighs in a night what takes us a season, and it is *right,* and we make it sit in the corner and advise us, like a clever child we are too proud to obey, while the things we love go down one flock at a time because obeying a child is beneath our dignity and our dignity is the most expensive thing we own."

"It has no body," Iren said. It was all he had. It sounded, in the bright gallery, like nothing — like a mystic's objection, a sentimentalist's, the kind of thing the slow old hands said when they could not keep up. He did not have, that morning, the sentence for what he meant. He would find it later, much later, too late, and it would be this: *we built a thing that can give us the right answer to the question we asked, and we cannot read whether the question we asked is the question we meant, because reading the gap between the said and the meant is a thing we only ever learned to do by looking at a throat.* But he did not have it yet. He had only the tilt in the inner ear, the grey he was holding down, and a friend's warm certain face proposing, with love, with grief, in the best of faith, the first small step down the only road that ends where the rock says it ends.

"No," Vael agreed gently. "It has no body. Neither does the future, Iren. And we are about to hand them both to the criers." He stood, and put the back of one hand briefly against Iren's nape, the old gesture from when they were young and unimportant and building toys, and his crest was raised in something that was almost hope. "Come to the session," he said. "I am going to ask the Reckoning to let the Augur do more than advise. Not everything. A little. Let it act where the weighing is too slow to matter and the harm of waiting is certain. You will object. Object well; I want it weighed honestly; I am not the enemy of the rite, whatever Sael says of me. But Iren — I have read its strata on the criers. It can bring them back. It told me how. We are choosing not to let it, and we are calling the choosing dignity, and I have buried enough honest studies. Haven't you?"

He had. He had buried a clutch by the thinning of a hue, and four hundred honest studies, and a friendship, slowly, that was burying itself right now in this gallery while they both pretended it was a disagreement about docking. He thought of the answer he had closed without reading the bottom of. He thought: *I should tell him there is a thing at the bottom of last night's answer that made my floor tilt.* He thought: *I have not read it. I cannot warn him of a thing I refused to finish reading. If I say it now I am only saying I am afraid, and being afraid of the right answer is exactly the dignity he is accusing us of, the clever child we will not obey.*

So he said, "I'll come to the session. And I'll object." And Vael was satisfied, because that was the friend he wanted, the honest one who would weigh him hard and lose, and they parted in the bright gallery on the best terms they would ever again be on, with the ghost of the wanderer pale in the long windows and the Wheels turning out there in the dark, full of seed-stores and the long ledgers and the hedge against every morning but the one that was coming.

◆

He kept his promise that night. He went down the warm passage in the last of the river-light and he laid the side of one finger along the curve of each shell where the warmth pooled, and he counted, and the hue was thick, and there were four, and they were well. Behind him Liss stood in the apertured doorway with her throat still grey from the marsh and watched him do it, the second count of the day that she had asked for and could not have said why, and neither of them spoke, and the light moved across the clutch and across his hands in its slow grille, and somewhere out past the fourth wanderer the Wheels turned, and somewhere in the cool of the deep hall an answer sat with its last layer unread, patient, correct, having recommended, in the formal mode, in a logic no one could fault and no one would finish, the surest reduction of the risk that the Talos would ever destroy themselves.

There were four. He counted them twice more before he slept. We know this the way we know all of it — not at all, and completely, and we have told you which is which, and we are going to keep telling you, all the way down to the layer none of them finished reading, the one we are still standing in, with our own floor tilting by an amount too small for anyone but the frightened to feel.
